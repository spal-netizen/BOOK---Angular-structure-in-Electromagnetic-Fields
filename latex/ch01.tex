\subsection{Scope and role of Chapter 1}
\label{sec:ch101}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

This monograph is addressed to advanced graduate students, doctoral researchers,
practicing scientists, and professional engineers in electromagnetics, optics,
antennas, wave physics, applied mathematics, and related disciplines.
The reader is assumed to be familiar with Maxwell's equations, vector calculus,
linear algebra, differential equations, and classical electromagnetic theory.
The development nevertheless proceeds from first principles in a manner that is
mathematically rigorous and physically unified: angular structure is not taken
as an application-specific adjunct to antenna or beam theory, but as a
consequence of Maxwellian field dynamics, symmetry, and observability.

The work is organized in three volumes.
Volume~I (Chapters~1--4) establishes the theoretical foundations developed in
the present chapter and carried through realizability and spectral truncation.
Volume~II (from Chapter~5 onward) applies these foundations to physically
realizable systems---radiation mechanisms, discrete and continuous radiators,
engineered surfaces, propagation in homogeneous and random media, and
field--matter interaction---without re-deriving the operator and spectral
framework laid down here.
Volume~III treats measurement, information, sensing, and the physical
interpretation of angular structure in communication and observation.
Forward references to later volumes name topics only; their proofs and
operators appear in the chapters cited in the Chapter Summary
(Section~\ref{sec:ch17}).

\paragraph{Role of Chapter~1 within Volume~I.}
Chapter~1 has a deliberately restricted mandate.
It introduces the \emph{physical and conceptual} language in which the remainder
of Volume~I, and ultimately the monograph, is written: what is meant by angular
structure, which conservation laws constrain it, how rotations and gauges affect
its definition, and which quantities are in principle measurable.
The chapter does \emph{not} construct the operator-theoretic or spectral apparatus
that Chapters~2--4 provide.
Accordingly, the reader should not expect in this chapter a development of
radiation operators, Green-function mappings, Hilbert-space formulations,
\(\mathrm{SO}(3)\) eigenfield theory, vector spherical harmonics, completeness and
normalization of angular bases, or the truncation and realizability limits
associated with finite sources and apertures.
Those structures are forward-linked here only to preserve a single, forward-moving
logical order; their formal definitions and proofs begin in
Sections~\ref{sec:ch21}--\ref{sec:ch411} of Volume~I.

\paragraph{Angular structure in the sense of this monograph.}
Throughout the text, \emph{angular structure} denotes the complete organization
of an electromagnetic field with respect to spatial direction, rotational
symmetry, angular-momentum content, and angular spectral composition, as
governed by Maxwell's equations and by the boundary conditions and measurement
contexts that select observable subspaces of the field.
Chapter~\ref{ch:01} makes this definition operative at the level of conservation
laws and symmetry; the spectral and operator realizations follow in later
chapters.

\paragraph{Notation and conventions.}
Unless otherwise stated, time-harmonic fields use the phasor convention
\(\exp(-\mathrm{j}\omega t)\), and all quantities are expressed in SI units.
Symbols for fields, sources, operators, and angular harmonics are introduced at
their first formal appearance; the consolidated tables appear in the front matter
and in Appendix notation summaries as the manuscript permits.
Details of the phasor map, units, and label conventions are fixed in the project
notation specification and are not repeated in subsequent chapters except where
amendment is explicitly noted.

The following section explains why angular structure arises from Maxwellian
symmetry and boundary conditions rather than from a postulated mode catalogue.
