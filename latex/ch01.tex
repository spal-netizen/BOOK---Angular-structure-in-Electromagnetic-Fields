\section{Introduction and Scope}
\label{sec:ch01.0}

Chapter~\ref{ch:01} opens the monograph by preparing a rigorous reading frame
for all later derivations. The immediate task is not to enumerate modal families
or applications, but to separate three layers that are frequently mixed in
electromagnetic writing: governing field law, representation choice, and
operationally admissible observable content. Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.0.1}
therefore states the chapter mandate and scope boundary,
Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.0.2} develops angular structure as a consequence of
Maxwell dynamics under geometry and boundary constraints, and
Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.0.3} defines the admissibility domain in which later
claims remain mathematically and physically meaningful.


\subsection{Scope and role of Chapter 1}
\label{sec:ch01.0.1}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Electromagnetic fields often exhibit strong azimuthal phase gradients, rotating
interference textures, and polarization patterns that appear to carry angular
content. Those signatures become physically meaningful only after one separates
three questions: whether the fields satisfy Maxwell equations on the stated
domain, whether the associated momentum flux supports angular transport, and
whether a measurement protocol can access the claimed quantity
\cite{jackson1999,collin2001,harrington2001}. The opening task is therefore to
state this separation as a mathematical filter, so that descriptive structure is
not confused with conserved transfer.

Let \(\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^{3}\) denote the spatial analysis region,
\(\Gamma=\partial\Omega\) its boundary, \(\chi\) the constitutive descriptor set,
\(\Jimp\) the impressed electric-current density, and \(\omega>0\) the harmonic
angular frequency under the \(\exp(-\jj\omega t)\) convention. Define the scope
map
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.scope-map}
\mathfrak{S}_{\mathrm{ch1}}:
(\Omega,\Gamma,\chi,\Jimp,\omega)
\longmapsto
\big(\mathcal{L}_M,\mathcal{T}_C,\mathcal{O}_A\big),
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.scope-map} defines the chapter-level admissibility map from
problem data to law, transport, and observability constraints.
where \(\mathcal{L}_M\) denotes Maxwell-law consistency,
\(\mathcal{T}_C\) denotes transport/conservation consistency, and
\(\mathcal{O}_A\) denotes operational observability consistency. The input tuple
specifies geometry, boundary, constitutive closure, source forcing, and temporal
scale; the output tuple specifies the three independent constraints any angular
statement must satisfy before it can be interpreted as physical transfer.

The acceptance rule is encoded by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.scope-admissibility-filter}
\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{ch1}}[\Xi]=1
\iff
\Xi\in\mathcal{L}_M
\ \land\
\Xi\in\mathcal{T}_C
\ \land\
\Xi\in\mathcal{O}_A,
\end{equation}
with \(\Xi\) any candidate angular statement and \(\land\) logical conjunction.
Term-level interpretation is immediate: each membership test verifies one
independent physical requirement. Taken together, Eq.
\eqref{eq:ch1.scope-admissibility-filter} classifies claims by three simultaneous
constraints. Azimuthal phase structure alone does not imply
angular-momentum transport unless constitutive-domain Maxwell closure, flux
balance, and measurement channel are simultaneously satisfied.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1.scope-control-gate} provides a geometric reading of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.scope-admissibility-filter}: the left block fixes the physical
problem data, the middle chain enforces law and transport constraints, and the

decorative because each block corresponds to an explicit operand in
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:ch1.scope-map}--\eqref{eq:ch1.scope-admissibility-filter}.

Two practical consequences follow. First, local phase winding, structured
polarization, or modal labels remain descriptive until transport and observability
tests pass. Second, conservation statements are interpreted only for fields that
satisfy the declared domain and constitutive closure; otherwise, apparent torque
or angular-flow signatures may arise from incompatible boundary/source conditions
rather than from true radiative transfer \cite{balanis2016,collin2001}.

The resulting criterion is used throughout Chapter~\ref{ch:01}: if any of the
three memberships in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.scope-admissibility-filter} fails, the
statement is retained as representation metadata and is not promoted to physical
angular-momentum transport.

\paragraph{Illustrative scope test.}
Suppose a near-field pattern displays azimuthal phase \(\exp(\jj m\phi)\) on an
aperture. Candidate statement \(\Xi\): ``the field carries orbital angular
momentum \(m\hbar\) per photon.'' Maxwell-law membership \(\Xi\in\mathcal{L}_M\)
requires a source-supported solution on \(\Omega\) with declared \(\chi\) and
\(\Gamma\). Transport membership \(\Xi\in\mathcal{T}_C\) requires nonzero
\(\dot J_z\) or torque channel consistent with
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.angular-balance-integral}. Observability membership
\(\Xi\in\mathcal{O}_A\) requires a calibrated map from \(\E,\H\) to the reported
number. A reactive-dominated shell can satisfy \(\mathcal{L}_M\) while failing
\(\mathcal{T}_C\); a gauge-dependent local density can satisfy visual inspection
while failing \(\mathcal{O}_A\). Only when all three pass does
\(\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{ch1}}[\Xi]=1\) \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001,balanis2016}.


\subsection{Angular structure from Maxwell symmetry and boundary conditions}
\label{sec:ch01.0.2}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Rotational signatures in electromagnetic fields become physically interpretable
only when they are traced to symmetry of the Maxwell boundary-value map. A field
can exhibit azimuthal phase winding yet fail to transport angular momentum if the
geometry or material closure redistributes channel content before flux leaves the
control surface \cite{jackson1999,collin2001,harrington2001}.

Let \(\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^{3}\) denote the analysis region,
\(\Gamma=\partial\Omega\) its boundary, \(\chi\) the constitutive descriptor set,
and \(\Jimp\) the impressed electric-current density. Let
\(\mathcal{M}_{\Omega,\Gamma,\chi}\) map admissible sources to the phasor field
pair \((\E,\H)\). For each active spatial rotation \(R_\alpha\in\mathrm{SO}(3)\),
parameterized by \(\alpha\), let \(\mathcal{R}_\alpha\) denote the induced action
on source and field spaces. Rotational compatibility is imposed by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.rotation-commutator-criterion}
\left[\mathcal{R}_\alpha,\mathcal{M}_{\Omega,\Gamma,\chi}\right]\Jimp
=\mathbf{0}
\quad\forall\alpha\in\mathcal{I}_\alpha,
\end{equation}
where \([\cdot,\cdot]\) is the commutator and \(\mathcal{I}_\alpha\) is the
rotation set admitted by the geometry. Operand interpretation:
\(\mathcal{R}_\alpha\) carries geometric action, while
\(\mathcal{M}_{\Omega,\Gamma,\chi}\) carries electrodynamic propagation under
boundary/material closure. Rotating the source before propagation and rotating
the propagated field afterward must produce the same state: angular organization is
dynamically inherited from symmetry of the full problem, not assigned by coordinate
labeling alone.

To quantify departures from perfect compatibility, define
\(\kappa_{\mathrm{rot}}(\alpha)\) by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.rotation-mismatch-index}
\kappa_{\mathrm{rot}}(\alpha)
=
\frac{
\left\|
\left[\mathcal{R}_\alpha,\mathcal{M}_{\Omega,\Gamma,\chi}\right]\Jimp
\right\|_{\mathcal{H}_F}
}{
\left\|
\mathcal{M}_{\Omega,\Gamma,\chi}\Jimp
\right\|_{\mathcal{H}_F}
+\epsilon_{\mathrm{rot}}
},
\end{equation}
where \(\|\cdot\|_{\mathcal{H}_F}\) is the norm on field space
\(\mathcal{H}_F\), and \(\epsilon_{\mathrm{rot}}>0\) is a regularization constant
that prevents denominator singularity near vanishing field magnitude. The
numerator measures non-commuting residue; the denominator scales that residue by
field amplitude. Thus \(\kappa_{\mathrm{rot}}\) is a dimensionless mismatch index
that reports boundary/material-induced redistribution among angular channels.

Symmetry compatibility is necessary but not sufficient for transport claims. Let
\(\Phi_J(S_c)\) denote the net angular-momentum flux through control surface
\(S_c\). A transport-capable regime is identified by
\[
\kappa_{\mathrm{rot}}(\alpha)\ll 1
\quad\text{and}\quad
|\Phi_J(S_c)|>0
\ \text{under declared measurement channel}.
\]
The first inequality enforces rotational coherence of propagation; the second
certifies that the organized field state carries transferable angular content.

The commuting branch preserves rotational channel identity; the non-commuting branch
redistributes channels through boundary or material structure. Transport meaning
requires measurable flux \(\Phi_J(S_c)\), not phase morphology alone
\cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}.

Validity conditions are explicit. The commutator model assumes linear local
constitutive closure and boundary regularity sufficient for well-posed Maxwell
mapping. Strong temporal modulation, nonlocal constitutive kernels, or unresolved
multiscale roughness can invalidate the model class in
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:ch1.rotation-commutator-criterion} and
\eqref{eq:ch1.rotation-mismatch-index}, in which case generalized operators and
alternate diagnostics are required \cite{collin2001,balanis2016}.


\subsection{Assumptions, finite-energy admissibility, and domain of validity}
\label{sec:ch01.0.3}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Finite-energy admissibility is the minimal mathematical condition that keeps
transport quantities, projections, and measurement models in the same functional
class. Without this condition, manipulations of momentum or angular observables
may remain algebraically legal while representing non-realizable fields
\cite{jackson1999,collin2001,harrington2001}.

Let \(\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^{3}\) denote the analysis region,
\(\Gamma=\partial\Omega\) its boundary, and \((\E,\H)\) the phasor electric and
magnetic fields under the \(\exp(-\jj\omega t)\) convention. The baseline
finite-energy condition is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.energy-class}
\int_{\Omega}\!\left(\E^*\!\cdot\E+\H^*\!\cdot\H\right)\,dV<\infty,
\end{equation}
where \((\cdot)^*\) denotes complex conjugation and \(dV\) is the volume element.
At term level, \(\E^*\!\cdot\E\) and \(\H^*\!\cdot\H\) are nonnegative electric
and magnetic energy-density surrogates in phasor form. Full-equation meaning:
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.energy-class} places admissible states in
\(L^2(\Omega)^3\times L^2(\Omega)^3\). Physical meaning: field states with
divergent norm content are excluded because they do not support stable finite
control-volume flux interpretation.

Energy admissibility alone is insufficient; source and boundary compatibility must
be imposed simultaneously. Define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.admissible-space}
\mathcal{X}_{\mathrm{ch1}}(\Omega)
=
\left\{
(\E,\H)\in L^2(\Omega)^3\times L^2(\Omega)^3:
\mathcal{B}_{\Gamma,\chi}(\E,\H)=0,\ 
\divg\Jimp-\jj\omega\rho=0
\right\},
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{B}_{\Gamma,\chi}\) is the boundary/constitutive closure operator,
\(\Jimp\) is impressed electric-current density, and \(\rho\) is free-charge
density. Operand interpretation is explicit: the \(L^2\) clause controls finite
energy; \(\mathcal{B}_{\Gamma,\chi}=0\) enforces compatibility with declared
boundary/material class; \(\divg\Jimp-\jj\omega\rho=0\) enforces harmonic charge
continuity for source data. Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.admissible-space} therefore
defines the complete state class in which subsequent conservation claims are read;
an angular claim is promoted to transport status only when field norm, boundary
closure, and source continuity are all simultaneously satisfied.

Eqs.~\eqref{eq:ch1.energy-class}--\eqref{eq:ch1.admissible-space} require finite
energy, compatible boundary traces, and harmonic source continuity before any
transport interpretation is assigned \cite{harrington2001,jackson1999}.

The admissible class above assumes linear local
constitutive response, classical continuum electrodynamics, and harmonic forcing on
timescales separated from fast modulation envelopes. Strongly nonlinear media,
active gain with saturation dynamics, nonlocal kernels, and quantum corrections are
outside the model class of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.admissible-space}; in those regimes,
additional state variables or generalized operators are required before angular
transport metrics can be interpreted consistently \cite{harrington2001,jackson1999}.

\paragraph{Section 1.0 summary and bridge.}
The field class considered in this chapter is now fully specified: candidate
statements must satisfy simultaneous law consistency, transport consistency, and
operational observability; rotational interpretation is tied to symmetry of the
boundary-value map; and admissible states are restricted by finite-energy,
boundary/constitutive closure, and harmonic source continuity. With these
conditions fixed, the next section develops Maxwell dynamics and conservation laws
as quantitative constraints on measurable energy, momentum, and angular transport.


\section{Maxwell Theory and Conservation Laws}
\label{sec:ch01.1}

Section~\ref{sec:ch01.1} establishes the governing dynamical core that all later
angular claims must inherit: Maxwell equations in the adopted SI phasor
convention, admissible source and boundary data, well-posed evolution, and the
continuity laws that connect local field structure to measurable transfer of
energy, linear momentum, and angular momentum. The subsection sequence is
organized so that conservation statements are read as consequences of the same
field equations, not as independent postulates.


\subsection{Maxwell equations: differential, integral, vacuum constitutive relations}
\label{sec:ch01.1.1}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Harmonic fields are represented with time factor \(\exp(-\jj\omega t)\), with
\(\omega>0\), in a piecewise smooth region \(\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^{3}\) bounded
by \(\Gamma=\partial\Omega\). The electric and magnetic phasors are
\(\E(\mathbf{r})\) and \(\H(\mathbf{r})\), the impressed electric-current density
is \(\Jimp(\mathbf{r})\), and free-charge density is \(\rho(\mathbf{r})\). Vacuum
parameters are \(\varepsilon_0\) and \(\mu_0\), all in SI units
\cite{collin2001,jackson1999,harrington2001}. With this notation fixed, Maxwell
equations are the governing balance laws for rotational and flux constraints of the
electromagnetic field state \cite{collin2001,jackson1999}.

The differential form is the local statement of those laws:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.maxwell-local}
\begin{aligned}
\curl \E&=-\jj\omega\mu_0\H, &
\curl \H&=\Jimp+\jj\omega\varepsilon_0\E,\\
\divg\!\left(\varepsilon_0\E\right)&=\rho, &
\divg\!\left(\mu_0\H\right)&=0.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
In the first curl row, \(\omega\mu_0\) scales magnetic contribution to induced
electric circulation. In the second, source forcing \(\Jimp\) and displacement term
\(\jj\omega\varepsilon_0\E\) jointly determine magnetic circulation. The divergence
rows impose flux bookkeeping: electric flux divergence equals free-charge density,
and magnetic flux has zero divergence. Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-local} thus
combines evolution-compatible curl coupling with algebraic divergence constraints
into one closed first-order system \cite{collin2001,jackson1999,harrington2001}.

Writing each Cartesian component makes the local nature explicit:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.maxwell-component-example}
\begin{aligned}
\partial_y E_z-\partial_z E_y&=-\jj\omega\mu_0 H_x,\\
\partial_z H_x-\partial_x H_z&=J_{\mathrm{imp},y}+\jj\omega\varepsilon_0 E_y,\\
\partial_x(\varepsilon_0 E_x)+\partial_y(\varepsilon_0 E_y)+\partial_z(\varepsilon_0 E_z)&=\rho.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The component form shows that derivatives of neighboring components are inseparable
from local source content; consequently, angular features cannot be assigned to one
field component in isolation.

An operator representation is convenient for admissibility analysis:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.maxwell-operator-block}
\mathcal{M}_\omega
\begin{bmatrix}\E\\ \H\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\curl \E+\jj\omega\mu_0\H\\
\curl \H-\Jimp-\jj\omega\varepsilon_0\E\\
\divg(\varepsilon_0\E)-\rho\\
\divg(\mu_0\H)
\end{bmatrix}
=\mathbf{0},
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{M}_\omega\) is the harmonic Maxwell operator. Each row is a
residual channel; null residual in all channels is equivalent to satisfaction of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-local}. This form is especially effective when checking
source consistency and trace consistency in numerical pipelines.

Charge conservation follows algebraically from the local curl-divergence structure.
Taking divergence of the Ampere-Maxwell row gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.charge-continuity-harmonic}
\divg\Jimp-\jj\omega\rho=0,
\end{equation}
because \(\divg(\curl\H)=0\). Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.charge-continuity-harmonic}
is not an external add-on; it is embedded in Maxwell structure itself
\cite{collin2001,jackson1999}. The same identity is recovered by differentiating
\(\divg(\varepsilon_0\E)=\rho\) in harmonic time and substituting
\(\curl\E=-\jj\omega\mu_0\H\), confirming closure consistency.

Integrating Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.charge-continuity-harmonic} over a control volume
\(V\) with boundary \(\partial V\) and outward normal \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\) yields
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.continuity-integral}
\int_{\partial V}\Jimp\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS
-\jj\omega\int_V\rho\,dV=0.
\end{equation}
The first term is net impressed current leaving \(V\), and the second is harmonic
charge variation inside \(V\). Physical interpretation is immediate: accumulation
and outflow must remain balanced at every admissible control volume.

The integral form of Maxwell equations follows from Stokes and divergence theorems.
For oriented surface \(S\subset\Omega\) with boundary \(\partial S\), oriented
volume \(V\subset\Omega\), line element \(d\boldsymbol{\ell}\), area element \(dS\),
and volume element \(dV\),
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.maxwell-integral}
\begin{aligned}
\oint_{\partial S}\E\cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell}
&=-\jj\omega\mu_0\int_S\H\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS,\\
\oint_{\partial S}\H\cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell}
&=\int_S\Jimp\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS
+\jj\omega\varepsilon_0\int_S\E\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS,\\
\int_{\partial V}\varepsilon_0\E\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS
&=\int_V\rho\,dV,\qquad
\int_{\partial V}\mu_0\H\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS=0.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
These relations are exact finite-control statements of the same laws written
locally in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-local}
\cite{collin2001,jackson1999,harrington2001}. Line integrals represent circulation
measurements; surface integrals represent flux transfer; volume integrals represent
stored free charge over a finite domain.

\begin{proposition}[Local-integral equivalence on admissible sets]
\label{prop:ch1.local-integral-equivalence}
Let \(\E,\H\in H(\curl;\Omega)\), \(\varepsilon_0\E,\mu_0\H\in H(\divg;\Omega)\),
\(\Jimp\in L^2(\Omega)^3\), and \(\rho\in L^2(\Omega)\). If \(S\) and \(V\) are
piecewise smooth oriented controls, then
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-local} and Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-integral} are
equivalent in weak form.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Applying Stokes and divergence theorems to the local rows gives the integral rows.
Conversely, localization of integral balances to shrinking controls recovers local
rows in the distributional sense. Regularity assumptions guarantee interchange of
limit and integration operations.

Vacuum constitutive relations supply the closure that transforms field-intensity
variables into flux-density variables:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.vacuum-constitutive}
\mathbf{D}=\varepsilon_0\E,\qquad
\mathbf{B}=\mu_0\H,\qquad
c=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\varepsilon_0\mu_0}}.
\end{equation}
Here \(\mathbf{D}\) is electric flux density, \(\mathbf{B}\) magnetic flux density,
and \(c\) vacuum wave speed \cite{collin2001,jackson1999,harrington2001}. Without
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.vacuum-constitutive}, Maxwell balance laws remain under-closed in
terms of unknowns \((\E,\H,\mathbf{D},\mathbf{B})\). With closure, the model is
fully evaluable for vacuum media.

Derived vacuum scales follow directly:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.vacuum-impedance-wavenumber}
\eta_0=\sqrt{\frac{\mu_0}{\varepsilon_0}},\qquad
k_0=\omega\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}=\frac{\omega}{c},\qquad
\lambda_0=\frac{2\pi}{k_0}.
\end{equation}
\(\eta_0\) is intrinsic impedance, \(k_0\) is wavenumber, and \(\lambda_0\) is
wavelength. These scales set amplitude and phase calibration for every source-free
vacuum propagation sector \cite{collin2001,jackson1999,balanis2016}.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1.vacuum-closure-map} makes an important point for students:
constitutive closure and wave propagation are not separate modules. The constants
that map \(\E,\H\) to \(\mathbf{D},\mathbf{B}\) also set wave speed and impedance,
so material assumptions and propagation predictions are intrinsically linked.

Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.vacuum-constitutive} into curl equations yields
second-order source-driven wave equations:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.vacuum-wave-source-driven}
\nabla^2\E+k_0^2\E=\frac{1}{\varepsilon_0}\nabla\rho-\jj\omega\mu_0\Jimp,\qquad
\nabla^2\H+k_0^2\H=-\curl\Jimp.
\end{equation}
The \(\nabla\rho\) and \(\Jimp\) terms are electric-source forcing channels; the
\(-\curl\Jimp\) term is magnetic-source forcing for \(\H\). Equation
\eqref{eq:ch1.vacuum-wave-source-driven} clarifies that spatial curvature and
source gradients jointly shape field structure \cite{collin2001,jackson1999}.

In source-free vacuum, Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.vacuum-wave-source-driven} reduces to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.vacuum-helmholtz-pair}
\nabla^2\E+k_0^2\E=\mathbf{0},\qquad
\nabla^2\H+k_0^2\H=\mathbf{0},
\end{equation}
which is the vector Helmholtz pair. The reduction is valid only when both
\(\Jimp=\mathbf{0}\) and \(\rho=0\), and when the constitutive closure remains
vacuum linear isotropic.

The same source-free physics may be written in curl-curl form:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.vacuum-curl-second-order}
\curl\!\left(\mu_0^{-1}\curl \E\right)-\omega^2\varepsilon_0\E=\mathbf{0},\qquad
\curl\!\left(\varepsilon_0^{-1}\curl \H\right)-\omega^2\mu_0\H=\mathbf{0}.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.vacuum-curl-second-order} and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.vacuum-helmholtz-pair} are equivalent in vacuum. Curl-curl form
is often preferable in weak formulations because it interfaces naturally with
tangential trace spaces and variational bilinear forms
\cite{harrington2001,collin2001}.

Local plane-wave sectors in homogeneous source-free vacuum obey
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.local-plane-wave-relations}
\H=\frac{1}{\eta_0}\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}\times\E,\qquad
\hat{\mathbf{k}}\cdot\E=0,\qquad
\hat{\mathbf{k}}\cdot\H=0,
\end{equation}
where \(\hat{\mathbf{k}}\) is unit propagation direction. The first relation fixes
vector triad orientation and impedance scaling, while the last two enforce
transversality relative to \(\hat{\mathbf{k}}\). These relations are local
templates underlying directional transport and angular-structure decomposition in
subsequent subsections \cite{collin2001,jackson1999,balanis2016}.

\paragraph{Worked problem: uniform plane wave in vacuum.}
Take \(\E=E_0 e^{\jj k_0 z}\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) with \(k_0=\omega\sqrt{\varepsilon_0\mu_0}\).
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-plane-wave-relations} gives
\(\H=(E_0/\eta_0)e^{\jj k_0 z}\hat{\mathbf{y}}\) and
\(\mathbf{S}=\tfrac12\E\times\H^*=(|E_0|^2/2\eta_0)\hat{\mathbf{z}}\).
Substituting into Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.poynting-complex} with \(\Jimp=\mathbf{0}\) yields
\(\divg\mathbf{S}=\mathbf{0}\): power flows without local source or storage imbalance.
The Maxwell stress of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress} is constant in the propagation
region, so traction on a plane normal to \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) is uniform. This
configuration is the baseline against which directional and angular descriptors in
Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2} are tested: isotropic power, zero net angular export, yet
well-defined transverse polarization \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}.

A dimensional check strengthens model reliability. In
\(\curl\E=-\jj\omega\mu_0\H\), the unit of \(\curl\E\) is
\(\mathrm{V\,m^{-2}}\), and the right-hand side has the same unit from
\([\omega]=\mathrm{s^{-1}}\), \([\mu_0]=\mathrm{H\,m^{-1}}\), and
\([\H]=\mathrm{A\,m^{-1}}\). Carrying this check across all rows of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-local} prevents hidden normalization errors when sources
or constitutive data are imported from mixed simulation and measurement pipelines.

The derivation boundary is precise. Equivalence of differential and integral forms
requires the regularity needed by Stokes and divergence theorems. Vacuum closure
in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.vacuum-constitutive} is invalid for dispersive, anisotropic,
nonlinear, nonlocal, or rapidly time-modulated media, where constitutive response
must be operator-valued rather than scalar-constant
\cite{collin2001,jackson1999,balanis2016}. Within the admitted vacuum class,
Maxwell equations, their differential-integral equivalence, and constitutive
closure together establish a complete and internally consistent field model for
all later angular-structure developments.


\subsection{Sources, boundary conditions, and physical admissibility}
\label{sec:ch01.1.2}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

The three heading components in this subsection are treated as one coupled chain.
Sources specify how excitation enters the field equations through volume and
surface forcing terms. Boundary conditions specify which tangential and normal
traces are admitted at material interfaces and at the outer boundary. Physical
admissibility specifies whether source data and boundary traces belong to mutually
compatible classes so that Maxwell balances, continuity, and energy interpretation
remain valid in the same model \cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001}.

Let \(\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^3\) be decomposed into piecewise smooth material
subdomains \(\{\Omega_a\}_{a=1}^{N_\Omega}\), and let
\(\Sigma_{ab}=\partial\Omega_a\cap\partial\Omega_b\) be an interface with unit
normal \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ab}\) directed from \(\Omega_a\) to \(\Omega_b\). Let
\(\mathbf{D}_a\) and \(\mathbf{B}_a\) denote electric and magnetic flux-density
phasors in \(\Omega_a\), \(\mathbf{J}_{s,ab}\) the electric surface-current
density on \(\Sigma_{ab}\), and \(\rho_{s,ab}\) the free surface-charge density
on \(\Sigma_{ab}\). A compact data tuple is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.data-specification-tuple}
\mathfrak{D}=
\Big(\Jimp,\rho,\{\mathbf{J}_{s,ab}\},\{\rho_{s,ab}\},\mathcal{B}_\Gamma,\mathbf{g}_\Gamma\Big),
\end{equation}
which lists all source and trace channels required for one harmonic solve.
On \(\Gamma=\partial\Omega\), data closure is represented by a boundary
operator \(\mathcal{B}_\Gamma\) acting on the boundary traces of \((\E,\H)\):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.boundary-data-operator}
\mathcal{B}_\Gamma[\E,\H]=\mathbf{g}_\Gamma,
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{g}_\Gamma\) is prescribed boundary data.
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.boundary-data-operator} closes the boundary-value problem
mathematically and encodes termination physics physically
\cite{harrington2001,collin2001,jackson1999}.

The same closure can be written in operator domain-range form,
\[
\mathcal{B}_\Gamma:\mathcal{T}_\Gamma\to\mathcal{Y}_\Gamma,
\]
where \(\mathcal{T}_\Gamma\) denotes the trace space generated by admissible
interior fields and \(\mathcal{Y}_\Gamma\) denotes the data space for boundary
specifications. Boundary conditions are therefore operator maps between spaces, not
standalone symbolic constraints.

Interface transmission follows from the integral Maxwell balances
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-integral} by shrinking control loops and pillboxes
across \(\Sigma_{ab}\). For piecewise smooth interfaces and no impressed magnetic
sheet current, one obtains
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.interface-conditions}
\begin{aligned}
\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ab}\times(\E_b-\E_a)&=\mathbf{0},\\
\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ab}\times(\H_b-\H_a)&=\mathbf{J}_{s,ab},\\
\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ab}\cdot(\mathbf{D}_b-\mathbf{D}_a)&=\rho_{s,ab},\\
\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ab}\cdot(\mathbf{B}_b-\mathbf{B}_a)&=0.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The analytical structure is explicit. The cross product with \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ab}\)
extracts tangential jumps; the dot product extracts normal-flux jumps. The second
row ties tangential-\(\H\) discontinuity to \(\mathbf{J}_{s,ab}\), and the third
row ties normal-\(\mathbf{D}\) discontinuity to \(\rho_{s,ab}\). Read as a block,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.interface-conditions} is the transmission law that keeps the
field pair consistent with Maxwell constraints while allowing physically necessary
jump mechanisms.

Intermediate limiting relations leading to Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.interface-conditions}
can be read from control-set shrinkage in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-integral}:
for a vanishing loop straddling \(\Sigma_{ab}\),
\[
\lim_{\delta\to 0}\oint_{\partial S_\delta}\E\cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell}=0
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ab}\times(\E_b-\E_a)=\mathbf{0},
\]
and for a vanishing pillbox,
\[
\lim_{\delta\to 0}\int_{\partial V_\delta}\mathbf{D}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS
=\rho_{s,ab}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ab}\cdot(\mathbf{D}_b-\mathbf{D}_a)=\rho_{s,ab}.
\]
These limiting expressions expose the derivational path from integral balances to
compact jump laws, and make explicit which terms survive when control sets collapse
toward the interface.

From a physical viewpoint, interface law determines how angularly structured field
content crosses material partitions. Continuity classes and jump channels identify
which tangential and normal components transmit transport information, and which
components are reflected, screened, or converted into surface-supported response.

Outer closure is specified by partitioning
\(\Gamma=\Gamma_{\mathrm{PEC}}\cup\Gamma_{\mathrm{PMC}}\cup\Gamma_Z\), where
\(\Gamma_{\mathrm{PEC}}\) is perfect-electric-conductor boundary,
\(\Gamma_{\mathrm{PMC}}\) is perfect-magnetic-conductor boundary, and \(\Gamma_Z\)
is an impedance boundary with surface impedance \(Z_s\):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.outer-boundary-classes}
\begin{aligned}
\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times\E&=\mathbf{0}\quad &&\text{on }\Gamma_{\mathrm{PEC}},\\
\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times\H&=\mathbf{0}\quad &&\text{on }\Gamma_{\mathrm{PMC}},\\
\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times\E&=Z_s\,(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times\H)\times\hat{\mathbf{n}}
\quad &&\text{on }\Gamma_Z,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
with \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\) the outward unit normal on \(\Gamma\). Here
\(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times\E\) and \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times\H\) are tangential
electric and magnetic traces, while \((\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times\H)\times\hat{\mathbf{n}}\)
reorients tangential magnetic content into the impedance-coupled direction.
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.outer-boundary-classes} therefore functions as a boundary
trace selector mathematically, and as a model of ideal reflection or finite
surface dissipation physically.

The three boundary classes can be read as limiting responses for tangential field
content. The PEC relation suppresses tangential electric trace, the PMC relation
suppresses tangential magnetic trace, and the impedance relation preserves both
while coupling them through \(Z_s\). This hierarchy determines which energy and
momentum channels can pass through the boundary and which are reflected
\cite{harrington2001,collin2001,balanis2016}.

For open-region truncation, an outgoing-trace condition is often required:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.radiation-trace-condition}
\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times\H
-\eta_0^{-1}(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times\E)\times\hat{\mathbf{n}}
=\mathbf{o}(R^{-1}),\qquad R\to\infty,
\end{equation}
where \(\eta_0=\sqrt{\mu_0/\varepsilon_0}\) and \(R\) is truncation radius.
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.radiation-trace-condition} enforces asymptotically outgoing
power flow and prevents unphysical incoming-wave closure in exterior problems
\cite{harrington2001,collin2001}.

Source admissibility is now imposed by regularity, localization, and continuity
compatibility. Let \(\Omega_s\subseteq\Omega\) be source support. The admissible
source class is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.source-admissibility}
\Jimp\in L^2(\Omega_s)^3,\qquad
\operatorname{supp}(\Jimp)\subseteq\overline{\Omega_s},\qquad
\divg\Jimp-\jj\omega\rho=0
\ \text{in }\mathcal{D}'(\Omega).
\end{equation}
The first term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.source-admissibility} enforces finite source
energy in the local \(L^2\)-sense; the support condition enforces spatial
localization of excitation; the distributional continuity relation enforces
charge-current consistency under the adopted \(\exp(-\jj\omega t)\) phasor
convention. Taken together, these conditions exclude non-conservative source
prescriptions that would violate Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-local}.

Physical admissibility is not exhausted by source regularity alone. Source,
interface, and boundary channels must be jointly consistent:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.admissible-data-class}
\mathfrak{D}\in\mathbb{A}_{\omega}
\iff
\begin{cases}
\text{Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.source-admissibility} holds,}\\
\text{Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.interface-conditions} holds on all interfaces,}\\
\text{Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.boundary-data-operator} holds with an admissible trace class,}\\
\text{Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.global-charge-compatibility} holds globally.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The set \(\mathbb{A}_{\omega}\) depends on frequency because continuity phase
balance and trace response depend on \(\omega\). If any channel fails, Maxwell
fields may still be computed numerically but no longer inherit one coherent
conservation interpretation.

\begin{proposition}[Global compatibility of source and boundary data]
\label{prop:ch1.source-boundary-compatibility}
Assume Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.source-admissibility} and the boundary operator
constraint Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.boundary-data-operator}. Then admissible data must
satisfy the global charge-balance identity
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.global-charge-compatibility}
\int_{\Omega}\!\left(\divg\Jimp-\jj\omega\rho\right)\,dV
=\int_{\Gamma}\!\Jimp\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS-\jj\omega\int_{\Omega}\rho\,dV=0.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

Integrating the distributional continuity relation in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.source-admissibility} over \(\Omega\), applying divergence
theorem to \(\divg\Jimp\), and using admissible boundary traces from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.boundary-data-operator} gives the stated identity. The integral
\(\int_{\Gamma}\Jimp\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS\) is net outward impressed-current
flux, while \(\jj\omega\int_{\Omega}\rho\,dV\) is harmonic charge-storage rate.
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.global-charge-compatibility} is therefore a global
charge-balance law on admissible data classes.

Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.global-charge-compatibility} links boundary current flux to
interior charge storage at whole-domain scale. Local constitutive detail may vary
across subdomains, yet this balance remains mandatory for any admissible dataset.

A practical residual for source-continuity verification is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.admissibility-residual}
\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{adm}}=
\frac{\|\divg\Jimp-\jj\omega\rho\|_{L^2(\Omega)}}
{\|\divg\Jimp\|_{L^2(\Omega)}+\omega\|\rho\|_{L^2(\Omega)}+\epsilon_0},
\end{equation}
where \(\epsilon_0>0\) is a fixed nondimensional regularizer. Small
\(\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{adm}}\) indicates numerically consistent source channels
before full field solution.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1.source-boundary-admissibility} highlights the coupled nature
of admissibility: source support, interface transmission, and exterior trace class
form a single data manifold. Later transport and observable claims inherit this
manifold directly, because angular descriptors computed from \((\E,\H)\) are
credible only when the entire data chain is compatible.

Boundary class selects trace channels: PEC removes tangential \(\E\), PMC removes
tangential \(\H\), impedance couples both through \(Z_s\), and the radiation trace
enforces outgoing-wave dominance \cite{collin2001,harrington2001}.

The validity boundary is equally clear. The derivation assumes piecewise smooth
interfaces, local constitutive response within each subregion, and standard trace
theory for boundary operators. Strongly nonlocal media, rough multiscale
interfaces, active time-modulated surfaces, and stochastic boundary operators lie
outside this subsection and require expanded functional frameworks
\cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001,balanis2016}.

\paragraph{Worked problem: impressed dipole source with charge continuity.}
Let \(\Jimp(\mathbf{r})=J_0\delta(\mathbf{r})\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) in harmonic form
with no free charge, \(\rho=0\). Then \(\divg\Jimp=0\) and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.global-charge-compatibility} holds exactly. If one instead models
\(\rho(\mathbf{r})=\rho_0\delta(\mathbf{r})\) without adjusting \(\Jimp\), then
\(\divg\Jimp-\jj\omega\rho=-\jj\omega\rho_0\delta(\mathbf{r})\neq 0\) and
\(\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{adm}}\) in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.admissibility-residual} becomes
order unity. A field solver may still return a numerical \(\E\)-field, but power and
torque integrals computed from that pair lack a consistent conservation interpretation
until \(\Jimp\) is corrected, for example by adding a feed capacitance current
\(\Jimp\leftarrow \Jimp+\jj\omega\varepsilon_0\E\) in the impressed-source slot
\cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}. This is the operational meaning of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.source-admissibility}: the source tuple must lie on the
charge-continuous manifold before angular transport diagnostics are applied.


\subsection{Initial-boundary value problems and uniqueness}
\label{sec:ch01.1.3}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

The heading has two technical parts that must be separated before they are linked.
An \emph{initial-boundary value problem} specifies both time initialization and
boundary trace class for Maxwell evolution. \emph{Uniqueness} asks whether one
admissible dataset can generate more than one electromagnetic state. Without
uniqueness, subsequent statements on power, momentum, and angular transport lose
physical identifiability \cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001}.

Start from the time-domain formulation in a region \(\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^3\)
with boundary \(\Gamma\). Let \(\E(\mathbf{r},t)\), \(\H(\mathbf{r},t)\) be real
fields, \(\mathbf{D}\), \(\mathbf{B}\) flux densities, \(\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{imp}}\)
impressed source, and \(\rho\) free-charge density:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ibvp-time-domain}
\begin{aligned}
\curl \E(\mathbf{r},t)&=-\partial_t\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r},t),\\
\curl \H(\mathbf{r},t)&=\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{imp}}(\mathbf{r},t)+\partial_t\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{r},t),\\
\divg \mathbf{D}(\mathbf{r},t)&=\rho(\mathbf{r},t),\qquad
\divg \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r},t)=0.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Initial data are \(\E(\mathbf{r},0)=\E_0(\mathbf{r})\),
\(\H(\mathbf{r},0)=\H_0(\mathbf{r})\), and boundary data are imposed through a
trace operator \(\mathcal{B}_\Gamma\). Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.ibvp-time-domain}
specifies local dynamics, but the pair \((\E_0,\H_0)\) and boundary trace class
determine which trajectory is selected from all formally admissible trajectories
\cite{harrington2001,collin2001,jackson1999}.

For monochromatic steady operation with factor \(\exp(-\jj\omega t)\), the same
problem reduces to a frequency-domain boundary value problem:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ibvp-harmonic-bvp}
\begin{aligned}
\curl \E&=-\jj\omega\mathbf{B},\qquad
\curl \H=\Jimp+\jj\omega\mathbf{D},\\
\divg \mathbf{D}&=\rho,\qquad
\divg \mathbf{B}=0,\qquad
\mathcal{B}_\Gamma[\E,\H]=\mathbf{g}_\Gamma.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Hence, in the phasor regime, initial data enter only through consistency with the
selected harmonic branch; boundary traces and constitutive model then govern
solvability and uniqueness for each \(\omega\) \cite{collin2001,jackson1999}.

To formulate uniqueness, consider two solutions
\((\E_1,\H_1)\), \((\E_2,\H_2)\) for the same dataset
\((\Jimp,\rho,\mathcal{B}_\Gamma,\mathbf{g}_\Gamma)\), and define
\(\Delta\E=\E_1-\E_2\), \(\Delta\H=\H_1-\H_2\). The difference fields satisfy the
homogeneous system
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.uniqueness-difference-system}
\begin{aligned}
\curl \Delta\E&=-\jj\omega\mu\,\Delta\H,\qquad
\curl \Delta\H=\jj\omega\varepsilon\,\Delta\E,\\
\divg(\varepsilon\Delta\E)&=0,\qquad
\divg(\mu\Delta\H)=0,\qquad
\mathcal{B}_\Gamma[\Delta\E,\Delta\H]=\mathbf{0},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
for constitutive parameters \(\varepsilon,\mu\) in the chosen model class. This
homogeneous reduction is the precise mathematical content of uniqueness testing
\cite{harrington2001,collin2001,jackson1999}.

The uniqueness statement is therefore
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.uniqueness-null}
\mathcal{B}_{\chi,\Gamma}[\Delta\E,\Delta\H]=0
\Longrightarrow
\Delta\E=\mathbf{0},\ \Delta\H=\mathbf{0},
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{B}_{\chi,\Gamma}\) denotes Maxwell operator with constitutive set
\(\chi\) and boundary class \(\Gamma\) fixed. Left side means zero residual under
homogeneous forcing; right side means trivial null branch only. Equation
\eqref{eq:ch1.uniqueness-null} is therefore a null-space condition on the
homogeneous operator, and physically it states that fixed data define one field
state rather than a family of hidden alternatives.

An energy identity for the difference state provides the key proof channel. Form
the complex Poynting vector
\(\mathbf{S}_\Delta=\tfrac12\Delta\E\times\Delta\H^{*}\), integrate over
\(\Omega\), and use divergence theorem:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.uniqueness-energy-identity}
\int_{\Gamma}\hat{\mathbf{n}}\cdot\mathbf{S}_\Delta\,dS
=-\frac12\int_{\Omega}\Delta\E\cdot\Delta\Jimp^{*}\,dV
-\jj\omega\!\int_{\Omega}\!\left(u_{\Delta H}-u_{\Delta E}\right)dV,
\end{equation}
where \(u_{\Delta E}=\tfrac14\varepsilon|\Delta\E|^{2}\),
\(u_{\Delta H}=\tfrac14\mu|\Delta\H|^{2}\), and \(\Delta\Jimp=\mathbf{0}\) for the
homogeneous problem. Boundary classes determine the real part of the surface term:
for passive traces it is nonnegative outgoing dissipation or radiation, while for
ideal lossless closed boundaries it vanishes \cite{harrington2001,collin2001}.

A sufficient coercive estimate is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.uniqueness-estimate}
\Re\!\left\langle
\mathcal{B}_{\chi,\Gamma}[\Delta\E,\Delta\H],(\Delta\E,\Delta\H)
\right\rangle
\ge c_{\chi,\Gamma}
\left(\|\Delta\E\|_{L^2(\Omega)^3}^{2}+\|\Delta\H\|_{L^2(\Omega)^3}^{2}\right),
\end{equation}
with coercivity constant \(c_{\chi,\Gamma}>0\). If the residual is zero, the left
side is zero; positivity of \(c_{\chi,\Gamma}\) forces both norms to vanish, hence
\(\Delta\E=\Delta\H=\mathbf{0}\). Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.uniqueness-estimate}
therefore converts analytical coercivity into physical identifiability.

\begin{proposition}[Uniqueness under passive admissible closure]
\label{prop:ch1.uniqueness-passive-closure}
Assume: (i) admissible source-boundary data from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.admissible-data-class}; (ii) passive constitutive response
\(\Re(\varepsilon)\ge 0\), \(\Re(\mu)\ge 0\) in \(\Omega\); (iii) boundary class
with nonnegative real power flux in the homogeneous difference problem. Then the
solution of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ibvp-harmonic-bvp} is unique.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Let \(\mathcal{F}_1=(\E_1,\H_1)\) and \(\mathcal{F}_2=(\E_2,\H_2)\) be two solutions
for the same data tuple. Set \(\Delta\E=\E_1-\E_2\), \(\Delta\H=\H_1-\H_2\). By
linearity, Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.uniqueness-difference-system} holds with
\(\Delta\Jimp=\mathbf{0}\). Insert \(\mathbf{S}_\Delta=\tfrac12\Delta\E\times\Delta\H^*\)
into Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.uniqueness-energy-identity}. Passivity and the stated boundary
class force \(\Re\int_\Gamma\hat{\mathbf{n}}\cdot\mathbf{S}_\Delta\,dS\ge 0\) with
zero forcing, hence \(\Delta\E=\Delta\H=\mathbf{0}\) \cite{harrington2001,jackson1999}.
\end{proof}

Uniqueness is frequency dependent because the homogeneous operator spectrum depends
on \(\omega\). Near resonant or poorly damped eigenfrequencies, null-space
amplification can occur unless radiation, loss, or additional constraints suppress
nontrivial homogeneous modes \cite{harrington2001,collin2001,jackson1999}. A
practical indicator is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.uniqueness-condition-index}
\kappa_{\mathrm{uniq}}(\omega)=
\frac{1}{\inf_{\|(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v})\|=1}
\|\mathcal{B}_{\chi,\Gamma,\omega}[\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}]\|},
\end{equation}
where large \(\kappa_{\mathrm{uniq}}(\omega)\) indicates weak coercivity and
increased sensitivity to modeling and discretization errors.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1.uniqueness-map} emphasizes a pedagogical point often missed in

but from Maxwell equations plus admissible constitutive and boundary closure. The
difference-system route is therefore central rather than optional.

The validity envelope is explicit. The derivation assumes standard trace regularity
for \(H(\curl)\)-admissible fields, constitutive passivity or controlled loss, and
boundary classes without uncontrolled active gain
\cite{harrington2001,collin2001,jackson1999}. In active media with net gain,
strongly nonlocal interfaces, non-selfadjoint inverse closures, or ill-conditioned
discretizations, coercivity can fail and multiple branches may coexist
\cite{jackson1999,balanis2016}. Within the admitted class, however, the initial-
boundary formulation and uniqueness criterion together guarantee that one admissible
dataset corresponds to one physically interpretable electromagnetic state.

\paragraph{Worked problem: PEC cavity uniqueness.}
Consider a rectangular cavity \(\Omega=[0,a]\times[0,b]\times[0,c]\) with perfectly
conducting walls (\(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times\E=\mathbf{0}\)) and no impressed sources
(\(\Jimp=\mathbf{0}\), \(\rho=0\)). Seek time-harmonic modes
\(\E(\mathbf{r})=\mathbf{E}_{mnp}\sin(k_xx)\sin(k_yy)\sin(k_zz)\) satisfying
\(\nabla^2\E+k_0^2\E=\mathbf{0}\) with \(k_x= m\pi/a\), \(k_y=n\pi/b\),
\(k_z=p\pi/c\). Uniqueness of the excited branch at fixed \((m,n,p)\) follows from
Proposition~\ref{prop:ch1.uniqueness-passive-closure}: subtract two candidate
solutions to form \(\Delta\E\), apply
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.uniqueness-energy-identity}, and note that passive PEC walls make
\(\Re\int_{\Gamma}\hat{\mathbf{n}}\cdot\mathbf{S}_\Delta\,dS\ge 0\) while the
volume source term vanishes. The only compatible homogeneous branch is
\(\Delta\E=\mathbf{0}\) \cite{harrington2001,jackson1999}.

For the lowest nonzero TM mode \(m=n=1\), \(p=0\), the eigenfrequency follows from
\(k_0^2=k_x^2+k_y^2=(\pi/a)^2+(\pi/b)^2\), hence
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.worked-cavity-frequency}
f_{110}=\frac{c}{2\pi}\sqrt{\left(\frac{\pi}{a}\right)^2+\left(\frac{\pi}{b}\right)^2}.
\end{equation}
With \(a=b=0.1\,\mathrm{m}\), Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.worked-cavity-frequency} gives
\(f_{110}\approx 2.12\,\mathrm{GHz}\). The corresponding field
\(\mathbf{E}_{110}=E_0\sin(\pi x/a)\sin(\pi y/b)\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) has vanishing
normal component on all PEC walls, so the boundary operator in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.boundary-data-operator} is satisfied. Applying
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.uniqueness-energy-identity} to the difference field
\(\Delta\E=\E^{(1)}-\E^{(2)}\) at fixed excitation frequency yields
\[
\int_\Omega \Re\!\left\{\Delta\E\cdot\curl\Delta\H^*\right\}dV
+\Re\int_{\Gamma_{\mathrm{PEC}}}\hat{\mathbf{n}}\cdot\mathbf{S}_\Delta\,dS=0.
\]
The volume integral vanishes for homogeneous cavity modes, and passive PEC walls
make the surface term nonnegative, forcing \(\Delta\E=\mathbf{0}\). A student
reading only the mode shape might confuse visual nodal structure with ambiguity;
the energy identity shows that, once \((m,n,p)\) and frequency are fixed, no
second physically distinct solution exists in the admitted class
\cite{harrington2001,collin2001}.


\subsection{Energy, linear momentum, and Maxwell stress tensor}
\label{sec:ch01.1.4}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

The three heading components are coupled: energy transport and storage, linear
momentum transfer, and Maxwell stress representation of traction. In the
\(\exp(-\jj\omega t)\) phasor convention, define
\(\mathbf{S}=\tfrac{1}{2}\E\times\H^*\), where \((\cdot)^*\) is complex
conjugation. Define stored-energy densities
\(u_E=\tfrac{1}{4}\varepsilon_0\E\cdot\E^*\) and
\(u_H=\tfrac{1}{4}\mu_0\H\cdot\H^*\). The complex power balance is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.poynting-complex}
\divg\mathbf{S}
=-\frac{1}{2}\E\cdot\Jimp^*
-\jj\omega\left(u_H-u_E\right).
\end{equation}
Term-level interpretation is explicit. The divergence term gives net power outflow
density from a differential control volume. The source term
\(\E\cdot\Jimp^*/2\) is electromagnetic work rate on impressed current. The term
\(-\jj\omega(u_H-u_E)\) is reactive exchange between magnetic and electric storage
channels. Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.poynting-complex} therefore couples transport,
source work, and storage imbalance in one local conservation statement.

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.poynting-real-imag-split}
\begin{aligned}
\divg\Re(\mathbf{S})&=-\frac12\Re\!\left(\E\cdot\Jimp^*\right),\\
\divg\Im(\mathbf{S})&=-\frac12\Im\!\left(\E\cdot\Jimp^*\right)-\omega(u_H-u_E).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.poynting-real-imag-split} separates transported average
power from reactive storage exchange under one harmonic field state.

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.power-balance-integral}
\int_{\partial V}\mathbf{S}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS
=-\frac12\int_V\E\cdot\Jimp^*\,dV
-\jj\omega\int_V(u_H-u_E)\,dV.
\end{equation}
This control-volume relation provides direct finite-set interpretation for probe
and aperture measurements.

Splitting into real and imaginary parts gives the measurement-relevant separation:
\(\Re(\mathbf{S})\) controls net transported power density and
\(\Im(\mathbf{S})\) controls reactive circulation associated with local
near-field energy exchange. This distinction prevents reactive phase structure from
being misread as long-range momentum transport.

Linear momentum flux is represented through the time-averaged Maxwell stress
tensor. In vacuum its dyadic form is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress}
\mathbf{T}
=\frac{1}{2}\Re\!\left[
\varepsilon_0\,\E\E^*
+\mu_0\,\H\H^*
-\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{I}
\left(\varepsilon_0\,\E\cdot\E^*+\mu_0\,\H\cdot\H^*\right)
\right],
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{I}\) is the identity dyad and juxtaposition such as \(\E\E^*\)
is dyadic product. Operand by operand, the first two dyads are directional field
intensity contributions, while the isotropic subtraction term
\(\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{I}(\cdots)\) removes trace pressure so that directional
tractions are represented correctly. The real-part operator keeps physically
observable time-averaged transfer.

As a complete object, Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress} maps electromagnetic
field state to mechanical traction on material control surfaces. For unit normal
\(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\), the traction density is
\(\mathbf{t}=\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\), and the net force on closed
surface \(\partial V\) is \(\mathbf{F}=\int_{\partial V}\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS\).
No new numbered equation is introduced for these definitions because they are
direct functional uses of the already-established stress tensor.

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.linear-momentum-balance}
\divg\mathbf{T}+\mathbf{f}_{\mathrm{em}}=\mathbf{0},
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{f}_{\mathrm{em}}\) denotes the time-averaged force density
delivered by the field to matter. This equation is the local momentum-balance
counterpart of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.poynting-complex}.

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.energy-momentum-directional-ratio}
\Pi_{\hat{\mathbf{q}}}
:=
\frac{\hat{\mathbf{q}}\cdot\Re(\mathbf{S})}
     {c\,\hat{\mathbf{q}}\cdot(\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{q}})+\epsilon_\Pi},
\end{equation}
for unit probe direction \(\hat{\mathbf{q}}\), vacuum speed \(c\), and fixed
nondimensional regularizer \(\epsilon_\Pi>0\). \(\Pi_{\hat{\mathbf{q}}}\) quantifies
directional agreement between energetic and mechanical transport channels.

\begin{proposition}[Energy-momentum consistency under passive closure]
\label{prop:ch1.energy-momentum-consistency}
Assume admissible data \(\mathfrak{D}\in\mathbb{A}_\omega\), passive boundary
closure, and vacuum constitutive law. Then finite exported real power through
\(\partial V\) implies finite integrated electromagnetic traction on \(\partial V\),
with both channels determined by the same solution branch \((\E,\H)\).
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.poynting-complex} and Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress} are
quadratic forms of \((\E,\H)\). Under admissibility and passivity, field norms are
finite in the control-volume class. Therefore both surface integrals remain finite,
and both are consistent descriptions of transport generated by one field state.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1.energy-momentum-control} links the two balance channels on the
same control set: \(\mathbf{S}\) governs energetic exchange and \(\mathbf{T}\)
governs mechanical traction exchange. Reading them together is essential when field
patterns exhibit strong spatial structure, because power circulation and momentum
transfer need not be co-located.

Equations~\eqref{eq:ch1.poynting-complex} and
\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress} are written for vacuum linear local closure and
time-harmonic averaging. In dispersive, anisotropic, or strongly time-varying
media, additional constitutive terms and averaging care are required before using
the same balance form without modification
\cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001,balanis2016}. Active boundaries or
gain media can violate passive-balance assumptions and require augmented source
terms in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.linear-momentum-balance}.

\paragraph{Worked problem: plane-wave power and momentum flux on a finite aperture.}
Consider \(\E=E_0 e^{\jj k_0 z}\hat{\mathbf{x}}\), \(\H=(E_0/\eta_0)e^{\jj k_0 z}\hat{\mathbf{y}}\)
in vacuum. From Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.poynting-complex}, \(\mathbf{S}=(|E_0|^2/2\eta_0)\hat{\mathbf{z}}\)
is uniform, so power through a disk of radius \(a\) normal to \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.worked-aperture-power}
P(a)=\int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^a \Re(\mathbf{S}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{z}})\,\rho\,d\rho\,d\phi
=\frac{\pi a^2 |E_0|^2}{2\eta_0}.
\end{equation}
The Maxwell stress of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress} reduces to
\(\mathbf{T}=\tfrac12\varepsilon_0|E_0|^2(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\hat{\mathbf{x}}+\hat{\mathbf{y}}\hat{\mathbf{y}})\)
for this TEM wave. Traction on the disk face \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}=\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) is
\(\mathbf{t}=\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{z}}=\mathbf{0}\): no normal momentum flux
through a transverse aperture, consistent with purely tangential field components.
On a hemispherical cap enclosing the same beam, however, the integrated traction is
nonzero and equals \(\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{em}}/c^2\) times the solid-angle fraction
intercepted \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}. This exercise shows why momentum
reports require explicit surface geometry: identical \(\E,\H\) produce different
integrated traction depending on whether the sensing surface is transverse, oblique,
or closed \cite{collin2001,balanis2016}.


\subsection{Angular momentum conservation and torque balance}
\label{sec:ch01.1.5}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

The subsection heading contains two coupled requirements. ``Angular momentum
conservation'' means that rotational field storage and rotational flux obey a local
balance law derived from Maxwell dynamics. ``Torque balance'' means that any net
change in field angular momentum is accounted by mechanical torque delivered to
matter. Without this pair, visual phase winding cannot be interpreted as physical
rotational transport \cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001}.

Let \(\mathbf{r}\) be position vector measured from a declared origin
\(\mathbf{r}_0\), let \(\mathbf{T}\) be Maxwell stress tensor from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress}, and define angular-flux tensor
\(\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{T}\). The local rotational balance law is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.angular-momentum-balance}
\frac{\partial\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}}{\partial t}
+\divg\mathbf{M}
=-\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\mathrm{mech}},
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}\) is electromagnetic angular-momentum density and
\(\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\mathrm{mech}}\) is mechanical torque density on matter.
\(\partial_t\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}\) tracks local rotational storage variation,
\(\divg\mathbf{M}\) tracks net angular-flux leakage, and the right-hand side is
field-to-matter exchange channel \cite{harrington2001,collin2001,jackson1999}.

Integrated control-volume form over \(V\subset\Omega\) with boundary
\(\partial V\) and outward normal \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\) is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.angular-balance-integral}
\frac{d}{dt}\int_V \mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}\,dV
+\int_{\partial V}\mathbf{M}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS
=-\int_V \boldsymbol{\tau}_{\mathrm{mech}}\,dV.
\end{equation}
The first term is stored angular content inside \(V\); the second is exported or
imported angular flux through \(\partial V\); the right term is net mechanical
torque uptake by enclosed matter.

In linear isotropic vacuum, a useful density split is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.angular-density-split}
\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}
=\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{em}}+\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{em}},\qquad
\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{em}}=\varepsilon_0\mu_0\,\Re(\mathbf{S}),
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{em}}\) is linear-momentum density and
\(\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{em}}\) is spin-associated local contribution. The first term
is orbital-type contribution about \(\mathbf{r}_0\), while \(\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{em}}\)
captures intrinsic polarization-linked contribution under the adopted averaging
framework \cite{jackson1999,collin2001}.

For torque observables about axis \(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\), define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.torque-observable}
\tau_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}(V)
=-\hat{\mathbf{u}}\cdot
\left(
\frac{d}{dt}\int_V \mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}\,dV
+\int_{\partial V}\mathbf{M}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS
\right),
\end{equation}
which is the axis-projected version of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.angular-balance-integral}.
This form makes measurement geometry explicit: torque is a projected transport
functional, not a scalar label inferred from phase pictures.

Directional rotational-transport concentration can be quantified by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.angular-flux-directional-index}
\xi_J(\theta,\phi;r)
:=
\frac{\left\|\mathbf{M}(r,\theta,\phi)\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}}\right\|}
     {\int_{4\pi}\left\|\mathbf{M}(r,\theta',\phi')\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}}'\right\|\,d\Omega'+\epsilon_J},
\end{equation}
with \(\epsilon_J>0\) fixed nondimensional regularizer. \(\xi_J\) identifies which
solid-angle channels dominate exported angular flux and therefore dominate
direction-selective torque response.

\begin{proposition}[Torque observability criterion]
\label{prop:ch1.torque-observable-criterion}
Consider two admissible states \(\mathcal{F}_1,\mathcal{F}_2\) at common radius
\(r\), with equal total radiated power but different directional angular-flux
densities \(\mathbf{M}_1\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}}\neq
\mathbf{M}_2\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}}\) on a nonzero-measure subset of \(4\pi\). Then
there exists at least one physically admissible interaction kernel for which the
measured torque differs between the two states.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Use Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.torque-observable} with a directional weighting kernel that
selects the subset where fluxes differ. Equal total power constrains only energetic
integral transport and does not constrain the angular-flux distribution channel by
channel; hence projected torque functionals can differ.

\centering
\fbox{\parbox{0.92\linewidth}{
\textbf{Torque-balance control-volume schematic.}
A control volume \(V\) centered at declared origin \(\mathbf{r}_0\) exchanges
angular flux \(\mathbf{M}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\) through \(\partial V\); interior
matter receives torque density \(\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\mathrm{mech}}\).}}
\caption{Control-volume relation between electromagnetic angular-momentum flux and measurable mechanical torque in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.angular-balance-integral}.}
\label{fig:ch1.torque-balance-control}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1.torque-balance-control} emphasizes origin declaration and
surface orientation bookkeeping: both are essential for reproducible torque
comparison across experiments and simulations.

\paragraph{Worked problem: torque balance on a closed radiative surface.}
For harmonic fields in vacuum with no mechanical loading inside \(V\),
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.angular-balance-integral} reduces at steady state to
\(\tau_z^{\mathrm{mech}}=-\int_{\partial V}[\mathbf{r}\times(\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})]\cdot\hat{\mathbf{z}}\,dS\).
On sphere \(S_R\), use \(\mathbf{r}=R\hat{\mathbf{r}}\) and
\(dS=R^2\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi\). For the linearly polarized shell of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.worked-rad-power}, symmetry under \(\phi\to\phi+\pi\) forces
the integrand to change sign under \(\phi\to\phi+\pi\) when \(m=0\), giving
\(\tau_z^{\mathrm{mech}}=0\) after full solid-angle integration. For the helical
shell of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.worked-helical-torque}, azimuthal phase breaks that
symmetry and yields nonzero \(\tau_z^{\mathrm{mech}}\propto -mP_{\mathrm{rad}}/\omega\).
The same conclusion follows whether one integrates \(\mathbf{M}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\)
directly or projects through Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.torque-observable}, confirming that
torque is an integral transport report, not a local phase label
\cite{jackson1999,harrington2001,collin2001}.

Equations~\eqref{eq:ch1.angular-momentum-balance}
and \eqref{eq:ch1.angular-balance-integral} assume declared origin, admissible
stress-tensor closure, and local constitutive behavior in the present regime. In
strongly nonlocal media, active metasurfaces, microscopic spin-lattice coupling,
or unresolved subwavelength torque channels, additional terms may be required
before torque partition is interpreted without ambiguity
\cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001,balanis2016}.

For forward use, rotational interpretation is accepted only when a strict
transport-qualified criterion holds:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.torque-transport-qualified-criterion}
\mathcal{Q}_{J}=1
\iff
\Big(
\|\mathbf{M}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\|_{L^1(\partial V)}>0,\ 
\|\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\mathrm{mech}}\|_{L^1(V)}>0,\ 
\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{adm}}\le \varepsilon_{\mathrm{adm}}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{adm}}\) is the admissibility residual from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.admissibility-residual}, and \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{adm}}>0\) is
a prescribed acceptance threshold. Thus a phase or polarization pattern is called
rotationally meaningful only when nonzero angular-flux transfer and nonzero torque
channel are simultaneously present under admissible data constraints.

This criterion provides the exact bridge to Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2}: directional
and topological descriptors introduced there will be treated as physically
credible only if they satisfy \(\mathcal{Q}_{J}=1\), so interpretation remains
anchored to transport and torque observables rather than visual morphology alone.

\paragraph{Worked problem: radiating spherical shell.}
Let \(\E,\H\) be outward-propagating fields on a sphere \(S_R\) in vacuum, with
\(\H=\eta_0^{-1}\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\E\) and \(\E=E_0 e^{-jk_0 r}/r\). Then
\(\mathbf{S}=\tfrac12\E\times\H^*=(|E_0|^2/2\eta_0 r^2)\hat{\mathbf{r}}\) and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.worked-rad-power}
P_{\mathrm{rad}}(R)=\int_{S_R}\Re(\mathbf{S}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}})\,dS
=\frac{4\pi |E_0|^2}{2\eta_0}.
\end{equation}
The axis-projected torque of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.torque-observable} vanishes for this
linearly polarized shell; adding helical phase \(\exp(\jj m\phi)\) modifies
\(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\) without changing Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.worked-rad-power}
\cite{jackson1999,balanis2016}.

\paragraph{Worked problem: helical shell with nonzero torque channel.}
Retain the radiating shell of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.worked-rad-power} but impose
\(\E=E_0 e^{-jk_0 r}e^{\jj m\phi}/r\) with integer \(m\neq 0\) and
\(\H=\eta_0^{-1}\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\E\). On \(S_R\), the traction from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress} produces axis torque density
\([(\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{T})\cdot\hat{\mathbf{z}}]\propto m/\omega\) times local
power flux. Integrating over \(S_R\) and using
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.angular-balance-integral} gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.worked-helical-torque}
\tau_z^{\mathrm{mech}}
\approx -\frac{m}{\omega}\,P_{\mathrm{rad}}(R),
\end{equation}
where \(P_{\mathrm{rad}}(R)\) is unchanged from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.worked-rad-power}. For \(m=1\), \(\omega=2\pi\times 10\,\mathrm{GHz}\),
and \(P_{\mathrm{rad}}=1\,\mathrm{W}\), Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.worked-helical-torque}
predicts \(|\tau_z^{\mathrm{mech}}|\approx 1.59\times 10^{-11}\,\mathrm{N\,m}\),
a small but nonzero load torque entirely fixed by exported power and helical index.
The linearly polarized shell (\(m=0\)) and helical shell (\(m=1\)) share the same
\(P_{\mathrm{rad}}\) yet differ in \(\tau_z\), illustrating why power alone cannot
certify angular transport \cite{jackson1999,balanis2016}.

\paragraph{Section 1.1 summary and transition.}
Section~\ref{sec:ch01.1} closes the Maxwell conservation chain---field laws,
admissible sources and boundaries, uniqueness, and the energy--momentum--angular
balance laws that fix measurable transfer channels
\cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001}. Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2} now asks
which directional and phase-polarization descriptors inherit transport meaning from
those balances rather than from visual pattern alone
\cite{harrington2001,collin2001,balanis2016}.


\section{Angular Structure of Electromagnetic Fields}
\label{sec:ch01.2}

Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2} develops the meaning of angular structure from
field quantities and transport criteria rather than from phase-pattern naming.
Its scope is to separate directional geometry, polarization-phase organization,
and genuine angular-momentum carriage under explicit radiative and reactive
regimes, so that each later modal interpretation remains tied to an operational
electromagnetic criterion.


\subsection{Directional dependence and angular momentum}
\label{sec:ch01.2.1}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Directional dependence is the first operational filter in angular analysis. A field
distribution can satisfy global energy conservation and still fail to produce
directional rotational transfer in the channels used by a detector or scatterer.
Therefore, this subsection defines angular interpretation through direction-resolved
transport observables rather than through visual phase morphology
\cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001}.

The transition from Section~\ref{sec:ch01.1} is governed by the strict gate
\(\mathcal{Q}_{J}=1\) in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.torque-transport-qualified-criterion}. Any directional angular
claim made below is physically accepted only when that gate remains satisfied; if
the gate fails, the same directional pattern is treated as kinematic appearance
without verified rotational transfer meaning.

Let \(\hat{\mathbf{r}}=\hat{\mathbf{r}}(\theta,\phi)\) be radial unit vector on a
radius-\(r\) sphere and \(d\Omega=\sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi\) the solid-angle
measure. Using \(\mathbf{S}\) from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.poynting-complex}, define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.directional-power-density}
\Pi(\theta,\phi;r)=
r^2\,\Re\!\left\{\mathbf{S}(r,\theta,\phi)\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}}\right\}.
\end{equation}
The product \(\mathbf{S}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}}\) selects outward energetic transport
in that channel, while \(r^2\) rescales local flux density to per-solid-angle
channel power.

The integrated total is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.directional-power-integral}
P_{\mathrm{rad}}(r)=\int_{4\pi}\Pi(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.directional-power-integral} is a projection of channelwise
structure. Equal projected totals do not imply equal directional physics.

For rotational transport, define from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.angular-momentum-balance}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.directional-am-flux}
\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J(\theta,\phi;r)=
r^2\,\mathbf{M}(r,\theta,\phi)\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}}.
\end{equation}
Here \(\mathbf{M}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}}\) is direction-resolved angular-flux
transfer. The pair \((\Pi,\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J)\) is the minimal dataset required to
separate energetic transport from rotational transport channel by channel.

The corresponding total outward angular flux is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.directional-am-total}
\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{rad}}(r)=
\int_{4\pi}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega.
\end{equation}
This vector integral cannot replace channelwise structure because cancellation among
opposing angular channels can leave a small total despite large directional flux
magnitudes.

A normalized directional efficiency observable is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.directional-transport-ratio}
\boldsymbol{\eta}_J(\theta,\phi;r)=
\frac{\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J(\theta,\phi;r)}
{\Pi(\theta,\phi;r)+\epsilon_\Pi},
\qquad \epsilon_\Pi>0.
\end{equation}
\(\epsilon_\Pi\) regularizes channels near energetic nulls. Large
\(\|\boldsymbol{\eta}_J\|\) identifies directions where modest power supports strong
rotational transfer, which is common in highly structured emission fields.

Channel qualification is imposed explicitly by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.directional-qualified-gate}
\mathcal{Q}_{J,\Omega}(\theta,\phi;r)=1
\iff
\Big(
\Pi(\theta,\phi;r)>0,\
\|\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J(\theta,\phi;r)\|>0,\
\mathcal{Q}_{J}=1
\Big).
\end{equation}
Thus only channels with \(\mathcal{Q}_{J,\Omega}=1\) contribute to transport-
qualified angular interpretation in later subsections.

Directional centroids are defined by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.directional-power-centroid}
\mathbf{c}_P(r)=
\frac{\int_{4\pi}\hat{\mathbf{r}}\,\Pi(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega}
{\int_{4\pi}\Pi(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.directional-am-centroid}
\mathbf{c}_J(r)=
\frac{\int_{4\pi}\hat{\mathbf{r}}\,
\|\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J(\theta,\phi;r)\|\,d\Omega}
{\int_{4\pi}\|\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J(\theta,\phi;r)\|\,d\Omega}.
\end{equation}
Mismatch \(\mathbf{c}_P\neq\mathbf{c}_J\) means dominant energetic direction and
dominant rotational direction are geometrically displaced, a measurable effect in
torque-sensitive coupling experiments.

\begin{proposition}[Directional non-equivalence criterion]
\label{prop:ch1.directional-nonequivalence}
Let two radiative states \(\mathcal{F}_1,\mathcal{F}_2\) at radius \(r\) satisfy
\(P_{\mathrm{rad}}^{(1)}(r)=P_{\mathrm{rad}}^{(2)}(r)\). If there exists a set
\(\mathcal{U}\subset4\pi\) of nonzero measure where
\(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J^{(1)}\neq\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J^{(2)}\) and
\(\mathcal{Q}_{J,\Omega}=1\), then the states are rotationally non-equivalent for
direction-selective interactions.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
The equality of \(P_{\mathrm{rad}}\) constrains only
\(\int_{4\pi}\Pi\,d\Omega\). It does not constrain channelwise rotational flux.
Define admissible interaction kernel \(K(\theta,\phi)\) supported on \(\mathcal{U}\):
\[
\mathbf{T}_{\mathrm{obs}}=
\int_{4\pi}K(\theta,\phi)\,\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega.
\]
Since qualified channels differ, observed torque differs.

\centering
\fbox{\parbox{0.93\linewidth}{
\textbf{Directional transport decomposition on an observation sphere.}
Each channel \((\theta,\phi)\) carries energetic transfer \(\Pi\) and rotational
transfer \(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\). The qualified subset selected by
\(\mathcal{Q}_{J,\Omega}=1\) defines the physically credible torque-carrying sector.}}
\caption{Direction-resolved energetic and rotational transport under the strict transport-qualified interpretation criterion inherited from Section~\ref{sec:ch01.1}.}
\label{fig:ch1.directional-transport-decomposition}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1.directional-transport-decomposition} clarifies that integrated
totals are terminal reductions of richer channel fields. Different channel topology
can produce nearly identical totals while yielding different torque outcomes.

Origin declaration remains essential because angular transport is moment-based.
Changing origin re-weights channel moments even when global balances remain
unchanged. For reproducible comparison, all directional flux and torque statements
must use the same origin convention fixed in Section~\ref{sec:ch01.1.5}.

Directional observables represent physical transport
only when outward channels are well-defined and \(\mathcal{Q}_{J}=1\) remains valid.
In strongly reactive near zones, strongly nonlocal media, or rapidly modulated
boundaries, extra decomposition terms may be necessary before \(\Pi\) and
\(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\) are treated as direct rotational-transfer observables
\cite{harrington2001,collin2001,jackson1999,balanis2016}.

\paragraph{Worked problem: equal power, unequal directional torque.}
Two far-zone states \(\mathcal{F}_A,\mathcal{F}_B\) on radius \(r\) share
\(P_{\mathrm{rad}}^{(A)}=P_{\mathrm{rad}}^{(B)}=P_0\) from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.directional-power-integral}. State~A carries angular flux only
in sector \(\Delta\Omega_+\) with \(\int_{\Delta\Omega_+}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J^{(A)}\,d\Omega
=\mathbf{J}_+\hat{\mathbf{z}}\), while state~B splits equal magnitude flux between
\(\Delta\Omega_+\) and opposite sector \(\Delta\Omega_-\) with
\(\int_{\Delta\Omega_-}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J^{(B)}\,d\Omega=-\mathbf{J}_+\hat{\mathbf{z}}\).
Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.worked-directional-cancellation}
\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{rad}}^{(A)}=\mathbf{J}_+\hat{\mathbf{z}},\qquad
\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{rad}}^{(B)}=\mathbf{0},
\end{equation}
even though \(\int_{4\pi}\|\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J^{(B)}\|\,d\Omega\) may exceed
\(\int_{4\pi}\|\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J^{(A)}\|\,d\Omega\). The cancellation factor
\(\mathcal{K}_J\) of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.directional-cancellation-factor} is near
unity for~A and near zero for~B. A direction-selective interaction kernel
\(K(\theta,\phi)\) supported on \(\Delta\Omega_+\) therefore produces different
observed torques despite matched radiated power, confirming
Proposition~\ref{prop:ch1.directional-nonequivalence} in operational form
\cite{harrington2001,jackson1999}.

\subsection{Amplitude, phase, polarization, and Poynting topology}
\label{sec:ch01.2.2}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Amplitude, phase, polarization, and Poynting topology are treated here as a single
diagnostic chain rather than independent descriptors. Amplitude determines local
channel weight, phase controls interference geometry, polarization encodes vector
state of oscillation, and Poynting topology encodes flow organization. None of the
four has direct rotational-transport meaning unless the transport-qualified gate
from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.torque-transport-qualified-criterion} remains satisfied
\cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001}.

In local transverse basis
\((\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}},\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}})\), write
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.transverse-components}
\E_t=E_\theta\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}+E_\phi\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}},\qquad
E_\theta=A_\theta e^{\jj\varphi_\theta},\quad
E_\phi=A_\phi e^{\jj\varphi_\phi},
\end{equation}
with amplitudes \(A_\theta,A_\phi\ge 0\) and phases
\(\varphi_\theta,\varphi_\phi\). The relative phase
\(\Delta\varphi=\varphi_\phi-\varphi_\theta\) controls polarization coupling,
whereas common phase shift leaves transport observables unchanged.

Polarization coherence is encoded through
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.coherency-matrix}
\mathbf{J}_p=
\begin{bmatrix}
\langle E_\theta E_\theta^* \rangle & \langle E_\theta E_\phi^*\rangle\\
\langle E_\phi E_\theta^*\rangle & \langle E_\phi E_\phi^*\rangle
\end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
where \(\langle\cdot\rangle\) is local temporal/ensemble average. Diagonal entries
measure component intensity and off-diagonal entries measure phase-coherent coupling
between channels.

Stokes invariants are
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.stokes-local}
\begin{aligned}
S_0&=|E_\theta|^2+|E_\phi|^2,\\
S_1&=|E_\theta|^2-|E_\phi|^2,\\
S_2&=2\Re\!\left\{E_\theta E_\phi^*\right\},\\
S_3&=-2\Im\!\left\{E_\theta E_\phi^*\right\}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
\(S_2\) and \(S_3\) vanish without coherence between \(E_\theta\) and \(E_\phi\),
so elliptical/handed structure requires phase-coupled component channels.

Ellipse parameters follow by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.polarization-ellipse-angles}
\psi=\frac{1}{2}\operatorname{atan2}(S_2,S_1),\qquad
\chi=\frac{1}{2}\arcsin\!\left(\frac{S_3}{S_0+\epsilon_S}\right),
\end{equation}
where \(\psi\) is orientation, \(\chi\) ellipticity, and \(\epsilon_S>0\) is
regularizer near low-intensity states. These are representation coordinates, not
transport guarantees.

\paragraph{Concrete polarization walk-through.}
Take \(E_\theta=1\) and \(E_\phi=\jj\). Then \(S_0=2\), \(S_1=0\), \(S_2=0\),
\(S_3=-2\), giving circular polarization with \(\chi=-\pi/4\). The Poynting vector
rotates locally, yet equal outward channel flux can yield vanishing net angular
export. For \(E_\theta=1\), \(E_\phi=0.9e^{\jj\pi/3}\), one obtains
\(S_0\approx 3.61\), \(S_1\approx 0.19\), \(S_2\approx 0.78\), \(S_3\approx -1.56\):
elliptical coupling with nonzero \(\curl\mathbf{P}\) but possibly small
\(\int_{4\pi}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\,d\Omega\). Polarization fixes local geometry;
transport meaning requires the closure tests of Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2.3}
\cite{jackson1999,balanis2016}. For the linear-polarization case
\(E_\theta=1\), \(E_\phi=0\), Stokes parameters give \(S_3=0\) and vanishing
helicity density, so no preferred handedness is present even if \(\curl\mathbf{P}\)
is nonzero locally. For circular polarization \(E_\phi=\jj E_\theta\), \(S_3=\mp 2\)
and helicity density has definite sign, yet net torque still requires channel
imbalance as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-net-am-transport}. Students should therefore
treat polarization and phase winding as orthogonal diagnostics: the first labels
local vector state, the second labels azimuthal organization, and neither replaces
\(\mathcal{D}_J=1\) from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.transport-qualified-decision}.

Phase-geometry gradients are
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.local-phase-gradient}
\mathbf{k}_{\theta}=\nabla\varphi_\theta,\qquad
\mathbf{k}_{\phi}=\nabla\varphi_\phi,\qquad
\Delta\mathbf{k}=\nabla\Delta\varphi.
\end{equation}
\(\Delta\mathbf{k}\) measures local shear in polarization-coupling phase; large
\(\|\Delta\mathbf{k}\|\) indicates rapid local vector-state rotation.

Poynting-flow topology is read from
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.poynting-average}
\mathbf{P}=\Re\!\left\{\mathbf{S}\right\},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.poynting-vorticity}
\boldsymbol{\Omega}_P=\curl \mathbf{P}.
\end{equation}
\(\mathbf{P}\) is active transport density; \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}_P\) marks local
circulation of that transport. Local circulation alone does not certify exported
rotational transport.

Phase-singularity support satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.phase-singularity-condition}
A_\theta=A_\phi=0.
\end{equation}
For contour \(\mathcal{C}\) excluding singular points,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.phase-winding-number}
q=\frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_{\mathcal{C}}\nabla\arg(E_\theta+\jj E_\phi)\cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell},
\end{equation}
with \(q\in\mathbb{Z}\) classifying local winding class.

To enforce transport-qualified interpretation at local level, define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.topology-qualified-gate}
\mathcal{Q}_{\mathrm{top}}(\mathbf{r})=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{Q}_{J}=1,\
\|\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J(\theta,\phi;r)\|>0,\
\|\mathbf{P}(\mathbf{r})\|>0
\Big).
\end{equation}
Hence local topology markers are interpreted as rotationally relevant only where
global admissibility and directional angular-flux support are simultaneously
verified.

\begin{proposition}[Topology does not imply exported angular transport]
\label{prop:ch1.topology-transport-separation}
There exist fields with nontrivial local phase topology
\((q\neq 0,\ \boldsymbol{\Omega}_P\neq\mathbf{0})\) and nontrivial polarization
texture \((S_3\neq 0)\) for which exported angular flux across a closed sphere
vanishes after channel integration.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Construct counter-rotating channel pairs with matched energetic content and opposite
directional angular flux. Local winding and handedness remain nontrivial, yet
angular-flux integral cancels. Therefore local topology is not sufficient for
torque-capable export.

Two states can therefore share similar \(S_0\) maps while differing in

markers can yield weak exported torque when directional-flux cancellation dominates.

bandwidth sufficient for coherency inference. In strongly lossy, highly
inhomogeneous, or truncated-aperture settings, windowing/regularization are needed
before cross-section topology comparison becomes transport-credible
\cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001,balanis2016}.


\subsection{Conditions for angular-momentum transport}
\label{sec:ch01.2.3}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

A field configuration exhibits genuine angular-momentum transport only when
directional structure, conservation closure, and outward energetic support hold
simultaneously. Any one in isolation is insufficient. The diagnostic question is
not whether angular patterns are visible, but whether they survive flux accounting,
torque closure, and admissibility filtering under the transport-qualified regime
\cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001}.

Let \(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J(\theta,\phi;r)\) be the directional angular-momentum flux
map from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.directional-am-flux}, and let
\(\Pi(\theta,\phi;r)\) be directional power flux from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.directional-power-density}. A necessary directional condition
for nontrivial net transport over a chosen observation sphere is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.am-net-transport-condition}
\int_{4\pi}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega \neq \mathbf{0}.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.am-net-transport-condition} states that local angular
structure must accumulate into a nonzero exported balance; otherwise the pattern
is confined to internal recirculation or compensating directional cancellation.

Directional sufficiency, however, cannot be judged from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.am-net-transport-condition} alone. The same field must satisfy
global conservation consistency with the integral law already established in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.angular-balance-integral}. That relation rules out interpretive
shortcuts: exported angular transport must appear in torque or storage channels,
not in phase appearance alone \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}.

Transport classification also requires separation of outward propagative support
from reactive exchange. Using \(\mathbf{P}=\Re\{\mathbf{S}\}\) from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.poynting-average}, outward channel support for a directional
sector \(\Delta\Omega\subseteq 4\pi\) is checked by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.propagative-support}
\int_{\Delta\Omega}\Pi(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega>0.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.propagative-support} enforces that angular-momentum
claims are tied to sectors carrying net outward power; in sectors where this
condition fails, strong local field complexity may persist without transport
credibility.

The strict carryover from Section~\ref{sec:ch01.1} remains active:
\(\mathcal{Q}_{J}=1\) in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.torque-transport-qualified-criterion}
is mandatory before any directional claim is accepted as physical transport.

The three relations above define a transport triad:
\[
\mathcal{C}_1:\ \text{directional angular export }
(\eqref{eq:ch1.am-net-transport-condition}),\qquad
\mathcal{C}_2:\ \text{control-balance consistency }
(\eqref{eq:ch1.angular-balance-integral}),\qquad
\mathcal{C}_3:\ \text{propagative support }
(\eqref{eq:ch1.propagative-support}).
\]
To convert this triad into a quantitative admissibility test, introduce the
dimensionless transport index
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.transport-admissibility-index}
\Lambda_J(r)=
\frac{\left\|\int_{4\pi}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega\right\|}
{\int_{4\pi}\Pi(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega+\epsilon_\Lambda},
\qquad \epsilon_\Lambda>0,
\end{equation}
where \(\|\cdot\|\) is Euclidean norm and \(\epsilon_\Lambda\) regularizes weak
radiation windows. The numerator is net exported angular transport; the
denominator is total exported power. Large \(\Lambda_J\) indicates high rotational
transfer efficiency per unit outward power.

Directional cancellation can be measured independently through
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.directional-cancellation-factor}
\mathcal{K}_J(r)=
\frac{
\left\|\int_{4\pi}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\,d\Omega\right\|
}{
\int_{4\pi}\left\|\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\right\|\,d\Omega+\epsilon_K},
\qquad 0\le \mathcal{K}_J\le 1,\ \epsilon_K>0.
\end{equation}
Here \(\mathcal{K}_J\approx 1\) means directional channels add coherently toward
net export, while \(\mathcal{K}_J\approx 0\) means local angular flux is largely
self-canceling over solid angle. This factor prevents a common interpretive
failure where large local \(\|\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\|\) is mistaken for strong net
transport despite angular cancellation.

Because Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.angular-balance-integral} couples storage, flux, and mechanical
torque, the same criterion can be written in torque-normalized form:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.torque-consistency-residual}
\mathcal{R}_\tau(V)=
\left\|
\frac{d}{dt}\int_V \mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}\,dV
+\int_{\partial V}\mathbf{M}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS
+\int_V \boldsymbol{\tau}_{\mathrm{mech}}\,dV
\right\|,
\end{equation}
with \(\mathcal{R}_\tau(V)=0\) for exact conservation closure on admissible data.
Finite \(\mathcal{R}_\tau\) in experiments quantifies mismatch from measurement
noise, truncation, or model incompleteness.

For operational classification, define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.transport-qualified-decision}
\mathcal{D}_J(r)=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{Q}_J=1,\
\Lambda_J(r)\ge \Lambda_{\min},\
\mathcal{K}_J(r)\ge K_{\min},\
\mathcal{R}_\tau(V)\le R_{\max}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(\Lambda_{\min}>0\), \(0<K_{\min}\le 1\), and \(R_{\max}\ge 0\) are declared
study thresholds. This converts triad logic into a reproducible decision map for
simulation and measurement workflows.

\begin{proposition}[Triad sufficiency for transport interpretation]
\label{prop:ch1.transport-triad-sufficiency}
Assume a harmonic field state in the admissible class of Section~\ref{sec:ch01.1},
and suppose \(\mathcal{C}_1,\mathcal{C}_2,\mathcal{C}_3\) hold on an observation
sphere of radius \(r\). Then angular structure descriptors in that state admit a
transport interpretation: exported angular-momentum flux is nontrivial, consistent
with control-volume conservation, and supported by outward energy propagation.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
\(\mathcal{C}_1\) gives nonzero net angular export; \(\mathcal{C}_3\) ties export to
outward power support; \(\mathcal{C}_2\) closes the balance of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.angular-balance-integral}. Together they exclude
representation-only phase patterns \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001,collin2001}.
\end{proof}

Thus the proposition establishes structural sufficiency, while
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.transport-qualified-decision} establishes operational acceptance.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1.transport-triad-map} emphasizes that transport qualification is
a conjunction, not a menu. A failure in any branch demotes the state from
transport-capable to representation-rich but transport-incomplete. This
classification is operationally useful in both simulation and measurement, where
complex phase and polarization fields may satisfy one or two branches but not all
three.

Origin declaration remains part of the condition set because angular moments are
computed through \(\mathbf{r}\times(\cdot)\). Changing origin can redistribute
directional partitions and alter modal bookkeeping; transport conclusions are
therefore comparable only when origin and boundary geometry are fixed across the
comparison set.

Practically, the decision sequence is: compute \(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\) and
\(\Pi\), evaluate \(\Lambda_J\) and \(\mathcal{K}_J\), verify
\(\mathcal{R}_\tau\) within admissible tolerance, enforce \(\mathcal{Q}_J=1\), and
apply Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.transport-qualified-decision}. This ordering removes false
positives where vivid local structure is present but exported rotational action is
weak, self-canceling, or torque-inconsistent.

The triad and derived indices above assume harmonic
steady state, sufficiently resolved directional sampling on \(4\pi\), and
constitutive closure in the linear local regime used in this chapter. Strongly
time-varying boundaries, broadband pulse windows, and highly nonlocal response can
require generalized transport operators before the same thresholds are interpreted
without ambiguity \cite{harrington2001,collin2001,jackson1999,balanis2016}.

\paragraph{Worked problem: transport triad on plane wave versus balanced vortex pair.}
Consider a uniform plane wave \(\E=E_0 e^{\jj k_0 z}\hat{\mathbf{x}}\),
\(\H=H_0 e^{\jj k_0 z}\hat{\mathbf{y}}\) on sphere \(S_r\) in the far zone.
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.directional-power-density} gives isotropic \(\Pi>0\), while
axisymmetric stress yields \(\int_{4\pi}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\,d\Omega=\mathbf{0}\);
condition \(\mathcal{C}_1\) fails and \(\Lambda_J=0\). By contrast, superpose two
equal counter-rotating helical modes as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-counter-rotating-superposition}:
strong azimuthal phase structure appears, yet
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-zero-transport-result} forces \(\dot J_z=0\), so
\(\mathcal{C}_1\) again fails despite large local \(\|\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\|\) on
some channels. Only when channel imbalance \(I_+-I_-\neq 0\) is present do all three
branches \(\mathcal{C}_1,\mathcal{C}_2,\mathcal{C}_3\) pass simultaneously and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.transport-qualified-decision} returns \(\mathcal{D}_J=1\)
\cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}. The triad therefore rejects both ``boring but
radiative'' and ``visually rich but balanced'' fields before any modal label is
assigned.


\subsection{Reactive and radiative regimes}
\label{sec:ch01.2.4}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Reactive and radiative regimes are separated here because angular-transport claims
change meaning across those regimes. In reactive domains, structured fields can
display strong local circulation while exporting little net action. In radiative
domains, the same directional channels can become torque-capable carriers. The
classification therefore controls whether directional/angular diagnostics are read as
storage geometry or as exported transport evidence
\cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001}.

For harmonic fields in a linear passive exterior region, total radiated power on a
sphere of radius \(r\) is the integral already defined in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.directional-power-integral}, with integrand
\(\Pi(\theta,\phi;r)\) from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.directional-power-density}. The
reactive spherical flux is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.reactive-flux-sphere}
Q_{\mathrm{re}}(r)=
r^2\int_{4\pi}\Im\!\left\{\mathbf{S}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}}\right\}\,d\Omega.
\end{equation}
\(P_{\mathrm{rad}}\) is irreversible exported power; \(Q_{\mathrm{re}}\) is
oscillatory exchange channel associated with near-field storage cycling.

Directional decomposition is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.local-regime-decomposition}
\Pi_{\mathrm{a}}(\theta,\phi;r)=r^2\Re\!\{\mathbf{S}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}}\},\qquad
\Pi_{\mathrm{re}}(\theta,\phi;r)=r^2\Im\!\{\mathbf{S}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}}\},
\end{equation}
with \(P_{\mathrm{rad}}=\int_{4\pi}\Pi_{\mathrm{a}}\,d\Omega\) and
\(Q_{\mathrm{re}}=\int_{4\pi}\Pi_{\mathrm{re}}\,d\Omega\). Strong
\(\Pi_{\mathrm{re}}\) can coexist with moderate \(\Pi_{\mathrm{a}}\), which is why
topology alone cannot certify export.

Asymptotic radiative qualification is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.radiative-regime-condition}
\lim_{r\to\infty}P_{\mathrm{rad}}(r)=P_\infty>0,\qquad
\lim_{r\to\infty}\frac{|Q_{\mathrm{re}}(r)|}{P_{\mathrm{rad}}(r)}=0.
\end{equation}
The first limit enforces nonzero export at infinity; the second removes reactive
dominance in that limit.

For finite-radius diagnosis, use
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.regime-index}
\chi(r)=\frac{|Q_{\mathrm{re}}(r)|}{P_{\mathrm{rad}}(r)+\epsilon_P},
\end{equation}
with \(\epsilon_P>0\) regularizer. \(\chi\gg1\) indicates reactive-dominant shell;
\(\chi\ll1\) indicates export-dominant shell.

Directional sector index is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.directional-regime-index}
\chi_\Omega(r;\Delta\Omega)=
\frac{\int_{\Delta\Omega}|\Pi_{\mathrm{re}}(\theta,\phi;r)|\,d\Omega}
{\int_{\Delta\Omega}\Pi_{\mathrm{a}}(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega+\epsilon_\Omega},
\qquad \epsilon_\Omega>0,
\end{equation}
where \(\Delta\Omega\subseteq4\pi\). Global shells may appear radiative while
specific sectors remain reactive-dominant; \(\chi_\Omega\) resolves this mismatch.

Crossover radius estimator is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.crossover-radius}
r_c=\inf\left\{r>0:\chi(r)\le \chi_{\mathrm{th}}\right\},
\end{equation}
with declared threshold \(\chi_{\mathrm{th}}\ll1\). For \(r<r_c\), regime mixing
contaminates export interpretation; for \(r\ge r_c\), propagative interpretation is
first-order credible.

Carryover from Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2.3} is enforced by combining regime metrics
with the transport-qualified gate \(\mathcal{Q}_J\). Define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.regime-qualified-decision}
\mathcal{D}_{J,\mathrm{reg}}(r;\Delta\Omega)=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_J(r)=1,\
\chi(r)\le \chi_{\mathrm{th}},\
\chi_\Omega(r;\Delta\Omega)\le \chi_{\Omega,\mathrm{th}}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{D}_J\) is Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.transport-qualified-decision}. This
equation prevents promotion of reactive-dominant channels to transport status.

\begin{proposition}[Regime-qualified transport interpretation]
\label{prop:ch1.regime-qualified-transport}
Consider an admissible state satisfying triad conditions of
Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.2.3}. If \(\mathcal{D}_{J,\mathrm{reg}}=1\), then
directional angular-flux channels on \(\Delta\Omega\) admit export interpretation.
If \(\mathcal{D}_{J,\mathrm{reg}}=0\), those channels are interpreted primarily as
reactive storage geometry.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Triad conditions enforce conservation and export structure; regime thresholds remove
reactive dominance from the same channels. Without the regime gate, locally strong
patterns can still be non-exporting due to oscillatory exchange dominance.

A concrete dipole illustration clarifies the indices. For an oscillating electric
dipole \(\mathbf{p}=p_0 e^{\jj\omega t}\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) in vacuum, the
near-zone Poynting field on a sphere of radius \(r\ll \lambda=2\pi c/\omega\) is
dominated by the reactive term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-regime-decomposition},

permitting \(\mathcal{D}_{J,\mathrm{reg}}=1\) once
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.transport-qualified-decision} is also satisfied
\cite{jackson1999,balanis2016}. The crossover radius \(r_c\) in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.crossover-radius} therefore marks the stand-off at which
angular-flux channels become export-diagnostic rather than storage-diagnostic; any
torque experiment performed inside \(r_c\) must report regime indices alongside
mechanical readouts.

Regime indices \(\chi,\chi_\Omega\) and the asymptotic limit in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.radiative-regime-condition} assume harmonic steady-state and
linear constitutive closure. Broadband pulses, strong nonlinearity, and pronounced
nonlocal dispersion can require generalized partition operators beyond this chapter
\cite{harrington2001,collin2001,jackson1999,balanis2016}. Within stated limits, the
reactive-radiative separation prevents regime-mixing errors in subsequent angular
arguments.

\section{Rotations, Tensors, and Covariance}
\label{sec:ch01.3}

Section~\ref{sec:ch01.3} formulates angular-field statements under explicit
rotation and translation actions, tensor transformation rules, and invariant
observable criteria. The section-level objective is to separate physical transfer
content from coordinate-dependent representation, so that subsequent gauge
analysis inherits only frame-consistent quantities and not chart artifacts.


\subsection{Active and passive rotations}
\label{sec:ch01.3.1}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

The phrase ``active and passive rotations'' names two distinct operations that must
be disentangled before covariance claims can be trusted. Active rotation moves the
physical field pattern in space. Passive rotation keeps the pattern fixed and moves
only the coordinate frame used for its representation. Confusing these operations
creates false differences between physically equivalent states
\cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001}.

Let \(R\in\mathrm{SO}(3)\), with \(R^\top R=I\), \(\det R=1\), and \(I\) identity.
For phasor field \(\E(\mathbf{r})\), active action is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.active-rotation-action}
\big(\mathcal{A}_R\E\big)(\mathbf{r})=R\,\E\!\left(R^{-1}\mathbf{r}\right).
\end{equation}
The argument \(R^{-1}\mathbf{r}\) performs geometric pullback to pre-rotation
location, and left multiplication by \(R\) rotates vector components into the
rotated physical orientation.

Passive action is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.passive-rotation-action}
\big(\mathcal{P}_R\E\big)(\mathbf{r})=R^{-1}\,\E\!\left(R\mathbf{r}\right),
\end{equation}
which re-expresses the same field in the rotated chart. Active and passive channels
are linked by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.active-passive-duality}
\mathcal{A}_R=\mathcal{P}_{R^{-1}}.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.active-passive-duality} is the algebraic mechanism ensuring
that physical and representational viewpoints remain synchronized by inverse group
elements.

Group closure for repeated rotations is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.rotation-group-action}
\mathcal{A}_{R_1}\mathcal{A}_{R_2}=\mathcal{A}_{R_1R_2},\qquad
\mathcal{P}_{R_1}\mathcal{P}_{R_2}=\mathcal{P}_{R_1R_2},
\end{equation}
for \(R_1,R_2\in\mathrm{SO}(3)\). This equation guarantees path consistency: two
successive rotations produce the same state as one composite rotation.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1.active-passive-rotation} makes the practical rule explicit:
geometry changes under active action, components change under passive action, and
observable predictions must coincide when inverse pairing is correctly applied.

Transport invariance is the physical check. Let \(\mathbf{S}\) be complex Poynting
vector, \(S_r\) sphere of radius \(r\), \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\) outward unit normal,
and \(dS\) area element. Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.rotation-power-invariance}
\int_{S_r}\Re\!\left\{\mathbf{S}_R\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\right\}\,dS
=
\int_{S_r}\Re\!\left\{\mathbf{S}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\right\}\,dS.
\end{equation}
Rigid rotation can redistribute channel direction, but not total exported active
power.

The same invariance applies to net angular-flux magnitude:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.rotation-am-invariance}
\left\|\int_{4\pi}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_{J,R}(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega\right\|
=
\left\|\int_{4\pi}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_{J}(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega\right\|,
\end{equation}
where \(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_{J}\) is directional angular-flux map from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.directional-am-flux}. Transport-qualified rotational content is
therefore frame-robust under rigid rotations.

\paragraph{Worked problem: active versus passive rotation of a dipole pattern.}
Let \(\E(\mathbf{r})=E_0 e^{\jj k_0 z}\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) in laboratory frame
\(\{x,y,z\}\). Active rotation \(R_z(\pi/2)\) maps the physical dipole to
\(\mathcal{A}_{R_z}\E=E_0 e^{\jj k_0 z}\hat{\mathbf{y}}\), reorienting radiation
in space. Passive rotation of the coordinate frame by the same \(R_z(\pi/2)\) yields
\(\mathcal{P}_{R_z}\E=E_0 e^{\jj k_0 z'}(-\hat{\mathbf{x}}')\) when components are
expressed in the rotated basis, related by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.active-passive-duality}. Total power through sphere \(S_r\) from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.rotation-power-invariance} is identical in both reports because
only the geometric embedding changed, not the source excitation. A common error is to
apply \(\mathcal{A}_R\) to sources while leaving boundary data unrotated; the
mismatch index \(\kappa_{\mathrm{rot}}\) of Section~\ref{sec:ch01.0.2} then becomes
nonzero even though the underlying physics is unchanged
\cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}.

Infinitesimal analysis clarifies local generator structure. For small
\(\delta\varphi\) about unit axis \(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\), \(\|\hat{\mathbf{u}}\|=1\),
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.infinitesimal-active-rotation}
\big(\mathcal{A}_{\delta\varphi}\E\big)(\mathbf{r})
=
\E(\mathbf{r})
+\delta\varphi\!\left[\hat{\mathbf{u}}\times\E(\mathbf{r})
-\big(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\times\mathbf{r}\big)\cdot\nabla\E(\mathbf{r})\right]
+O(\delta\varphi^2),
\end{equation}
with \(\nabla\E\) componentwise derivative. The first correction is local vector
reorientation; the second is orbital sampling shift along rotation trajectories.

Defining \(\mathcal{A}_{\delta\varphi}=I+\delta\varphi\,\mathcal{J}_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}
+O(\delta\varphi^2)\) gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.rotation-generator-split}
\mathcal{J}_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}\E
=
\hat{\mathbf{u}}\times\E
-\big(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\times\mathbf{r}\big)\cdot\nabla\E.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.rotation-generator-split} isolates the two generator channels
needed later for gauge-sensitive decomposition discussions.

Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2} imposes transport qualification through
\(\mathcal{D}_{J,\mathrm{reg}}\); this subsection preserves that constraint. In
practice, active/passive equivalence is used for physical interpretation only when
the compared states satisfy the same admissibility and regime gates.

\begin{proposition}[Observable equivalence under active-passive duality]
\label{prop:ch1.active-passive-observable-equivalence}
For rigid \(R\in\mathrm{SO}(3)\), active and passive descriptions linked by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.active-passive-duality} yield identical values for
frame-invariant scalar observables built from admissible transport integrals.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Duality maps field descriptions by inverse-group pairing. Invariant scalar
observables use invariant contractions and measure-preserving integration on rotated
manifolds. Therefore values are unchanged under representation channel choice.

Rigid-body rotation laws require Euclidean geometry,
orthonormal frames, and constitutive response for which transport observables
remain finite under the closure of Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2}. Non-rigid
deformations, manifold curvature requiring connection terms, and orientation-
dependent constitutive operators require extended covariance machinery.


\subsection{Transformation of vectors, tensors, and dyads}
\label{sec:ch01.3.2}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

The heading of this subsection names three algebraic layers that must be treated
explicitly: vectors, tensors, and dyads. Covariance failures at any one layer can
propagate into false conclusions about stress transfer, torque prediction, and modal
coupling. The objective here is therefore operational: assign each object its
rotation law, then prove that physically meaningful contractions remain objective
under rigid frame action \cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001}.

For proper rotation \(R\in\mathrm{SO}(3)\), with \(R^\top R=I\), \(\det R=1\), let
\(\mathbf{v}=v_j\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j\) be vector in orthonormal basis
\(\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j\). Component transformation is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.vector-rotation-components}
v_i' = R_{ij}v_j.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.vector-rotation-components} is the component-level
realization of active/passive vector rotation from Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.3.1}.

For second-rank tensor \(\mathbf{T}\) with components \(T_{pq}\),
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.tensor-rotation-rule}
\mathbf{T}' = R\,\mathbf{T}\,R^\top,
\qquad
T'_{ij}=R_{ip}R_{jq}T_{pq}.
\end{equation}
The two factors \(R_{ip}\), \(R_{jq}\) transform each tensor slot. This is the
objectivity law ensuring that stress tensor
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress} rotates geometrically rather than numerically.

Tensor invariants satisfy
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.tensor-invariant-trace}
\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{T}')=\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{T}),\qquad
\|\mathbf{T}'\|_F=\|\mathbf{T}\|_F,
\end{equation}
because \(\operatorname{tr}(R\mathbf{T}R^\top)=\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{T})\) and
orthogonality of \(R\) preserves Frobenius inner product.

If \(\mathbf{T}\mathbf{q}_k=\lambda_k\mathbf{q}_k\), then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.stress-eigen-covariance}
\mathbf{T}'(R\mathbf{q}_k)=\lambda_k(R\mathbf{q}_k),
\end{equation}
so eigenvalues are objective and principal directions rotate covariantly.

For dyadic map \(\mathbf{D}\) defined by \(\mathbf{w}=\mathbf{D}\cdot\mathbf{u}\):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.dyadic-covariance}
\mathbf{D}' = R\,\mathbf{D}\,R^\top,
\qquad
\mathbf{w}'=\mathbf{D}'\cdot\mathbf{u}'=R\mathbf{w},
\end{equation}
with \(\mathbf{u}'=R\mathbf{u}\). This equation establishes slotwise equivariance:
rotating input and operator yields rotated output.

Rank-one dyad \(\mathbf{D}=\mathbf{a}\mathbf{b}^\top\) transforms as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.rank1-dyad-rotation}
\mathbf{D}'=R\mathbf{a}\,(R\mathbf{b})^\top.
\end{equation}
Both generating directions rotate, preserving dyadic rank and directional coupling
interpretation used in mode-projection constructions.

Composition compatibility follows:
\[
\mathbf{D}'' = R_2(R_1\mathbf{D}R_1^\top)R_2^\top
=(R_2R_1)\mathbf{D}(R_2R_1)^\top.
\]
Hence staged rotations and single composite rotation are algebraically identical.

With \(\mathbf{t}=\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\) and
\(\hat{\mathbf{n}}'=R\hat{\mathbf{n}}\):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.traction-rotation-covariance}
\mathbf{t}'=\mathbf{T}'\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}' = R\mathbf{t}.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.traction-rotation-covariance} is the direct bridge from
covariance algebra to measurable mechanical response.

For operator \(\mathcal{F}\), define covariance residual
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.covariance-residual}
\mathcal{R}_{\mathcal{F}}(R;X)=
\left\|\mathcal{F}(R\cdot X)-R\cdot\mathcal{F}(X)\right\|,
\end{equation}
with admissible input \(X\). Exact covariance corresponds to
\(\mathcal{R}_{\mathcal{F}}=0\); finite values diagnose discretization-induced frame
artifacts.

Transport and regime indices fixed in Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2} constrain
covariance comparisons: frame maps are physically meaningful only when compared
states satisfy common transport closure and outward-power support.

\begin{proposition}[Covariant map criterion]
\label{prop:ch1.covariant-map-criterion}
If a tensor/dyadic map obeys \(X\mapsto RXR^\top\) and acts linearly on vector
slots transformed by \(v\mapsto Rv\), then derived vector outputs satisfy
\(y\mapsto Ry\), and scalar contractions built from invariant inner products are
frame-independent.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Linearity plus conjugation provides slotwise equivariance by substitution.
Orthogonality of \(R\) cancels paired \(R,R^\top\) in scalar contractions, leaving
objective values.

\paragraph{Worked problem: rank-one dyad under \(R_z(\pi/2)\).}
Let \(\mathbf{a}=a_0\hat{\mathbf{x}}\), \(\mathbf{b}=b_0\hat{\mathbf{y}}\), so
\(\mathbf{D}=\mathbf{a}\mathbf{b}^\top\) has only \(D_{xy}=a_0b_0\). Under
\(R=R_z(\pi/2)\), \(\mathbf{a}'=a_0\hat{\mathbf{y}}\) and
\(\mathbf{b}'=-b_0\hat{\mathbf{x}}\), giving \(D'_{yx}=-a_0b_0\) in the rotated frame.
For input \(\mathbf{u}=\hat{\mathbf{x}}\), \(\mathbf{w}=\mathbf{D}\mathbf{u}=a_0b_0\hat{\mathbf{y}}\)
and \(\mathbf{w}'=\mathbf{D}'\mathbf{u}'=a_0b_0\hat{\mathbf{y}}'\), confirming
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.dyadic-covariance}. Traction on normal \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}=\hat{\mathbf{z}}\)
uses \(\mathbf{T}\) rather than \(\mathbf{D}\), but the same slotwise rule applies when
stress dyads are decomposed into rank-one modal couplings in Section~\ref{sec:ch01.5.3}
\cite{harrington2001,jackson1999}.

Scope is restricted to proper rigid Euclidean rotations and orthonormal frames.
Improper transformations, curved manifolds with connection terms, and constitutive
operators tied to anisotropic geometric structure require extended machinery.


\subsection{Origin dependence and translational covariance}
\label{sec:ch01.3.3}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Origin dependence enters angular reporting through the moment arm, not through a
change of physical field state. If the same state is reported from two translated
origins, the reported angular quantity changes by an affine correction term. The
central objective is therefore to separate translation-induced bookkeeping from
transport-bearing content \cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001}.

Let \(O\) and \(O'=O+\mathbf{a}\) be two origins, with
\(\mathbf{a}\in\mathbb{R}^3\). Position vectors are related by
\(\mathbf{r}'=\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{a}\). Using linear-momentum density
\(\mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{em}}\) (in this chapter,
\(\mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{em}}=\mathbf{P}/c^2\)), define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.origin-angular-definition}
\mathbf{J}_O=\int_V \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{em}}\,dV,\qquad
\mathbf{J}_{O'}=\int_V \mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{em}}\,dV.
\end{equation}
Replacing \(\mathbf{r}'\) gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.origin-shift-law}
\mathbf{J}_{O'}=\mathbf{J}_O-\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{tot}},\qquad
\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{tot}}=\int_V \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{em}}\,dV.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.origin-shift-law} is the fundamental affine shift rule:
translation contributes an additive extrinsic term controlled by total linear
momentum.

An equivalent local statement appears for angular-flux tensor:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.origin-shift-flux-law}
\mathbf{M}_{O'}=\mathbf{M}_{O}-\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{T},
\end{equation}
with \(\mathbf{T}\) Maxwell stress tensor from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress}. Hence origin dependence is present at both
integral and local-flux levels.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1.origin-shift-map} emphasizes a practical rule: reported angular
differences are physically meaningful only after removing origin-shift contributions.

A translation-covariant intrinsic observable is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.intrinsic-angular-observable}
\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}
=
\mathbf{J}_O-\mathbf{r}_c\times\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{tot}},
\end{equation}
with declared reference point \(\mathbf{r}_c\). If \((O,\mathbf{r}_c)\) is
translated jointly, \(\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}\) is unchanged.

For finite-resolution data, translation residual is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.translation-consistency-residual}
\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{tr}}=
\left\|
\mathbf{J}_{O'}-\mathbf{J}_{O}+\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{tot}}
\right\|.
\end{equation}
Ideal affine closure gives \(\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{tr}}=0\); nonzero values diagnose
truncation, sampling mismatch, or incomplete momentum closure.

Origin comparison applies only when both reports use the same transport closure
from Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2}; otherwise bookkeeping shifts are indistinguishable
from genuine transport differences. Comparisons are accepted only when those
closure conditions remain valid and affine residual is controlled:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.origin-qualified-decision}
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{org}}=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_{J,\mathrm{reg}}=1,\
\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{tr}}\le R_{\mathrm{tr,max}}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(R_{\mathrm{tr,max}}\ge0\) is declared reporting tolerance.

\begin{proposition}[Origin-comparable angular reporting criterion]
\label{prop:ch1.origin-comparable-criterion}
Two datasets referenced to origins \(O_1,O_2\) are directly comparable in angular
transport if and only if either a common origin protocol is used, or both are
mapped to a common intrinsic form \(\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}\), and
\(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{org}}=1\).
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.origin-shift-law} contributes additive
\(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{tot}}\). Without common origin or intrinsic
conversion, this additive term contaminates physical differences. Residual control
in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.origin-qualified-decision} enforces reliable affine closure.

Origin-shift bookkeeping requires finite momentum integrals,
regular control boundaries, and rigid Euclidean translations. Open systems with
unresolved far-field flux, strongly nonlocal constitutive response, or actively
time-varying boundary injection require extended closure operators before
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:ch1.origin-shift-law}--\eqref{eq:ch1.origin-qualified-decision}
can be used without qualification.

\paragraph{Worked problem: origin shift on a finite dipole.}
Let a short dipole \(\mathbf{p}=p_0\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) produce total linear momentum
\(\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{tot}}=P_0\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) in the harmonic radiative zone.
Report angular momentum about origin \(O\) at the dipole center and about
\(O'=O+\mathbf{a}\) with \(\mathbf{a}=a\hat{\mathbf{x}}\). Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.origin-shift-law}
gives \(\mathbf{J}_{O'}=\mathbf{J}_O-\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{tot}}
=\mathbf{J}_O-aP_0\hat{\mathbf{y}}\). The shift term is pure bookkeeping: it
contains no new field physics, only lever-arm change. Removing it via
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.intrinsic-angular-observable} with \(\mathbf{r}_c=\mathbf{0}\)
restores a common intrinsic report before comparing two sources. If measured
\(\|\mathbf{J}_{O'}-\mathbf{J}_O+\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{tot}}\|\)
exceeds tolerance \(R_{\mathrm{tr,max}}\) in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.origin-qualified-decision}, the discrepancy is attributed to
finite aperture truncation or inconsistent \(\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{tot}}\) estimation,
not to intrinsic angular structure \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}.


\subsection{Invariants and frame-independent observables}
\label{sec:ch01.3.4}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Field diagnostics qualify as observables only when their numerical report survives
allowed frame transformations with the correct geometric law. This subsection
constructs a complete observable filter at three levels: scalar invariance, vector
covariance, and tensor-invariant reduction. The resulting filter is then coupled to
the directional transport quantities of Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2}.

Let \(\mathcal{F}\) be the electromagnetic state and \(\mathcal{A}_R\mathcal{F}\)
its rotated image for \(R\in\mathrm{SO}(3)\). A scalar functional
\(\mathcal{I}[\mathcal{F}]\in\mathbb{R}\) is frame-invariant when
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.scalar-observable-invariance}
\mathcal{I}[\mathcal{A}_R\mathcal{F}] = \mathcal{I}[\mathcal{F}]
\quad \forall R\in\mathrm{SO}(3).
\end{equation}
A vector observable \(\mathbf{Q}[\mathcal{F}]\in\mathbb{R}^3\) is frame-objective
when
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.vector-observable-covariance}
\mathbf{Q}[\mathcal{A}_R\mathcal{F}] = R\,\mathbf{Q}[\mathcal{F}].
\end{equation}
These two relations separate true observables from coordinate artifacts: violation
of both indicates representation dependence.

For second-rank tensors transformed by \( \mathbf{T}'=R\mathbf{T}R^\top \), define
principal invariants
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.tensor-principal-invariants}
I_1(\mathbf{T})=\mathrm{tr}(\mathbf{T}),\qquad
I_2(\mathbf{T})=\frac{1}{2}\!\left[(\mathrm{tr}\,\mathbf{T})^2-\mathrm{tr}(\mathbf{T}^2)\right].
\end{equation}
These are invariant because trace is unchanged under similarity action.
The third invariant
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.tensor-third-invariant}
I_3(\mathbf{T})=\det(\mathbf{T})
\end{equation}
is also frame-invariant for proper rotations. Hence
\((I_1,I_2,I_3)\) provides a coordinate-independent scalar signature of tensorial
content in three-dimensional Euclidean space.

Observable checks in computation or measurement require quantitative residuals:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.observable-frame-residual}
\Delta_{\mathcal{I}}(R;\mathcal{F})=
\left|\mathcal{I}[\mathcal{A}_R\mathcal{F}]-\mathcal{I}[\mathcal{F}]\right|,
\qquad
\Delta_{\mathbf{Q}}(R;\mathcal{F})=
\left\|\mathbf{Q}[\mathcal{A}_R\mathcal{F}]-R\mathbf{Q}[\mathcal{F}]\right\|.
\end{equation}
Exact objectivity implies zero residual; nonzero residual quantifies numerical,
experimental, or modeling non-objectivity.

Tensor-level residual can be introduced analogously:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.tensor-objectivity-residual}
\Delta_{\mathbf{T}}(R;\mathcal{F})=
\left\|\mathbf{T}[\mathcal{A}_R\mathcal{F}]-R\,\mathbf{T}[\mathcal{F}]\,R^\top\right\|_F,
\end{equation}
with \(\|\cdot\|_F\) Frobenius norm. This residual is useful when stress or
coherency tensors are reconstructed from discretized fields.

Using directional angular-flux \(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\) and active power density
\(\Pi\) from Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2}, their objectivity-coupled sector index is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.objective-sector-index}
\Gamma_J(\Delta\Omega;r)=
\frac{\left\|\int_{\Delta\Omega}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega\right\|}
{\int_{\Delta\Omega}\Pi(\theta,\phi;r)\,d\Omega+\epsilon_\Pi},
\end{equation}
where \(\Delta\Omega\subseteq4\pi\) is an interaction sector and
\(\epsilon_\Pi>0\) regularizes weak-power windows. Numerator captures net rotational
export over the sector; denominator captures energetic support. The ratio remains
scalar-objective when \(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\) and \(\Pi\) satisfy the covariance
and invariance conditions above.

For inter-frame comparability across multiple sectors, define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.multisector-objective-vector}
\boldsymbol{\Gamma}(r)=
\big[\Gamma_J(\Delta\Omega_1;r),\ldots,\Gamma_J(\Delta\Omega_N;r)\big]^\top,
\end{equation}
with partition \(\{\Delta\Omega_n\}_{n=1}^N\) of \(4\pi\). This vector is not a
spatial vector; it is a sector-index report. Its objectivity requires fixed
physical sector definition under frame rotation and consistent pullback of sector
boundaries.

Define observability admissibility set
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.observability-admissibility-map}
\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{adm}}[\mathcal{F}]
=
\Big\{\mathcal{X}:\Delta_{\mathcal{X}}(R;\mathcal{F})\le\tau_{\mathcal{X}},\
\forall R\in\mathrm{SO}(3)\Big\},
\end{equation}
where \(\tau_{\mathcal{X}}\) is declared tolerance for report class
\(\mathcal{X}\). This definition incorporates exact theory (\(\tau_{\mathcal{X}}=0\))
and practical finite-resolution settings (\(\tau_{\mathcal{X}}>0\)).

\begin{proposition}[Frame-objective transport index criterion]
\label{prop:ch1.frame-objective-index}
If \(\Pi\) is scalar-invariant and \(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\) is vector-covariant
under \(R\in\mathrm{SO}(3)\), then
\(\Gamma_J(\Delta\Omega;r)\) in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.objective-sector-index} is
frame-objective for fixed physical sector definition.
\end{proposition}

Proof sketch: sector-integrated \(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_J\) transforms covariantly, so
its norm is invariant; sector-integrated \(\Pi\) is invariant by scalar property.
Their ratio is therefore invariant.

\begin{proposition}[Invariant-tensor observable equivalence]
\label{prop:ch1.tensor-invariant-equivalence}
For two tensor reports \(\mathbf{T}_a,\mathbf{T}_b\) related by proper rotation
\(\mathbf{T}_b=R\mathbf{T}_aR^\top\), the invariant triplets are identical:
\((I_1,I_2,I_3)_a=(I_1,I_2,I_3)_b\). Conversely, if two symmetric tensors share the
same triplet and have non-degenerate eigenspaces, they are related by orthogonal
similarity.
\end{proposition}

The first statement is direct from invariance properties. The converse uses
spectral decomposition: shared characteristic polynomial implies shared eigenvalue
multiset; non-degenerate eigenspaces allow one orthogonal basis to rotate into the
other.

\paragraph{Worked problem: stress-tensor rotation check.}
Take vacuum stress tensor for \(\E=E_0\hat{\mathbf{x}}\), \(\H=H_0\hat{\mathbf{y}}\)
with \(E_0,H_0\in\mathbb{R}\). Under rotation \(R_z(\pi/2)\) about \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\),
\(\E'=(E_0,-E_0,0)^\top\) and \(\H'=(H_0,H_0,0)^\top\) in the rotated frame.
Direct construction from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress} gives
\(\mathbf{T}'=R\mathbf{T}R^\top\) with residual
\(\Delta_{\mathbf{T}}(R;\mathcal{F})=0\) in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.tensor-objectivity-residual}. The scalar invariants satisfy
\(I_1(\mathbf{T}')=I_1(\mathbf{T})\) and \(I_3(\mathbf{T}')=I_3(\mathbf{T})\) as
required by Eqs.~\eqref{eq:ch1.tensor-principal-invariants}--\eqref{eq:ch1.tensor-third-invariant}.
Traction on surface normal \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}=\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) rotates as
\(\mathbf{t}'=R(\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{x}})\), consistent with
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.traction-rotation-covariance}. A numerical implementation that
reports \(\Delta_{\mathbf{T}}>10^{-12}\) for this analytic case signals discretization
or passive/active rotation mix-up rather than physical non-objectivity
\cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}.

Objectivity tests presuppose proper rotations in Euclidean
space, gauge-robust field constructions, and sufficient regularity for the
integrals and residual norms used above. Non-Euclidean manifolds, improper
transformations, and gauge-dependent local decompositions require modified
objectivity operators handled in Section~\ref{sec:ch01.4}.

\paragraph{Section 1.3 closure and forward link.}
Section~\ref{sec:ch01.3} fixes active/passive rotation duality, tensor and dyadic
covariance, origin translation, and frame-invariant observables. Quantities carried
forward must survive rigid-frame action and declared origin protocol before entering
transport interpretation. Section~\ref{sec:ch01.4} treats gauge freedom within the
same screening discipline.


\section{Gauge Structure and Physical Observables}
\label{sec:ch01.4}

Section~\ref{sec:ch01.4} develops gauge structure as an equivalence-class framework
for electromagnetic representation: potentials are introduced as computational
coordinates on field space, gauge transformations as symmetry operations within that
representation class, and helicity-linked constructions as meaningful only after
explicit observability filtering. The section objective is therefore twofold: first,
to show which potential-level decompositions are gauge-dependent bookkeeping; second,
to isolate the combinations that remain invariant and can be connected to physical
transport channels established earlier. Every statement in this section is evaluated
against that invariant-content requirement before it is used in subsequent chapters.


\subsection{Potentials and gauge transformations}
\label{sec:ch01.4.1}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Potential theory in Maxwell electrodynamics is a representation framework, not an
extra layer of physics. Observable fields are invariant under a continuous family
of potential reparameterizations, so potential-level statements become meaningful
only after explicit gauge-invariance checks
\cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001}.

Let \(\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r})\) be vector potential, \(\phi(\mathbf{r})\) scalar
potential, \(\E(\mathbf{r})\) electric phasor, and \(\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r})\)
magnetic flux density, with \(\omega>0\) and time factor \(\exp(-j\omega t)\).
Potential-to-field map is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.potential-field-relations}
\mathbf{B}=\nabla\times\mathbf{A},\qquad
\E=-\nabla\phi+j\omega\mathbf{A}.
\end{equation}
The first identity enforces magnetic solenoidality in representation form; the
second decomposes electric field into irrotational and inductive channels.

Gauge freedom is generated by smooth scalar function \(\chi(\mathbf{r})\):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.gauge-transform}
\mathbf{A}'=\mathbf{A}+\nabla\chi,\qquad
\phi'=\phi-j\omega\chi.
\end{equation}
Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-transform} into
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.potential-field-relations} gives explicit invariance:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.gauge-field-invariance-check}
\nabla\times\mathbf{A}'=\nabla\times\mathbf{A},\qquad
-\nabla\phi'+j\omega\mathbf{A}'=-\nabla\phi+j\omega\mathbf{A}.
\end{equation}
Thus gauge transformation moves representation coordinates on one physical state
rather than generating new measurable fields.

Gauge-admissible scalar reporting functional \(\mathcal{I}\) must satisfy
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.gauge-observable-invariance}
\mathcal{I}[\mathbf{A}',\phi']=\mathcal{I}[\mathbf{A},\phi]
\quad\text{for all }\chi\text{ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-transform}}.
\end{equation}
Quantitative residual version is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.gauge-observable-residual}
\Delta_{\mathrm{g}}(\chi;\mathcal{I})=
\left|\mathcal{I}[\mathbf{A}+\nabla\chi,\phi-j\omega\chi]-\mathcal{I}[\mathbf{A},\phi]\right|.
\end{equation}
Exact gauge objectivity requires \(\Delta_{\mathrm{g}}=0\); finite values quantify
discretization or modeling contamination of gauge invariance.

Potential equations close after gauge condition choice. In Lorenz gauge:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.lorenz-gauge-phasor}
\nabla\cdot\mathbf{A}-j\omega\mu\varepsilon\,\phi=0,
\end{equation}
with \(\mu\) permeability and \(\varepsilon\) permittivity. In homogeneous regions
this yields Helmholtz-type equations
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.potential-wave-equations}
\nabla^2\mathbf{A}+k^2\mathbf{A}=-\mu\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{imp}},\qquad
\nabla^2\phi+k^2\phi=-\frac{\rho}{\varepsilon},
\end{equation}
where \(k^2=\omega^2\mu\varepsilon\). This form is computationally convenient and
keeps gauge-fixed propagation and source coupling explicit.

\paragraph{Worked problem: Lorenz-gauge potential for a point dipole.}
For \(\mathbf{p}=p_0\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) in vacuum, the Lorenz-gauge potential in the
far zone is \(\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r})\approx -\jj\omega\mu_0 p_0 e^{\jj k_0 r}/(4\pi r)\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\)
and \(\phi(\mathbf{r})\approx p_0\cos\theta/(4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2)\) to leading
order in \(kr\gg 1\). Substituting into Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.potential-field-relations}
recovers \(\E,\mathbf{B}\) consistent with the dipole radiation fields. A gauge update
\(\chi=\alpha r\) shifts \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\phi\) but leaves \(\E,\mathbf{B}\)
unchanged by Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-field-invariance-check}. Residual
\(\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{L}}\) from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-condition-residual} should
vanish for the Lorenz-gauge solution; a numerical solver that reports
\(\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{L}}>0\) is using an inconsistent potential pair even if
\(\E,\mathbf{B}\) appear correct \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}.

Gauge-choice compatibility can be monitored by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.gauge-condition-residual}
\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{L}}=
\left\|\nabla\cdot\mathbf{A}-j\omega\mu\varepsilon\,\phi\right\|_{L^2(\Omega)}.
\end{equation}
\(\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{L}}=0\) indicates exact Lorenz-gauge compliance; nonzero
values identify closure drift in numerical or measured reconstructions.

Transport and regime closure from Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2} constrain
gauge-level reports: potential statements are accepted only when transport closure
and gauge invariance hold jointly:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.gauge-qualified-decision}
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{g}}=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_{J,\mathrm{reg}}=1,\
\Delta_{\mathrm{g}}\le \delta_{\mathrm{g,max}},\
\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{L}}\le R_{\mathrm{L,max}}
\Big),
\end{equation}
with declared tolerances \(\delta_{\mathrm{g,max}},R_{\mathrm{L,max}}\ge0\).

\begin{proposition}[Gauge-orbit observable criterion]
\label{prop:ch1.gauge-orbit-observable-criterion}
A potential-derived report is physically admissible in this chapter only if it is
invariant on the full gauge orbit and satisfies \(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{g}}=1\).
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Gauge transforms leave \((\E,\mathbf{B})\) invariant by
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:ch1.potential-field-relations}--\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-transform}
\cite{jackson1999}. Reports must satisfy
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-observable-invariance} and the residual bounds in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-qualified-decision}, with transport closure inherited from
Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2}.
\end{proof}

Gauge-orbit analysis presumes smooth fields on simply connected regions,
linear constitutive closure, and single-frequency excitation.
Topological gauge patching, strongly nonlocal kernels, and nonlinear conversion
require generalized gauge bundles and nonlinear observability filters beyond
Chapter~\ref{ch:01}.

With Eqs.~\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-transform}--\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-qualified-decision}
fixed, the next subsection can classify which local densities are genuinely
observable and which remain gauge-dependent bookkeeping.


\subsection{Gauge dependence of local densities}
\label{sec:ch01.4.2}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Gauge freedom preserves measurable fields but can alter local densities written
explicitly in terms of potentials. The technical objective of this subsection is to
separate three classes: strictly gauge-invariant local reports, gauge-variant local
decompositions with boundary-canceling integrals, and gauge-contaminated reports
that cannot be promoted to observables \cite{harrington2001,jackson1999,collin2001}.

Let \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\phi\) denote a potential pair and
\((\mathbf{A}',\phi')\) the transformed pair from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-transform}. Let \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) be the
physical fields reconstructed through Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.potential-field-relations},
and let \(\mathbf{d}_{\mathrm{loc}}(\mathbf{r})\) be a candidate local density
vector at position \(\mathbf{r}\). The dependence structure can be written as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.local-density-functional}
\mathbf{d}_{\mathrm{loc}}(\mathbf{r})
=
\mathcal{D}\!\left[\mathbf{A},\phi,\mathbf{E},\mathbf{B}\right](\mathbf{r}),
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{D}\) is a density-construction functional. If
\(\mathcal{D}\) uses potentials explicitly, then the gauge update in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-transform} generally induces
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.local-density-gauge-shift}
\delta_\chi \mathbf{d}_{\mathrm{loc}}
\equiv
\mathbf{d}_{\mathrm{loc}}' - \mathbf{d}_{\mathrm{loc}}
=
\nabla\cdot\mathbf{\Xi}_\chi + \mathbf{\Psi}_\chi,
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{\Xi}_\chi\) is a gauge-generated flux tensor and
\(\mathbf{\Psi}_\chi\) is a residual gauge source term. Equation
\eqref{eq:ch1.local-density-gauge-shift} is the core diagnostic of this
subsection: local density objectivity fails whenever either term is nonzero.

The decomposition has direct physical meaning. The divergence branch can contribute
only through boundary flux after volume integration; the residual branch modifies
interior report values even with closed boundaries. Thus the second term is the
stronger indicator of non-observability.

The roles of the two terms are distinct. The divergence contribution
\(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{\Xi}_\chi\) can integrate to boundary transport only; the
residual term \(\mathbf{\Psi}_\chi\) changes local value even in closed domains.
Thus, a density may be locally gauge-dependent but still yield gauge-robust
integrated totals under admissible boundary conditions. Let \(V\) be a finite
volume with boundary \(\partial V\) and outward unit normal
\(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\). Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.integrated-density-gauge-test}
\int_V \delta_\chi \mathbf{d}_{\mathrm{loc}}\,dV
=
\int_{\partial V}\mathbf{\Xi}_\chi\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS
\;+\;
\int_V \mathbf{\Psi}_\chi\,dV.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.integrated-density-gauge-test} follows by divergence
theorem and separates boundary leakage from volumetric gauge contamination.
Mathematically, the left side is gauge variation of integrated density; physically,
it tests whether a reported quantity is robust or representation-contaminated.

To connect with the gauge gate \(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{g}}\) from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-qualified-decision}, define normalized local-gauge index
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.local-gauge-index}
\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{loc}}=
\frac{\left\|\int_V \delta_\chi\mathbf{d}_{\mathrm{loc}}\,dV\right\|}
{\int_V\|\mathbf{d}_{\mathrm{loc}}\|\,dV+\epsilon_d},
\qquad \epsilon_d>0.
\end{equation}
Small \(\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{loc}}\) indicates gauge-stable integrated reporting for
the selected volume protocol.

The operational criterion that aligns this subsection with
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-observable-invariance} is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.local-observable-admissibility}
\delta_\chi \mathbf{d}_{\mathrm{loc}}=\mathbf{0}
\quad\text{or}\quad
\int_V \delta_\chi \mathbf{d}_{\mathrm{loc}}\,dV=\mathbf{0}
\ \text{under stated boundary conditions}.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-observable-admissibility} does not force every
useful local decomposition to be gauge-invariant pointwise; it states the exact
conditions under which such decompositions may still be interpreted without
violating observable objectivity \cite{jackson1999,collin2001}.

Operational acceptance in this chapter is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.local-density-qualified-decision}
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{loc}}=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{g}}=1,\
\mathcal{D}_{J,\mathrm{reg}}=1,\
\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{loc}}\le G_{\mathrm{loc,max}},\
\text{Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-observable-admissibility} holds}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(G_{\mathrm{loc,max}}\ge 0\) is declared tolerance. This criterion prevents
gauge-unstable local densities from entering transport interpretation.

\begin{proposition}[Local-density observability criterion]
\label{prop:ch1.local-density-observable-criterion}
A local density report derived from potentials is admissible as an observable in
this chapter only if \(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{loc}}=1\).
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-density-gauge-shift} gives the full gauge variation.
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-observable-admissibility} enforces local or integrated
invariance. Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-density-qualified-decision} adds the gauge
and transport/regime gates, ensuring that accepted reports are both representation-
robust and physically qualified.

Within Chapter~\ref{ch:01}, this result is used as a filter on local
spin-orbit-style density narratives: forms that fail
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-observable-admissibility} remain representation tools,
whereas forms that pass can be linked to measurable channels from
Section~\ref{sec:ch01.5}. This distinction prevents gauge artefacts from being
misreported as physical transport structure \cite{harrington2001,balanis2016}.

The derivation assumes smooth fields, linear media, and boundary regularity
sufficient for divergence theorem use. Singular gauge choices, topological branch
cuts, and nonlocal constitutive operators require generalized distributional
treatment outside the present subsection.

\paragraph{Worked problem: Coulomb versus Lorenz gauge on a local density.}
For \(\mathbf{A}=\mathbf{A}_C\), \(\phi=\phi_C\) in Coulomb gauge
(\(\divg\mathbf{A}_C=0\)) and \((\mathbf{A}_L,\phi_L)\) in Lorenz gauge, both
reproduce the same \(\E,\mathbf{B}\). A candidate local density
\(\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}=\mathbf{r}\times(\varepsilon_0\E+\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B}/\mu_0)\)
generally satisfies Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-density-gauge-shift} with
\(\delta_\chi\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}\neq\mathbf{0}\) under
\(\mathbf{A}'=\mathbf{A}+\nabla\chi\). Integrating over volume \(V_g\) and applying
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.integrated-density-gauge-test}, the boundary term
\(\int_{\partial V_g}\mathbf{\Xi}_\chi\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS\) may cancel while
the residual \(\int_{V_g}\mathbf{\Psi}_\chi\,dV\) remains finite, so
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-observable-admissibility} fails. By contrast, total
\(\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}=\int_{V_g}\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}\,dV\) built from
gauge-invariant \(\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}\) of Section~\ref{sec:ch01.1.5} survives
the gauge update. The lesson for measurement design is direct: report surface or
volume integrals of invariant densities; treat potential-weighted local maps as
diagnostics unless Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-density-qualified-decision} is verified
\cite{jackson1999,harrington2001,collin2001}.

Having separated gauge-dependent local densities from admissible observable
content, the next subsection formalizes gauge-invariant observables alongside
helicity and dual-symmetry structure.


\subsection{Gauge-invariant observables, helicity, and dual symmetry}
\label{sec:ch01.4.3}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

The previous subsection showed that potential-dependent local densities can fail
observable status unless gauge variation is explicitly closed. The present
development advances from that screening step to a constructive objective: identify
field reports that remain invariant under gauge relabeling, remain objective under
rigid-frame transformations, and admit a transport law linked to helicity and dual
symmetry \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001,collin2001}.

Let the phasor field state be \(\mathcal{F}=(\E,\H)\) in a linear isotropic region
with scalar permittivity \(\varepsilon\), scalar permeability \(\mu\), angular
frequency \(\omega\), and intrinsic impedance
\(\eta=\sqrt{\mu/\varepsilon}\). Let \((\mathbf{A},\phi)\) denote a potential pair
that reproduces \(\E\) and \(\mathbf{B}=\mu\H\) through
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.potential-field-relations}. A scalar report \(\mathcal{O}_s\),
vector report \(\mathbf{O}_v\), and pseudoscalar report \(\mathcal{O}_p\) are
accepted as gauge-invariant observables only if all three components of the
admissibility contract are satisfied:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.observable-admissibility-triad}
\mathcal{O}_\alpha[\mathbf{A}',\phi']=\mathcal{O}_\alpha[\mathbf{A},\phi],\qquad
\mathcal{O}_\alpha[\mathcal{A}_R\mathcal{F}]=\mathcal{R}_\alpha(R)\,\mathcal{O}_\alpha[\mathcal{F}],
\qquad
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{loc}}=1,
\end{equation}
where \(\alpha\in\{s,v,p\}\), \((\mathbf{A}',\phi')\) is generated by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-transform}, \(\mathcal{A}_R\) is active spatial rotation
from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.active-rotation-action}, and \(\mathcal{R}_\alpha(R)\) is
representation map: identity for scalars, \(R\) for vectors, and parity-aware sign
rule for pseudoscalars. The final clause imports the local-density gate of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-density-qualified-decision}; therefore a report can be
algebraically elegant yet rejected if local gauge contamination survives.

The phrase \emph{gauge-invariant observable} in this subsection means more than
formal cancellation in a symbolic expression. It means that the reported quantity
is insensitive to potential-gauge choice and also carries a consistent rigid-frame
transformation law, so that two observers using rotated coordinates assign
equivalent physical content to the same field configuration
\cite{jackson1999,collin2001}. This dual requirement removes ambiguity that appears
when gauge robustness is checked without covariance, or covariance is checked
without gauge screening.

Within the admissible class, helicity is introduced as a field-intrinsic
pseudoscalar density:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.helicity-density}
h(\mathbf{r})=
\frac{1}{2\omega}\Im\!\left\{
\varepsilon\,\E^*\cdot(\curl\E)
\;+\;
\mu\,\H^*\cdot(\curl\H)
\right\}.
\end{equation}
Here \((\cdot)^*\) denotes complex conjugation, \(\Im\{\cdot\}\) extracts imaginary
part, and \(\curl=\nabla\times\). The electric contribution
\(\varepsilon\,\E^*\cdot(\curl\E)\) measures local rotational phase-structure of
\(\E\), while the magnetic contribution
\(\mu\,\H^*\cdot(\curl\H)\) measures the corresponding structure in \(\H\). Division
by \(2\omega\) places helicity on a frequency-normalized scale so that comparisons
between configurations at fixed \(\omega\) are not dominated by trivial frequency
rescaling.

Gauge robustness follows directly because \(h\) is built from \(\E\), \(\H\), and
their curls, each invariant under potential gauge relabeling for fixed physical
fields. The local gauge-variation identity is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.helicity-density-wave-reduction}
\delta_\chi h
\equiv
h[\mathbf{A}',\phi']-h[\mathbf{A},\phi]
=
0.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.helicity-density-wave-reduction} is the local statement that
helicity density is gauge-objective when evaluated from physical field data.

Noether-type continuity structure associated with dual rotations gives the local
helicity-balance law
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.helicity-balance}
\frac{\partial h}{\partial t}
+\divg\mathbf{f}_h
=
-q_h,
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{f}_h\) is helicity flux density and \(q_h\) is material helicity
conversion rate. In source-free homogeneous domains \(q_h=0\), so helicity is
transported but not created or annihilated locally
\cite{jackson1999,collin2001}. Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.helicity-balance} is the
helicity counterpart of the momentum and angular-momentum conservation forms
developed earlier in Sections~\ref{sec:ch01.1}--\ref{sec:ch01.2}.

\paragraph{Worked problem: right-hand versus left-hand circular polarization.}
For \(\E=E_0(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\pm\jj\hat{\mathbf{y}})/\sqrt{2}\) and matching
\(\H=\pm(E_0/\eta_0)(\hat{\mathbf{y}}\mp\jj\hat{\mathbf{x}})/\sqrt{2}\) in a
\(+\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) plane wave, Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.helicity-density} gives
\(h=\pm |E_0|^2/(2\omega\eta_0)\): opposite signs for opposite helicities.
Under gauge transform \(\mathbf{A}'=\mathbf{A}+\nabla\chi\), both \(\E\) and \(\H\)
are unchanged, so \(h\) is unchanged as required by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.helicity-density-wave-reduction}. Helicity therefore tracks
signed polarization handedness, not azimuthal phase index \(m\); a field with
\(m\neq 0\) but linear polarization can have \(h=0\) while still exhibiting strong
phase winding in \(\arg E_t\) \cite{jackson1999,balanis2016}.

For phasor diagnostics used throughout Chapter~\ref{ch:01}, define the time-average
helicity divergence residual
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.helicity-closure-residual}
\mathcal{R}_h
=
\frac{\left\|\divg\!\left\langle \mathbf{f}_h\right\rangle
+\left\langle q_h\right\rangle\right\|}
{\left\|\left\langle \mathbf{f}_h\right\rangle\right\|/\ell_h+\epsilon_{rh}},
\end{equation}
where \(\langle\cdot\rangle\) is time average, \(\ell_h>0\) a characteristic length,
and \(\epsilon_{rh}>0\) a regularization constant. Small \(\mathcal{R}_h\) certifies
that computed helicity channels satisfy the local conservation closure demanded by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.helicity-balance}.

Dual symmetry is the continuous rotation symmetry in the
\((\E,\eta\H)\)-plane:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.duality-rotation}
\begin{bmatrix}
\E_\xi\\[1mm]
\eta\H_\xi
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\xi & \sin\xi\\
-\sin\xi & \cos\xi
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
\E\\[1mm]
\eta\H
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad \xi\in\mathbb{R}.
\end{equation}
The angle \(\xi\) parameterizes continuous redistribution between electric and
magnetic components while preserving source-free Maxwell form in isotropic media.
This action is not a gauge transform; it is a physical symmetry of the field
equations under constitutive conditions that keep electric and magnetic sectors on
equal structural footing \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}.

Invariance tests under Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.duality-rotation} can be written in
closed form. Local energy density
\(u=\frac{1}{4}(\varepsilon\|\E\|^2+\mu\|\H\|^2)\) satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.dual-invariant-energy}
u_\xi
=
\frac{1}{4}\!\left(\varepsilon\|\E_\xi\|^2+\mu\|\H_\xi\|^2\right)
=u,
\end{equation}
and time-average Poynting vector
\(\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle=\frac{1}{2}\Re\{\E\times\H^*\}\) obeys
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.dual-invariant-poynting}
\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle_\xi
=
\frac{1}{2}\Re\{\E_\xi\times\H_\xi^*\}
=
\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle.
\end{equation}
Equations~\eqref{eq:ch1.dual-invariant-energy}--\eqref{eq:ch1.dual-invariant-poynting}
show that dual mixing redistributes electric/magnetic partitioning without changing
total transport and energy content in the symmetry-valid regime.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1.helicity-duality-map} gives the operational logic used in
subsequent sections: passing one gate does not imply passing the others. A quantity
may satisfy gauge/frame objectivity and still fail helicity closure in a numerical
or material setting; similarly, a helicity-stable report may lose dual-symmetry
meaning when constitutive assumptions break.

To quantify departure from the symmetry regime, introduce dual-defect index
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.duality-defect-index}
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{dual}}
=
\frac{\left\|u_\xi-u\right\|+\ell_s^{-1}\left\|\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle_\xi-\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle\right\|}
     {\left\|u\right\|+\ell_s^{-1}\left\|\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle\right\|+\epsilon_d},
\end{equation}
where \(\ell_s>0\) is a scaling length and \(\epsilon_d>0\) regularization. In an
ideal dual-symmetric domain, \(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{dual}}=0\). In lossy,
anisotropic, magnetoelectric, or strongly dispersive media,
\(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{dual}}>0\), signaling that dual rotation cannot be used as an
exact invariance principle.

The normalized helicity-content monitor remains
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.normalized-helicity-index}
\mathcal{H}_n(\mathbf{r})=
\frac{h(\mathbf{r})}
{\frac{1}{4}\!\left(\varepsilon\|\E\|^2+\mu\|\H\|^2\right)+\epsilon_h},
\end{equation}
with \(\epsilon_h>0\). Because denominator is local energy scale,
\(\mathcal{H}_n\) compares handedness strength across nonuniform fields without
conflating magnitude variation with chirality content.

Combine all subsection criteria into one decision equation:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.helicity-observable-decision}
\mathcal{D}_{h}=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{loc}}=1,\
\mathcal{R}_h\le R_{h,\max},\
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{dual}}\le D_{\mathrm{dual,max}}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(R_{h,\max}\ge 0\) and \(D_{\mathrm{dual,max}}\ge 0\) are declared
tolerances. Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.helicity-observable-decision} states that
helicity-based interpretation is accepted only when gauge locality, continuity
closure, and dual-symmetry validity are simultaneously controlled.

\begin{proposition}[Admissible helicity observable class]
\label{prop:ch1.helicity-observable-class}
In this chapter, helicity-derived reports are physically admissible observables if
and only if \(\mathcal{D}_{h}=1\).
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.observable-admissibility-triad} enforces gauge/frame
admissibility, Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.helicity-balance} with
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.helicity-closure-residual} enforces conservation closure, and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.duality-defect-index} enforces structural validity of dual
symmetry in the medium class. Their conjunction is exactly
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.helicity-observable-decision}.

The analysis here assumes linear isotropic constitutive response in the region where
duality is invoked. Conductive loss, anisotropy, bianisotropy, and pronounced
dispersion require replacing the ideal dual-rotation interpretation by a perturbed
or broken-symmetry formulation, while keeping the gauge-objectivity filter intact
\cite{jackson1999,collin2001,balanis2016}.

With gauge-invariant observable criteria, helicity closure, and dual-symmetry
qualification now fixed, the next subsection can define operational observables in
measurement language without conflating representation algebra with measurable
transport channels.


\subsection{Operational definitions of observables}
\label{sec:ch01.4.4}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

After gauge/frame admissibility is fixed, the next question is operational: by
what measurement chain does a symmetry-admissible field quantity become a reported
number with declared uncertainty and reproducibility? An operational definition in
electromagnetics is not a verbal convention; it is a closed map from field state
to instrument response, from response to estimator, and from estimator to a
traceable report contract \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001,collin2001}.

Let \(\mathcal{F}\) be a field state that already passes
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.observable-admissibility-triad}. Let
\(\mathcal{M}_\alpha\) denote the forward measurement operator for channel setting
\(\alpha\), \(y_\alpha\) the recorded datum, and \(n_\alpha\) additive disturbance.
The channel law is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.operational-measurement-model}
y_\alpha
=
\mathcal{M}_\alpha[\mathcal{F}]
+n_\alpha.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.operational-measurement-model} is the first operational
pillar: no observable is read from symbolic fields directly; it is inferred through
channel transduction physics plus disturbance structure
\cite{jackson1999,collin2001}. The disturbance term includes thermal noise,
digitization residue, drift, and calibration bias.

Collect \(N\) channel outputs in
\(\mathbf{y}=[y_1,\dots,y_N]^\mathsf{T}\in\mathbb{C}^{N}\), and let
\(\widehat{\mathcal{O}}\) denote estimator for target observable
\(\mathcal{O}[\mathcal{F}]\). Reconstruction map \(\mathcal{R}\) is accepted only if
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.operational-consistency}
\widehat{\mathcal{O}}=\mathcal{R}[\mathbf{y}],
\qquad
\lim_{\|n\|\rightarrow 0}\widehat{\mathcal{O}}
=
\mathcal{O}[\mathcal{F}],
\end{equation}
where \(\|n\|\) is norm on disturbance vector. First relation defines estimator
architecture; second relation enforces asymptotic consistency. If either fails, the
quantity is mathematically expressible yet operationally undefined.

Consistency alone is insufficient. The inverse problem must also be identifiable.
Let \(\mathcal{S}_{\mathcal{O}}\) be admissible state set restricted by the
symmetry/gauge gates from Sections~\ref{sec:ch01.3}--\ref{sec:ch01.4}. Then
observability-identifiability for \(\mathcal{O}\) requires
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.observable-identifiability}
\forall \mathcal{F}_1,\mathcal{F}_2\in\mathcal{S}_{\mathcal{O}}:\ 
\mathbf{M}[\mathcal{F}_1]=\mathbf{M}[\mathcal{F}_2]
\Longrightarrow
\mathcal{O}[\mathcal{F}_1]=\mathcal{O}[\mathcal{F}_2],
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{M}=[\mathcal{M}_1,\ldots,\mathcal{M}_N]^\mathsf{T}\). The logical
content of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.observable-identifiability} is fundamental: if two
admissible states are indistinguishable by the entire channel set but produce
different \(\mathcal{O}\), then no estimator can assign that observable uniquely.

In practice, identification quality is measured by local sensitivity. Let
\(\boldsymbol{\theta}\in\mathbb{R}^p\) parameterize admissible states, with Jacobian
\(\mathbf{J}_{\alpha i}=\partial \mathcal{M}_\alpha/\partial \theta_i\), and let
\(\mathbf{W}\succ 0\) be channel-weight matrix. Define weighted information matrix
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.operational-information-matrix}
\mathbf{G}
=
\mathbf{J}^\mathsf{H}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{J},
\end{equation}
where \((\cdot)^\mathsf{H}\) denotes Hermitian transpose. A poorly conditioned
\(\mathbf{G}\) indicates ill-posed inversion and large estimator variance
amplification; a well-conditioned \(\mathbf{G}\) indicates robust local inversion.

Uncertainty in reported observable is summarized by first-order propagation:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.operational-uncertainty-budget}
\sigma_{\mathcal{O}}^2
\approx
\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\mathcal{O}^{\mathsf{T}}
\mathbf{\Sigma}_{\theta}
\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\mathcal{O}
+\sigma_{\mathrm{cal}}^2
+\sigma_{\mathrm{model}}^2,
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{\Sigma}_{\theta}\) is parameter covariance induced by channel noise,
\(\sigma_{\mathrm{cal}}^2\) is calibration contribution, and
\(\sigma_{\mathrm{model}}^2\) is model-mismatch contribution. This equation makes
explicit that uncertainty is a structural part of the definition of an operational
observable, not an optional appendix \cite{harrington2001,balanis2016}.

Gauge and frame admissibility must hold at the channel level, not only at symbolic
field level. For rotated state \(\mathcal{F}'=\mathcal{A}_R\mathcal{F}\) and
gauge-related potentials \((\mathbf{A}',\phi')\), each channel must satisfy
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.channel-covariance}
\mathcal{M}_\alpha[\mathcal{F}']
=
\mathcal{T}_\alpha(R)\,\mathcal{M}_\alpha[\mathcal{F}],
\qquad
\mathcal{M}_\alpha[\mathbf{A}',\phi']
=
\mathcal{M}_\alpha[\mathbf{A},\phi],
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{T}_\alpha(R)\) is known channel transformation law. Violation of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.channel-covariance} means the instrumentation itself injects
symmetry bias, and any reported value cannot be claimed as observer-independent.

Calibration closure provides a direct test. Let
\(\mathcal{M}^{\mathrm{ref}}_\alpha\) be reference response and
\(\widehat{\mathcal{M}}_\alpha\) calibrated model used in inversion. Define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.channel-calibration-residual}
\rho_{\alpha}
=
\frac{\left\|\widehat{\mathcal{M}}_\alpha-\mathcal{M}^{\mathrm{ref}}_\alpha\right\|}
     {\left\|\mathcal{M}^{\mathrm{ref}}_\alpha\right\|+\epsilon_{\alpha}},
\qquad
\rho_{\max}=\max_{\alpha}\rho_\alpha,
\end{equation}
with \(\epsilon_\alpha>0\) regularization. Small \(\rho_{\max}\) certifies that the
forward model used by \(\mathcal{R}\) is compatible with metrological reference
behavior over the declared operating domain.

An operational report is accepted only when the operational layer in
Table~\ref{tab:ch1.admissibility-corollaries} is satisfied:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.observable-report-contract}
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{op}}=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_{h}=1,\
\kappa(\mathbf{G})\le \kappa_{\max},\
\rho_{\max}\le \rho_{\max}^{\mathrm{tol}},\
\sigma_{\mathcal{O}}\le \sigma_{\max}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(\kappa(\mathbf{G})\) is condition number of
\(\mathbf{G}\), \(\kappa_{\max}\) conditioning tolerance,
\(\rho_{\max}^{\mathrm{tol}}\) calibration tolerance, and \(\sigma_{\max}\)
uncertainty tolerance. This equation closes the operational definition in a form
that is testable, reproducible, and directly reusable in Chapters~2--4.

\begin{proposition}[Operational observable criterion]
\label{prop:ch1.operational-observable-criterion}
A field-derived quantity in this chapter is operationally admissible as an
observable if and only if \(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{op}}=1\).
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
The condition \(\mathcal{D}_{h}=1\) imports symmetry, gauge, and helicity validity
from Section~\ref{sec:ch01.4.3}. Identifiability and conditioning enforce stable
invertibility of data-to-observable mapping. Calibration and uncertainty bounds
ensure metrological reproducibility. Removing any clause breaks at least one of the
requirements of operational reportability.

For torque channels used later, the operational form becomes
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.operational-torque-channel}
\tau_z^{\mathrm{meas}}
=
\int_S\left(\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{t}\right)\cdot\hat{\mathbf{z}}\,dS
+\delta\tau_z,
\end{equation}
where \(S\) is sensing surface, \(\mathbf{r}\) position relative to declared origin,
\(\mathbf{t}\) traction vector from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.traction-rotation-covariance},
\(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) measurement axis, and \(\delta\tau_z\) net channel disturbance.
The deterministic integral and disturbance term must be reported jointly; otherwise
the quantity is incomplete from an operational standpoint.

To compare setups with different torque scales, define normalized channel
reliability index
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.operational-torque-reliability}
\mathcal{Q}_{\tau}
=
\frac{\left|\tau_z^{\mathrm{meas}}-\tau_z^{\mathrm{model}}\right|}
     {\left|\tau_z^{\mathrm{model}}\right|+\epsilon_{\tau}},
\end{equation}
where \(\tau_z^{\mathrm{model}}\) is model prediction under calibrated channel model
and \(\epsilon_\tau>0\) regularization. Small \(\mathcal{Q}_{\tau}\) indicates
agreement between field-based prediction and measured report within declared
instrument protocol.

This subsection assumes channels that are linear or weakly nonlinear around the
operating point, and disturbance statistics that permit stable covariance
estimation. Strong hysteresis, severe nonstationarity, hidden cross-couplings, or
unknown transfer nonlinearities require extended inverse frameworks beyond the
present chapter \cite{jackson1999,balanis2016}.

Operational definition is therefore fixed in rigorous terms: an observable is a
symmetry-admissible field quantity equipped with identifiable channel map, stable
reconstruction, calibrated closure, and explicit uncertainty budget. The following
subsection uses this foundation to show why local spin-orbit decompositions remain
interpretive tools unless they satisfy the same operational gates.


\subsection{Limits of local spin-orbit decompositions}
\label{sec:ch01.4.5}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Local spin-orbit decomposition remains mathematically valuable but physically
delicate: two admissible formalisms may assign different local ``spin-like'' and
``orbital-like'' maps while preserving identical total angular-momentum transfer.
The limits established here are constructive rather than prohibitive: local splits
serve as interpretation tools, and they are promoted to direct observables only
when gauge, boundary, and channel-level closure are all verified.

Let \(\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}(\mathbf{r})\) denote electromagnetic
angular-momentum density already fixed in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.angular-momentum-balance}. A generic local decomposition takes
the form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.local-spin-orbit-split}
\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}
=
\mathbf{l}_{\mathrm{loc}}
\;+\;
\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}
\;+\;
\divg\mathbf{C},
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{l}_{\mathrm{loc}}\) is orbital-labeled local density,
\(\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}\) is spin-labeled local density, \(\mathbf{C}\) is a
second-rank superpotential tensor, and \(\divg\mathbf{C}\) is the divergence
correction generated by decomposition convention. Equation
\eqref{eq:ch1.local-spin-orbit-split} makes clear that label assignment is not
unique at point level: superpotential terms can move content between
\(\mathbf{l}_{\mathrm{loc}}\) and \(\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}\) while preserving
\(\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}\). This means local decomposition does not define a
single physical density pair; it defines an admissible equivalence class.

To represent this class explicitly, let
\((\mathbf{l}_a,\mathbf{s}_a,\mathbf{C}_a)\) and
\((\mathbf{l}_b,\mathbf{s}_b,\mathbf{C}_b)\) denote two admissible local splits of
the same \(\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}\). Their difference can always be written
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-equivalence-class}
\mathbf{l}_b-\mathbf{l}_a
=
\divg\mathbf{Q}+\mathbf{q},
\qquad
\mathbf{s}_b-\mathbf{s}_a
=
-\divg\mathbf{Q}-\mathbf{q},
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{Q}\) is redistribution tensor and \(\mathbf{q}\) is residual
balancing vector. The paired signs guarantee that local relabeling does not alter
the summed quantity \(\mathbf{l}+\mathbf{s}\), but they do alter each local
component separately.

Under a gauge update \((\mathbf{A},\phi)\to(\mathbf{A}',\phi')\) from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.gauge-transform}, the local pair transforms as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-gauge-redistribution}
\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}'
=
\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}+\divg\mathbf{U}_\chi+\mathbf{v}_\chi,\qquad
\mathbf{l}_{\mathrm{loc}}'
=
\mathbf{l}_{\mathrm{loc}}-\divg\mathbf{U}_\chi-\mathbf{v}_\chi,
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{U}_\chi\) is gauge-generated redistribution tensor and
\(\mathbf{v}_\chi\) is residual redistribution vector. The paired signs enforce
that \(\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}'+\mathbf{l}_{\mathrm{loc}}'
=\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}+\mathbf{l}_{\mathrm{loc}}\), but individual local
components remain gauge-sensitive. In functional terms,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-gauge-redistribution} moves between admissible local
representatives; in physical terms, it explains why local spin-orbit maps can
differ without contradicting total transport predictions.

Integrating over a finite volume \(V\) with boundary \(\partial V\) and outward
unit normal \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\) yields
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-volume-test}
\int_V\!\left(\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}'+\mathbf{l}_{\mathrm{loc}}'\right)\!dV
=
\int_V\!\left(\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}+\mathbf{l}_{\mathrm{loc}}\right)\!dV
=
\int_V\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}\,dV
\;-\;
\int_{\partial V}\mathbf{C}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS.
\end{equation}
The first equality shows decomposition invariance of the sum; the second shows
explicit boundary dependence through \(\mathbf{C}\). Therefore local partitions
can be physically compared only after boundary prescription is declared, exactly
as required by the control-balance logic in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.angular-balance-integral}.

For two admissible split representatives \(a\) and \(b\), integrated difference
obeys
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-boundary-closure}
\Delta_V^{(ab)}
\equiv
\int_V(\mathbf{s}_b-\mathbf{s}_a)\,dV
=
-\int_V(\mathbf{l}_b-\mathbf{l}_a)\,dV
=
\int_{\partial V}\mathbf{Q}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS+\int_V\mathbf{q}\,dV.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-boundary-closure} resolves disagreement into
boundary and volumetric channels, which must both be controlled before integral
spin-orbit attribution can be interpreted as physically comparable.

To quantify decomposition fragility for a given field realization, define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-nonuniqueness-index}
\kappa_{\mathrm{so}}(V)=
\frac{\int_V\|\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}'-\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}\|\,dV}
{\int_V\|\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}\|\,dV+\epsilon_{\mathrm{so}}},
\end{equation}
where \(\|\cdot\|\) is Euclidean norm and \(\epsilon_{\mathrm{so}}>0\) is a small
regularization constant. Small \(\kappa_{\mathrm{so}}\) indicates decomposition
stability for that setup; large \(\kappa_{\mathrm{so}}\) signals strong
representation sensitivity and therefore reduced direct observability of local
spin/orbit attribution \cite{jackson1999,collin2001}.

Operationally reconstructed split components must also satisfy internal closure.
Let \(\widehat{\mathbf{s}}_{\mathrm{loc}}\),
\(\widehat{\mathbf{l}}_{\mathrm{loc}}\), and
\(\widehat{\mathbf{j}}_{\mathrm{em}}\) be channel-reconstructed fields from
Section~\ref{sec:ch01.4.4}. Define split-closure residual
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-measurement-residual}
\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{so}}
=
\frac{
\left\|
\widehat{\mathbf{s}}_{\mathrm{loc}}
+\widehat{\mathbf{l}}_{\mathrm{loc}}
-\widehat{\mathbf{j}}_{\mathrm{em}}
\right\|
}{
\left\|\widehat{\mathbf{j}}_{\mathrm{em}}\right\|+\epsilon_r},
\end{equation}
where \(\epsilon_r>0\) is regularization. If \(\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{so}}\) is not
small, then even before gauge comparison the measured local split fails closure.

The operational decision criterion for direct local-spin/local-orbit observability
is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-observable-decision}
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{so}}=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{op}}=1,\
\kappa_{\mathrm{so}}\le \kappa_{\mathrm{so,max}},\
\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{so}}\le R_{\mathrm{so,max}},\
\|\Delta_V^{(ab)}\|\le \Delta_{\max}
\Big),
\end{equation}
with declared tolerances
\(\kappa_{\mathrm{so,max}}\), \(R_{\mathrm{so,max}}\), and \(\Delta_{\max}\). This
condition is intentionally strict: it prevents representation-sensitive local
decompositions from being reported as if they were unique observables.

\paragraph{Worked problem: equivalent splits, different local labels.}
Suppose \(\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}=\mathbf{j}_0(\mathbf{r})\) is fixed on volume
\(V\). Decomposition~A sets \(\mathbf{l}_{\mathrm{loc}}^{(A)}=\mathbf{j}_0\),
\(\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}^{(A)}=\mathbf{0}\). Decomposition~B adds
\(\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}^{(B)}=\tfrac12\nabla\times(\mathbf{r}\times\E)\) and
adjusts \(\mathbf{l}_{\mathrm{loc}}^{(B)}\) so that
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-spin-orbit-split} still sums to \(\mathbf{j}_0\). Both splits
integrate to the same \(\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}\), yet
\(\|\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}^{(A)}-\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}^{(B)}\|\) is pointwise
large. Nonuniqueness index \(\kappa_{\mathrm{so}}\) from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-nonuniqueness-index} is therefore order unity, and
\(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{so}}=0\) unless channel reconstruction drives
\(\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{so}}\) below tolerance. Only the total
\(\int_V\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}\,dV\) is reportable without extra closure; local
spin/orbit images remain convention-tagged diagnostics \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}.

\begin{proposition}[Limits of local split observability]
\label{prop:ch1.spin-orbit-limit-criterion}
Local spin-orbit components are direct observables in this chapter only when
\(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{so}}=1\); otherwise they remain controlled interpretive
diagnostics attached to a fixed total observable.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.local-spin-orbit-split} defines decomposition class.
Equations~\eqref{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-equivalence-class} and
\eqref{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-gauge-redistribution} establish local non-uniqueness.
Equations~\eqref{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-volume-test} and
\eqref{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-boundary-closure} quantify boundary-conditioned integral
differences. Equations~\eqref{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-measurement-residual} and
\eqref{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-observable-decision} enforce operational closure. The
conjunction yields the criterion.

Projection dependence produces an additional interpretive limit. For any detector
weighting kernel \(w(\mathbf{r})\), define projected split reports
\(\Pi_s=\int_V w(\mathbf{r})\,\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{loc}}(\mathbf{r})\,dV\) and
\(\Pi_l=\int_V w(\mathbf{r})\,\mathbf{l}_{\mathrm{loc}}(\mathbf{r})\,dV\). Two
equally admissible kernels \(w_a\) and \(w_b\) need not preserve these projections.
The projection-sensitivity index
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.spin-orbit-projection-sensitivity}
\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{so}}
=
\frac{\|\Pi_s^{(a)}-\Pi_s^{(b)}\|+\|\Pi_l^{(a)}-\Pi_l^{(b)}\|}
{\|\Pi_s^{(a)}+\Pi_l^{(a)}\|+\epsilon_p},
\end{equation}
with \(\epsilon_p>0\), quantifies this dependence. Large
\(\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{so}}\) indicates that local split claims are dominated by
sampling protocol, not invariant transport content.

The derivation assumes smooth fields, linear constitutive response, and boundary
regularity sufficient for divergence manipulations. Singular gauges, strongly
inhomogeneous media, and nonlocal material response can enlarge redistribution
terms and require generalized treatment not developed in Chapter~\ref{ch:01}.

The limit is therefore explicit and constructive: local spin-orbit decomposition
remains a controlled interpretive lens, whereas frame/gauge-admissible totals and
channel-defined measurements remain the primary observables
\cite{harrington2001,balanis2016}.

\paragraph{Section 1.4 summary and continuation.}
Section~\ref{sec:ch01.4} filters potential-level statements by gauge invariance,
local-density closure, helicity symmetry, and spin-orbit equivalence limits; the
corollary symbols appear in Table~\ref{tab:ch1.admissibility-corollaries}
\cite{jackson1999,harrington2001,collin2001}. Section~\ref{sec:ch01.5} converts
surviving quantities into instrument-ready reports.


\section{Measurement and Interpretation}
\label{sec:ch01.5}

Section~\ref{sec:ch01.5} converts the admissible field quantities from
Section~\ref{sec:ch01.4} into measurable reports under finite sensors, finite
bandwidth, and finite inference accuracy. The central task is to define the full
measurement chain---interaction law, transduction channel, reconstruction map, and
uncertainty-qualified interpretation---for torque, flux, and modal content in a way
that preserves gauge/frame objectivity at every stage
\cite{jackson1999,collin2001,balanis2016}. Accordingly, each subsection separates
direct observables from model-dependent reconstructions, states the closure
conditions under which inferred quantities are admissible, and ties interpretation
claims to explicit transfer equations rather than to decomposition labels alone.


\subsection{Field-matter interaction}
\label{sec:ch01.5.1}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Field--matter interaction is the physical gate through which electromagnetic fields
become measurable laboratory signals. In this subsection, \emph{field} denotes the
phasor state \((\E,\H)\), \emph{matter} denotes the charged and polarized medium in a
declared region, and \emph{interaction} denotes transfer of energy, linear
momentum, and angular momentum into material degrees of freedom under explicit
constitutive and boundary assumptions \cite{jackson1999,collin2001}. This framing
is necessary because angular-structure claims are not observations by themselves;
they become observable only after a channel from field variables to material
response is stated quantitatively.

Let \(V_m\subset\Omega\) denote the material interaction region, with boundary
\(\partial V_m\), outward unit normal \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\), and volume element
\(dV\). Let \(\E(\mathbf{r})\) and \(\H(\mathbf{r})\) be phasor fields under the
\(\exp(-j\omega t)\) convention, let \(\mathbf{J}_c(\mathbf{r})\) be conduction-current
density inside matter, and let \(\rho(\mathbf{r})\) be free-charge density. The
time-averaged electromagnetic power-transfer density into matter is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.material-power-density}
p_m(\mathbf{r})=\frac{1}{2}\Re\!\left\{\E\cdot\mathbf{J}_c^*\right\},
\end{equation}
where \((\cdot)^*\) denotes complex conjugation and \(\Re\{\cdot\}\) extracts the
real part. Operand by operand, \(\E\cdot\mathbf{J}_c^*\) is local field-current work-rate
density in complex form, and the factor \(1/2\) converts phasor bilinear form to
time average. As a complete relation,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-power-density} identifies the local channel by which
electromagnetic energy is converted into measurable material response such as
heating or mechanical actuation \cite{jackson1999,collin2001}.

Mechanical transfer is described by the cycle-averaged Lorentz-force density
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.material-force-density}
\mathbf{f}_m(\mathbf{r})
=\frac{1}{2}\Re\!\left\{
\rho\,\E+\mathbf{J}_c\times\mu\,\H^*
\right\},
\end{equation}
where \(\mu\) is permeability and \(\times\) is vector cross product. The first
term \(\rho\,\E\) is electric-force density on free charge, while
\(\mathbf{J}_c\times\mu\H^*\) is magnetic-force density on moving charge carriers. Taken
together, Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-force-density} is the local force-transfer
law linking measurable mechanical loading to field state and material current.

To separate dissipative and source-driven channels, decompose
\(\mathbf{J}_c=\sigma\E+\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{ext}}\), where \(\sigma\ge 0\) is
effective conductivity and \(\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{ext}}\) externally impressed
current density. Substitution into Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-power-density} yields
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.material-power-decomposition}
p_m
=
\frac{1}{2}\sigma\|\E\|^2
+\frac{1}{2}\Re\!\left\{\E\cdot\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{ext}}^*\right\}.
\end{equation}
The first term is irreversible Joule deposition; the second term is controllable
exchange with the impressed source network.

Finite-time momentum transfer is obtained by temporal integration of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-force-density}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.material-momentum-transfer}
\Delta\mathbf{p}_m(t_1,t_2)
=
\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\!\!\int_{V_m}\mathbf{f}_m(\mathbf{r},t)\,dV\,dt.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-momentum-transfer} links local loading density to
impulse measurements over finite acquisition windows.

Torque-channel transfer follows from the moment of force density about declared
origin \(\mathbf{r}_0\):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.material-torque-density}
\boldsymbol{\tau}_m(\mathbf{r};\mathbf{r}_0)
=
(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_0)\times\mathbf{f}_m(\mathbf{r}).
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-torque-density} makes origin dependence explicit at
the interaction stage, so origin declaration is part of the measurement definition,
not a secondary reporting detail \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}.

Integrating local channels over \(V_m\) gives measurable global interaction outputs:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.material-global-channels}
P_m=\int_{V_m}p_m\,dV,\qquad
\mathbf{F}_m=\int_{V_m}\mathbf{f}_m\,dV,\qquad
\mathbf{T}_m=\int_{V_m}\boldsymbol{\tau}_m\,dV.
\end{equation}
The triple \((P_m,\mathbf{F}_m,\mathbf{T}_m)\) is the first complete observable map
for this subsection: thermal conversion, net mechanical load, and torque transfer
must be interpreted jointly when evaluating field structure.

Control-volume closure links boundary flux to deposited material power. With energy
density \(u=\frac{1}{4}(\varepsilon\|\E\|^2+\mu\|\H\|^2)\) and time-average Poynting
vector \(\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle=\frac{1}{2}\Re\{\E\times\H^*\}\),
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.material-balance-control-volume}
\int_{\partial V_m}\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS
+\int_{V_m}p_m\,dV
+\frac{d}{dt}\int_{V_m}u\,dV
=0.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-balance-control-volume} is the local-to-global
consistency condition used to test whether inferred interaction channels are
physically closed at the chosen averaging scale.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1.field-matter-transfer} identifies the exact transition from
field description to measurement statement: every reported quantity is mediated by
declared interaction channels, not by pattern visualization alone. In particular,
the presence of angular structure in the field does not imply measurable torque
unless the force and torque channels satisfy closure and calibration constraints.

For regime comparison, define the field--matter coupling index
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.material-coupling-index}
\mathcal{C}_m
=
\frac{\left|\int_{V_m}p_m\,dV\right|}
     {\left|\int_{\partial V_m}\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS\right|+\epsilon_c},
\qquad \epsilon_c>0.
\end{equation}
Small \(\mathcal{C}_m\) indicates weak conversion and predominantly transmissive
interaction; large \(\mathcal{C}_m\) indicates strong local conversion into thermal
or mechanical response. Because \(\mathcal{C}_m\) is normalized, it compares
interaction strength across setups with different absolute power levels.

Interaction reports are accepted in this chapter only when operational and closure
requirements are both met:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.material-interaction-admissibility}
\mathcal{D}_m=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{op}}=1,\
\left|\int_{\partial V_m}\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS
+\int_{V_m}p_m\,dV
+\frac{d}{dt}\int_{V_m}u\,dV\right|\le \delta_E,\
\mathcal{C}_m\le C_{m,\max}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(\delta_E\ge 0\) is closure tolerance and \(C_{m,\max}\ge 0\) is interaction
classification threshold. Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-interaction-admissibility}
prevents non-closed transfer channels from being interpreted as physical evidence.

\begin{proposition}[Admissible field--matter interaction report]
\label{prop:ch1.field-matter-interaction-admissibility}
The interaction outputs \((P_m,\mathbf{F}_m,\mathbf{T}_m)\) are admissible
observables in Chapter~\ref{ch:01} only if \(\mathcal{D}_m=1\).
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Equations~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-power-density}--\eqref{eq:ch1.material-torque-density}
define local transfer channels. Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-global-channels}
maps these channels to measurable totals. Equation
\eqref{eq:ch1.material-balance-control-volume} enforces energy closure, and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-interaction-admissibility} imposes operational
admissibility inherited from Section~\ref{sec:ch01.4}. Their conjunction yields the
criterion.

For finite-time sampling windows, impulse-channel measurement follows from
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.material-impulse-measurement}
\mathbf{I}_m(t_1,t_2)
=
\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\mathbf{F}_m(t)\,dt
=
\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\!\!\int_{V_m}\mathbf{f}_m(\mathbf{r},t)\,dV\,dt.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-impulse-measurement} links cycle-averaged force
densities to time-window detector outputs, making the temporal meaning of reported
mechanical response explicit.

The validity envelope of this subsection remains linear local constitutive response,
harmonic or quasi-harmonic averaging, and continuum-scale charge/current
descriptions. Strong nonlinear transport, discrete-particle kinetics, ultrafast
transients, or unresolved multiscale material structure require generalized channel
models beyond the present establishment \cite{harrington2001,balanis2016}.

\paragraph{Worked problem: Joule heating and torque on a conducting disk.}
Let a thin conducting disk of radius \(a\), conductivity \(\sigma\), occupy
\(V_m=\{0\le z\le t,\ \rho\le a\}\) in a uniform phasor field
\(\E=E_0\hat{\mathbf{x}}\), \(\H=H_0\hat{\mathbf{y}}\). From
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-power-decomposition} with \(\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{ext}}=\mathbf{0}\),
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.worked-disk-joule}
P_m=\int_{V_m}\frac{1}{2}\sigma\|\E\|^2\,dV
=\frac{1}{2}\sigma E_0^2\pi a^2 t.
\end{equation}
The Lorentz-force density of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-force-density} reduces to
\(\mathbf{f}_m=\tfrac{1}{2}\Re\{\mathbf{J}_c\times\mu\H^*\}\) with
\(\mathbf{J}_c=\sigma\E\), giving net force \(\mathbf{F}_m=\mathbf{0}\) by symmetry
but edge-local torque density about \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) when \(\mathbf{E}\) and
\(\mathbf{H}\) are not collinear. Integrating
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-torque-density} and closing with
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.material-balance-control-volume} checks that deposited
\(P_m\) equals net Poynting influx through \(\partial V_m\) minus storage change.
For \(a=1\,\mathrm{cm}\), \(t=1\,\mathrm{mm}\), \(\sigma=5.8\times 10^7\,\mathrm{S/m}\),
and \(|E_0|=10^3\,\mathrm{V/m}\), Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.worked-disk-joule} gives
\(P_m\approx 0.91\,\mathrm{W}\), a measurable heating channel that must accompany
any torque report on the same fixture \cite{jackson1999,collin2001}.

Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.1.4} into calibrated torque and momentum-flux measurement
relations.


\subsection{Torque, momentum flux, and stress measurements}
\label{sec:ch01.5.2}
% TOC tag: [USE SS1.1.4]

Stress-based measurement in this chapter is inherited directly from the owner
framework in Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.1.4}; no re-derivation of field momentum
balance is introduced here. The objective is strictly operational: map the
already-established stress tensor to instrument outputs for force and torque.
Accordingly, every reported value in this subsection is a channel-qualified
measurement statement with declared geometry, calibration, and uncertainty
assumptions \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001,collin2001}.

Let \(\mathbf{T}\) be Maxwell stress tensor from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress}, let \(S_m\) be the sensing surface around a
test body, and let \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\) denote outward surface normal on \(S_m\).
The traction density is \(\mathbf{t}=\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\). For a
declared reference origin \(\mathbf{r}_0\), measured force vector
\(\mathbf{y}_F\), and measured torque component \(y_\tau\) about axis unit vector
\(\hat{\mathbf{u}}_\tau\), a channel-calibrated measurement model is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.stress-measurement-model}
\mathbf{y}_F
=\mathbf{K}_F\!\left(\int_{S_m}\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS\right)
+\mathbf{n}_F,\qquad
y_\tau
=K_\tau\!\left(
\int_{S_m}\!\big[(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_0)\times(\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})\big]
\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_\tau\,dS
\right)+n_\tau,
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{K}_F\) and \(K_\tau\) are calibration operators/scalars, and
\(\mathbf{n}_F,n_\tau\) collect channel disturbances. The two surface integrals are
deterministic electromagnetic load predictions inherited from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress}; calibration and disturbance terms convert that
prediction into practical measurement channels.

For full vector torque reporting, define an explicit channel
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.stress-torque-vector-channel}
\mathbf{y}_T
=
\mathbf{K}_T\!\left(
\int_{S_m}\!\big[(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_0)\times(\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})\big]\,dS
\right)+\mathbf{n}_T,
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{K}_T\) is vector torque calibration map and \(\mathbf{n}_T\) is
channel disturbance. The scalar channel \(y_\tau\) is then
\(y_\tau=\mathbf{y}_T\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_\tau\) after consistent axis
normalization.

Origin declaration controls comparability of torque channels. If origin changes
from \(\mathbf{r}_0\) to \(\mathbf{r}_0'=\mathbf{r}_0+\Delta\mathbf{r}\), the
deterministic torque integral satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.stress-origin-shift-torque}
\mathbf{T}_{\mathrm{det}}'
=
\mathbf{T}_{\mathrm{det}}-\Delta\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{det}},
\end{equation}
with
\(\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{det}}=\int_{S_m}\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS\) and
\(\mathbf{T}_{\mathrm{det}}=\int_{S_m}(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_0)\times(\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})\,dS\).
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.stress-origin-shift-torque} is the operational form of
origin dependence needed for laboratory-to-laboratory torque reconciliation.

At mathematical level, Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.stress-measurement-model} composes
field-to-traction mapping, surface integration, and instrument transfer in one
statement. At physical level, it explains why two laboratories can report
different torque values from the same field realization unless origin declaration,
axis definition, and calibration normalization are matched.

Surface selection is itself a measurable limit. For two admissible enclosing
surfaces \(S_a\) and \(S_b\), define stress-flux closure residual
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.stress-surface-closure-residual}
\mathcal{R}_S
=
\frac{
\left\|\int_{S_a}\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS-\int_{S_b}\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS\right\|
+\ell_\tau^{-1}\left\|\int_{S_a}\mathbf{r}\times(\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})\,dS-\int_{S_b}\mathbf{r}\times(\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})\,dS\right\|
}{
\left\|\int_{S_a}\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS\right\|
+\ell_\tau^{-1}\left\|\int_{S_a}\mathbf{r}\times(\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})\,dS\right\|
+\epsilon_S
},
\end{equation}
where \(\ell_\tau>0\) is scaling length and \(\epsilon_S>0\) is regularization.
Small \(\mathcal{R}_S\) indicates robust stress-channel closure across declared
sampling surfaces.

First-order torque uncertainty combines calibration, geometry, and noise:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.stress-torque-uncertainty}
\sigma_\tau^2
\approx
\left\|\frac{\partial y_\tau}{\partial K_\tau}\right\|^2\sigma_{K_\tau}^2
+\left\|\frac{\partial y_\tau}{\partial \mathbf{r}_0}\right\|^2\sigma_{r_0}^2
+\left\|\frac{\partial y_\tau}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{u}}_\tau}\right\|^2\sigma_u^2
+\sigma_{n_\tau}^2.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.stress-torque-uncertainty} makes explicit that geometric
registration accuracy is as important as electrical calibration in torque
measurement quality.

Stress-based reports are accepted in this chapter only when interaction and channel
gates hold simultaneously:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.stress-measurement-admissibility}
\mathcal{D}_{\sigma}=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_m=1,\
\mathcal{R}_{S}\le R_{S,\max},\
\sigma_\tau\le \sigma_{\tau,\max},\
\|\mathbf{n}_F\|\le n_{F,\max}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(R_{S,\max}\), \(\sigma_{\tau,\max}\), and \(n_{F,\max}\) are declared
tolerances. Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.stress-measurement-admissibility} excludes
stress-channel claims with unresolved surface inconsistency or uncontrolled
uncertainty.

\begin{proposition}[Admissible stress-channel torque report]
\label{prop:ch1.stress-torque-admissibility}
Stress-derived force and torque outputs are operationally admissible observables in
Chapter~\ref{ch:01} only if \(\mathcal{D}_{\sigma}=1\).
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.stress-measurement-model} defines calibrated stress channels.
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:ch1.stress-origin-shift-torque} and
\eqref{eq:ch1.stress-surface-closure-residual} enforce geometric and surface
closure. Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.stress-torque-uncertainty} enforces uncertainty
quantification. Their conjunction with Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.stress-measurement-admissibility}
is necessary and sufficient for admissible reporting.

The model assumes quasi-static sensor response over one cycle-average window and
surface geometry known to sufficient precision for traction integration. Large
vibration bandwidth, unknown moving boundaries, or unresolved near-contact fields
can invalidate the simple calibration form in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.stress-measurement-model} and require dynamic inverse
identification \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}.

\paragraph{Worked problem: cylindrical control-surface torque integration.}
Enclose a short dipole along \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) with cylinder
\(S_m=\{\rho=\rho_0,\ 0\le z\le L\}\) in the radiative zone. For outward normal
\(\hat{\mathbf{n}}=\hat{\boldsymbol{\rho}}\) on the curved wall, traction
\(\mathbf{t}=\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{\rho}}\) from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress} integrates to net force \(\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{det}}\)
and torque \(\mathbf{T}_{\mathrm{det}}\) in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.stress-measurement-model}. With origin at the dipole center and
\(\hat{\mathbf{u}}_\tau=\hat{\mathbf{z}}\), only azimuthal stress asymmetry contributes
to \(y_\tau\). Numerically, discretize \(S_m\) into \(N_\phi\times N_z\) panels,
accumulate \(\sum_k (\mathbf{r}_k\times\mathbf{t}_k)\cdot\hat{\mathbf{z}}\,\Delta S_k\),
and compare with independent far-zone formula
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-torque-power} for single-mode excitation. Surface closure
residual \(\mathcal{R}_S\) between cylinder \(S_a\) and enclosing sphere \(S_b\)
should fall below \(R_{S,\max}\) when both lie in the radiative zone; failure indicates
truncated near-field content on \(S_a\) \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001,collin2001}.

The section next states how modal coefficients are estimated from finite
measurements and where those estimates become ill-conditioned.


\subsection{Modal projections and measurement limits}
\label{sec:ch01.5.3}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

Angular interpretation often proceeds through modal coefficients, but those
coefficients are not primitive observables; they are reconstructions from finite
channel data. The projection model introduced below exposes the resolution
limits imposed by finite aperture, finite bandwidth, and noise.

The heading terms are used in the strict operational sense. A \emph{modal
projection} is a channel-weighted linear functional on measured boundary data; a
\emph{measurement limit} is any bound that prevents stable recovery of a modal
degree of freedom from available channels. This distinction is essential: a mode
can be mathematically definable yet experimentally unresolved.

Let \(\Gamma_m\) denote a measurement surface, let
\(\{\mathbf{w}_q(\mathbf{r})\}_{q=1}^{Q}\) be known channel weighting functions on
\(\Gamma_m\), and let \(y_q\) be measured channel data. For tangential electric
field \(\E_t\), channel outputs satisfy
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.modal-projection-data}
y_q=\int_{\Gamma_m}\mathbf{w}_q^*(\mathbf{r})\cdot\E_t(\mathbf{r})\,dS+n_q,
\qquad q=1,\dots,Q,
\end{equation}
where \(n_q\) is measurement noise in channel \(q\). Each integral is a weighted
projection of \(\E_t\) onto channel kernel \(\mathbf{w}_q\), so the data vector
\(\mathbf{y}\) captures only the subspace interrogated by the chosen sensing set.

Represent \(\E_t\) in a model basis \(\{\boldsymbol{\psi}_m\}_{m=1}^{M}\) with
coefficients \(a_m\): \(\E_t\approx\sum_{m=1}^{M}a_m\boldsymbol{\psi}_m\). Then
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.modal-projection-data} becomes the finite-dimensional inverse
system \(\mathbf{y}=G\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{n}\), where
\(G_{qm}=\int_{\Gamma_m}\mathbf{w}_q^*\cdot\boldsymbol{\psi}_m\,dS\). Define also
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.modal-projection-matrix}
G_{qm}
=
\int_{\Gamma_m}\mathbf{w}_q^*(\mathbf{r})\cdot\boldsymbol{\psi}_m(\mathbf{r})\,dS,
\end{equation}
and the channel information matrix
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.modal-information-matrix}
\mathbf{F}_{a}=G^*\Sigma_n^{-1}G,
\end{equation}
with \(\Sigma_n=\mathrm{Cov}[\mathbf{n}]\). Small eigenvalues of
\(\mathbf{F}_a\) identify weakly observable modal directions.

A stable regularized estimator is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.modal-regularized-estimator}
\widehat{\mathbf{a}}_\lambda
=\left(G^*G+\lambda I\right)^{-1}G^*\mathbf{y},
\end{equation}
where \(G^*\) is conjugate transpose, \(I\) is identity matrix, and \(\lambda>0\)
is regularization parameter. Operator-wise, \(G^*G\) encodes channel sensitivity
to basis directions, \(\lambda I\) suppresses small-singular-value amplification,
and left multiplication by \((G^*G+\lambda I)^{-1}G^*\) yields the reconstructed
coefficient vector.

Using singular value decomposition \(G=U\Sigma V^*\), reconstruction acquires
spectral-filter form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.modal-spectral-filter}
\widehat{\mathbf{a}}_\lambda
=
\sum_{k=1}^{r}
\frac{\sigma_k}{\sigma_k^2+\lambda}
\left(\mathbf{u}_k^*\mathbf{y}\right)\mathbf{v}_k,
\end{equation}
where \(r=\mathrm{rank}(G)\), and \((\mathbf{u}_k,\mathbf{v}_k,\sigma_k)\) are
singular triplets. The filter factor
\(\sigma_k/(\sigma_k^2+\lambda)\) suppresses weakly measured directions.

Bias--variance tradeoff for this estimator is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.modal-bias-variance}
\mathbb{E}\!\left[\widehat{\mathbf{a}}_\lambda-\mathbf{a}\right]
=
-\lambda\left(G^*G+\lambda I\right)^{-1}\mathbf{a},
\qquad
\mathrm{Cov}\!\left[\widehat{\mathbf{a}}_\lambda\right]
=
\left(G^*G+\lambda I\right)^{-1}G^*\Sigma_nG\left(G^*G+\lambda I\right)^{-1}.
\end{equation}
Larger \(\lambda\) lowers variance at the price of increased modal bias; smaller
\(\lambda\) reduces bias but raises sensitivity to channel noise.

As a complete inverse relation,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.modal-regularized-estimator} formalizes a central measurement
fact: modal descriptions are model-dependent estimates whose reliability depends
on channel geometry and conditioning, not on phase pattern alone. This condition
is the section-level safeguard against over-interpreting visually appealing but
weakly observable modal decompositions \cite{collin2001,balanis2016}.

Quantitative resolution is bounded by singular-spectrum decay of \(G\). Let
\(\sigma_{\min}^{(\mathrm{eff})}\) be the smallest retained singular value after
truncation. Then, for perturbation \(\delta\mathbf{y}\), one obtains the
conditioning bound
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.modal-conditioning-bound}
\|\delta\widehat{\mathbf{a}}_\lambda\|_2
\le
\left\|\left(G^*G+\lambda I\right)^{-1}G^*\right\|_2\,
\|\delta\mathbf{y}\|_2
\equiv
\kappa_\lambda\|\delta\mathbf{y}\|_2.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.modal-conditioning-bound} defines amplification factor
\(\kappa_\lambda\), the primary conditioning index for modal reconstruction quality.

Effective observable modal dimension at threshold \(\tau_\sigma\in(0,1)\) is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.modal-effective-rank}
r_{\mathrm{eff}}(\tau_\sigma)
=
\#\left\{k:\sigma_k/\sigma_1\ge\tau_\sigma\right\}.
\end{equation}
The value \(r_{\mathrm{eff}}\) is the number of modal directions that remain
resolvable above the chosen singular-value floor.

To quantify aperture dependence, let
\(\widehat{\mathbf{a}}_\lambda^{(a)}\) and
\(\widehat{\mathbf{a}}_\lambda^{(b)}\) be reconstructions from two admissible
measurement surfaces \(\Gamma_a,\Gamma_b\). Define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.modal-cross-aperture-consistency}
\mathcal{C}_{ab}
=
\frac{\|\widehat{\mathbf{a}}_\lambda^{(a)}-\widehat{\mathbf{a}}_\lambda^{(b)}\|_2}
{\|\widehat{\mathbf{a}}_\lambda^{(a)}\|_2+\epsilon_a},
\qquad \epsilon_a>0.
\end{equation}
Small \(\mathcal{C}_{ab}\) indicates modal interpretation robust to admissible
aperture variation; large \(\mathcal{C}_{ab}\) indicates geometry-contingent modal
labels.

\paragraph{Worked problem: two-mode reconstruction with rank-deficient sensing.}
Let \(M=2\) with basis modes \(\boldsymbol{\psi}_1,\boldsymbol{\psi}_2\) and
\(Q=2\) channels producing
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.worked-modal-G}
G=
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 1\\
1 & -1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf{a}=
\begin{bmatrix}
a_1\\ a_2
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf{y}=G\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{n}.
\end{equation}
Then \(\sigma_1=\sigma_2=\sqrt{2}\), \(\kappa(G)=\sigma_1/\sigma_2=1\), and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.modal-regularized-estimator} recovers \(\mathbf{a}\) exactly when
\(\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{0}\). Replace the second channel with a duplicate of the first
(\(G=[1\ 1;\ 1\ 1]\)): now \(\sigma_2=0\), \(\kappa(G)\to\infty\), and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.modal-conditioning-bound} shows that noise \(\delta\mathbf{y}\)
amplifies without bound unless \(\lambda>0\) in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.modal-spectral-filter}. With \(\lambda=0.1\), \(\sigma_2=0\), and
unit noise, the second coefficient estimate becomes bias-dominated rather than
observable. Effective rank \(r_{\mathrm{eff}}\) from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.modal-effective-rank} drops from two to one, forcing the second
modal label to inference-only status under
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.observable-decision-rule} \cite{collin2001,balanis2016}.

Modal reports are accepted only when stress-channel admissibility and modal limits
are simultaneously satisfied:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.modal-reportability-gate}
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{mod}}=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_{\sigma}=1,\
r_{\mathrm{eff}}\ge r_{\min},\
\kappa_\lambda\le \kappa_{\max},\
\mathcal{C}_{ab}\le C_{\max}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(r_{\min}\), \(\kappa_{\max}\), and \(C_{\max}\) are protocol thresholds.
This gate prevents inversion-fragile coefficients from being over-claimed as direct
angular observables.

\begin{proposition}[Admissible modal inference report]
\label{prop:ch1.modal-reportability}
Modal coefficients in this chapter are admissible inference reports only if
\(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{mod}}=1\).
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.modal-projection-data} defines measured channels and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.modal-regularized-estimator} defines inversion. The limits
\(r_{\mathrm{eff}}\), \(\kappa_\lambda\), and \(\mathcal{C}_{ab}\) quantify rank,
conditioning, and cross-aperture stability, respectively. Their conjunction with
upstream stress-channel admissibility yields
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.modal-reportability-gate}.

The present derivation assumes linear measurement kernels, known basis functions,
and perturbations small enough for first-order conditioning analysis. Model
mismatch, nonlinear detectors, or incomplete channel characterization require
broader uncertainty quantification than addressed here.

The final subsection formulates a decision criterion that separates direct
observables from quantities that remain inference-dependent descriptors.


\subsection{Observables versus inferred quantities}
\label{sec:ch01.5.4}
% TOC tag: [HOME]

This subsection closes Section~\ref{sec:ch01.5} by resolving a recurrent ambiguity:
which reported quantities are direct observables and which are inference-dependent
descriptors. The distinction is mandatory for Chapter~\ref{ch:01}, because a
mathematically coherent descriptor can still fail measurement admissibility if its
classification tag is omitted.

Let \(\mathcal{O}_d\) denote a direct observable produced by calibrated channel map
\(\mathcal{M}\), and \(\mathcal{Q}_i\) denote an inferred quantity produced by
reconstruction map \(\mathcal{R}\) from data \(\mathbf{y}\). The operational role
split is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.direct-inferred-role-split}
\mathcal{O}_d=\mathcal{M}[\mathcal{F}],\qquad
\mathcal{Q}_i=\mathcal{R}[\mathbf{y}],\qquad
\mathbf{y}=\mathcal{M}[\mathcal{F}]+\mathbf{n}.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.direct-inferred-role-split} separates channel-output space
from reconstruction space: direct observables are channel-native, inferred
quantities are estimator-native.

To compare reliability classes on one scale, define inference admissibility index
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.observable-inference-criterion}
\mathfrak{A}(\mathcal{Q}_i)
=
\frac{\mathrm{Std}[\mathcal{Q}_i]}{\sigma_d}
\cdot
\kappa_{\mathrm{eff}}
\cdot
\chi_{\mathrm{sym}},
\end{equation}
where \(\sigma_d\) is direct-channel uncertainty floor,
\(\kappa_{\mathrm{eff}}\) inversion-sensitivity gain, and
\(\chi_{\mathrm{sym}}\ge 1\) symmetry-compliance penalty factor derived from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.channel-covariance}. The index rises when estimator uncertainty,
conditioning burden, or symmetry mismatch increases.

Because reconstruction reliability also depends on model discrepancy, define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.inference-model-mismatch-index}
\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{mis}}
=
\frac{\|\mathbf{y}-\mathcal{M}[\widehat{\mathcal{F}}]\|_2}
{\|\mathbf{y}\|_2+\epsilon_m},
\qquad \epsilon_m>0,
\end{equation}
where \(\widehat{\mathcal{F}}\) is reconstructed field consistent with
\(\mathcal{Q}_i\). Small \(\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{mis}}\) indicates channel-data
compatibility; large \(\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{mis}}\) indicates unresolved model
error.

The reportability decision is stated by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.observable-decision-rule}
\begin{aligned}
\mathfrak{A}(\mathcal{Q}_i)\le \tau_A
\ \wedge\ 
\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{mis}}\le M_{\max}
&\Longrightarrow
\text{report as constrained observable surrogate},\\
\mathfrak{A}(\mathcal{Q}_i)> \tau_A
\ \vee\ 
\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{mis}}> M_{\max}
&\Longrightarrow
\text{report as inference-only descriptor}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Here \(\tau_A>0\) and \(M_{\max}>0\) are protocol thresholds fixed before analysis.
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.observable-decision-rule} converts qualitative caution into
an explicit classifier.

At chapter level, direct-observable status requires all upstream gates:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.global-observable-admissibility}
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{obs}}=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_{\sigma}=1,\
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{mod}}=1,\
\mathfrak{A}(\mathcal{Q}_i)\le \tau_A,\
\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{mis}}\le M_{\max}
\Big).
\end{equation}
This equation joins channel calibration, modal conditioning, and inference
classification in one acceptance statement.

\begin{proposition}[Observable-inference classification criterion]
\label{prop:ch1.observable-inference-criterion}
Any reported quantity in this chapter is admissible as a direct observable or
constrained surrogate only under Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.observable-decision-rule}; all
other quantities must be labeled inference-only.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.direct-inferred-role-split} defines role separation.
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.observable-inference-criterion} quantifies uncertainty/symmetry
burden, and Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.inference-model-mismatch-index} quantifies channel
compatibility. Their thresholded combination in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.observable-decision-rule}, with upstream gates in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.global-observable-admissibility}, yields the stated criterion.

This framework assumes calibrated channels, declared thresholds, and uncertainty
models consistent with Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.operational-measurement-model}. Under
adversarial noise, severe model mismatch, or nonstationary drift, fixed-threshold
classification should be replaced by Bayesian model comparison or worst-case robust
decision procedures \cite{harrington2001,collin2001,balanis2016}.

\paragraph{Section 1.5 consolidation and handoff.}
Section~\ref{sec:ch01.5} closes the measurement stack---field--matter transfer,
stress-flux torque channels, modal reconstruction limits, and
observable-versus-inference classification---as specialized layers of
Table~\ref{tab:ch1.admissibility-corollaries}. Section~\ref{sec:ch01.6} stress-tests
this contract on worked configurations.

\label{sec:ch01.6}

Section~\ref{sec:ch01.6} serves as the verification layer of Chapter~\ref{ch:01}:
each subsection is posed as a complete problem with schematic geometry, explicit

\subsection{Angular momentum conservation in radiation}
\label{sec:ch01.6.1}
% TOC tag: [FIG+PROB][USE SS1.1.5]

Consider a finite radiator enclosed by a closed control surface \(S_R\) positioned
in the radiative zone. Let \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\) be outward unit normal on \(S_R\),
\(\mathbf{r}\) the position vector measured from declared origin \(\mathbf{r}_0\),
and \(\mathbf{T}\) the cycle-averaged Maxwell stress tensor inherited from
Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.1.4}. The problem is to compute and interpret measurable
mechanical torque about axis \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) from radiated power and
channel-resolved angular transport without re-deriving the conservation theorem
owned by Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.1.5} \cite{jackson1999,collin2001}.

\paragraph{Problem statement and solution outline.}
A compact helical antenna at the origin radiates in the far zone. A spherical
control surface \(S_R=\{\mathbf{r}:\|\mathbf{r}\|=R\}\) with \(k_0R\gg 1\) encloses
the source. Mechanical torque about \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) is measured on the feed
structure, and total radiated power is monitored on \(S_R\). The task is to predict
\(\tau_z^{\mathrm{mech}}\) from \(P_{\mathrm{rad}}\) using inherited conservation
laws, then compare prediction with calibrated channels.

\emph{Step 1---Field and stress on \(S_R\).}
For dominant mode \((m,\sigma)=(1,+1)\), far-zone fields scale as
\(|\E|\propto 1/R\) with azimuthal phase \(e^{\jj\phi}\). The Maxwell stress
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.maxwell-stress} is evaluated on \(S_R\) with outward normal
\(\hat{\mathbf{n}}=\hat{\mathbf{r}}\).

\emph{Step 2---Angular flux integration.}
The angular-momentum flux density is
\([\mathbf{r}\times(\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})]\cdot\hat{\mathbf{z}}\).
Integrating over \(S_R\) yields \(\dot J_z\) in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-transport-factor}.

\emph{Step 3---Torque balance.}
By Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-torque-flux}, \(\tau_z^{\mathrm{mech}}=-\dot J_z\).

\emph{Step 4---Modal power sum.}
If multiple modes contribute, use Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-multimode-torque} with
measured \(P_{m,\sigma}\) from directional decomposition of
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:ch1.directional-power-density}--\eqref{eq:ch1.directional-am-flux}.

\emph{Step 5---Closure test.}
Compute \(\mathcal{R}_\tau\) from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-torque-closure-residual}
and verify \(\mathcal{D}_{61}=1\) from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-admissibility-gate}.

Write calibrated measurement channels as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex61-channel-model}
y_{\tau}=K_{\tau}\tau_z^{\mathrm{mech}}+n_{\tau},
\qquad
y_{P}=K_{P}P_{\mathrm{rad}}+n_{P},
\end{equation}
where \(y_{\tau}\) and \(y_P\) are reported torque and power outputs,
\(K_{\tau}\) and \(K_P\) are calibration factors, and
\(n_{\tau},n_{P}\) are channel disturbances. Equation
\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-channel-model} fixes the measurement semantics before any field
transport interpretation is attempted.

For steady harmonic operation, storage variation over one cycle-average window is
small. The inherited angular-momentum balance therefore reduces to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex61-torque-flux}
\tau_z^{\mathrm{mech}}
=
-\int_{S_R}
\big[\mathbf{r}\times(\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})\big]
\cdot\hat{\mathbf{z}}\,dS,
\end{equation}
where \(\tau_z^{\mathrm{mech}}\) is measured mechanical torque component on the
load. The integrand is local angular-momentum flux density crossing \(S_R\),
projected on \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\); the surface integral is total exported
electromagnetic angular momentum per unit time.

Let \(P_{\mathrm{rad}}\) denote radiated power through \(S_R\), and define
dimensionless angular transport factor \(\chi_J\) by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex61-transport-factor}
\chi_J=\frac{\dot J_z}{P_{\mathrm{rad}}},
\qquad
\dot J_z=
\int_{S_R}
\big[\mathbf{r}\times(\mathbf{T}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})\big]
\cdot\hat{\mathbf{z}}\,dS.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-transport-factor} separates a geometric/dynamical
conversion factor \(\chi_J\) from absolute power scale \(P_{\mathrm{rad}}\), so
that torque prediction can be compared across different radiated-power levels.

In the common single-helical-channel limit with azimuthal index \(m\in\mathbb{Z}\)
and circular-polarization helicity \(\sigma\in\{-1,+1\}\), the channel factor is
\(\chi_J\approx (m+\sigma)/\omega\), where \(\omega\) is angular frequency
\cite{jackson1999,balanis2016}.
Substituting into Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-torque-flux} yields the application law
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex61-torque-power}
\tau_z^{\mathrm{mech}}
\approx
-\frac{m+\sigma}{\omega}\,P_{\mathrm{rad}}.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-torque-power} is not a new conservation theorem; it
is a regime-specific evaluator obtained by applying the conservation
statement of Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.1.5} to a radiative control surface.

For multimode radiation, the same mapping becomes
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex61-multimode-torque}
\tau_z^{\mathrm{mech}}
=
-\sum_{m,\sigma}\frac{m+\sigma}{\omega}\,P_{m,\sigma}
-\Delta\tau_{\mathrm{leak}},
\end{equation}
where \(P_{m,\sigma}\) is radiated power carried by modal channel \((m,\sigma)\),
and \(\Delta\tau_{\mathrm{leak}}\) collects unresolved leakage channels outside the
declared modal set. Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-multimode-torque} provides the
problem-grade extension needed when multiple channels are simultaneously excited.

The closure residual associated with this model is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex61-torque-closure-residual}
\mathcal{R}_{\tau}
=
\frac{\left|\tau_z^{\mathrm{mech}}+\sum_{m,\sigma}\frac{m+\sigma}{\omega}\,P_{m,\sigma}+\Delta\tau_{\mathrm{leak}}\right|}
     {\left|\sum_{m,\sigma}\frac{m+\sigma}{\omega}\,P_{m,\sigma}\right|+\epsilon_{\tau}},
\qquad \epsilon_{\tau}>0.
\end{equation}
Small \(\mathcal{R}_{\tau}\) indicates that measured torque and stress-flux
transport prediction are mutually consistent within declared channel resolution.

An admissibility gate for this worked case is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex61-admissibility-gate}
\mathcal{D}_{61}=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_{\sigma}=1,\
\mathcal{R}_{\tau}\le R_{\tau,\max},\
|n_{\tau}|\le n_{\tau,\max},\
|n_{P}|\le n_{P,\max}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(R_{\tau,\max}\), \(n_{\tau,\max}\), and \(n_{P,\max}\) are protocol
thresholds. Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-admissibility-gate} forces every torque
claim in this example to carry an explicit channel-quality tag.

\begin{proposition}[Radiative torque verification criterion]
\label{prop:ch1.ex61-torque-verification}
The radiation-to-torque conclusion of this subsection is accepted as physically
verified only if \(\mathcal{D}_{61}=1\).
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-torque-flux} gives inherited conservation mapping,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-multimode-torque} gives modal evaluator,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-torque-closure-residual} quantifies mismatch, and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-admissibility-gate} applies the measurement admissibility
contract. Their conjunction is necessary for verification.

\paragraph{Numerical closure walk-through.}
Take single-mode excitation \(m=1\), \(\sigma=+1\), \(\omega=2\pi f\) with
\(f=10\,\mathrm{GHz}\). Suppose power monitoring on \(S_R\) reports
\(y_P=1.00\,\mathrm{W}\) with calibration \(K_P=1\) and \(n_P=0\), so
\(P_{\mathrm{rad}}=1\,\mathrm{W}\). Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-torque-power} predicts
\(\tau_z^{\mathrm{pred}}=-(m+\sigma)/\omega=-1/\omega\approx -1.59\times 10^{-11}\,\mathrm{N\,m}\).
If the torque channel reports \(y_\tau=-1.55\times 10^{-11}\,\mathrm{N\,m}\) with
\(K_\tau=1\) and \(n_\tau=0\), then
\(\mathcal{R}_\tau\approx 0.025\) from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-torque-closure-residual},
well within typical \(R_{\tau,\max}=0.1\). Adding \(n_\tau=2\times 10^{-12}\,\mathrm{N\,m}\)
raises \(\mathcal{R}_\tau\) and may fail Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex61-admissibility-gate}
even when the underlying field is correct, illustrating that channel noise enters
the verification chain on equal footing with physics \cite{jackson1999,collin2001}.

The result is valid when \(S_R\) lies in a region where radiative flux dominates
reactive recirculation, material back-action on the source is weak over one cycle
average, and modal leakage to uncontrolled channels is negligible. Strong
near-field storage, multimode cross-coupling, or moving boundaries require a
broader control model \cite{jackson1999,collin2001,balanis2016}.

The next problem deliberately constructs a field with conspicuous phase structure
but vanishing net angular-momentum transport, thereby testing necessity versus
sufficiency of phase winding.

\subsection{Phase structure without angular-momentum transport}
\label{sec:ch01.6.2}
% TOC tag: [FIG+PROB][HOME]

Let a monochromatic transverse field on observation surface \(S_o\) be formed by
superposing two coherent counter-rotating helical channels with common radial
envelope \(A(\rho)\), cylindrical coordinates \((\rho,\phi)\), and integer order
\(m>0\):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex62-counter-rotating-superposition}
E_t(\rho,\phi)
=
A(\rho)e^{j m\phi}
+
A(\rho)e^{-j m\phi}
=
2A(\rho)\cos(m\phi).
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-counter-rotating-superposition} displays strong angular
patterning through lobe/nodal structure of \(\cos(m\phi)\), so any purely geometric
inspection would classify the field as richly phase-structured.

\paragraph{Complete derivation chain.}
Start from left- and right-circular components on \(S_o\):
\(E_+ = A(\rho)e^{\jj m\phi}/\sqrt{2}\) and
\(E_- = A(\rho)e^{-\jj m\phi}/\sqrt{2}\), each normalized so
\(|E_\pm|^2 = |A(\rho)|^2/2\). Superposition gives
\(E_t = \sqrt{2}A(\rho)\cos(m\phi)\) as in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-counter-rotating-superposition}. The intensity difference is
\(I_+ - I_- = |E_+|^2 - |E_-|^2 = 0\) at every \(\rho\) when amplitudes match.

For each helical channel, the time-averaged angular-momentum flux through \(S_o\)
scales as \((\pm m/\omega)\) times channel power
\(P_\pm = \int_{S_o} I_\pm\,dS\) \cite{jackson1999,balanis2016}. Summing channels,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex62-channel-sum}
\dot J_z
=
\frac{m}{\omega}(P_+ - P_-)
=
\frac{m}{\omega}\int_{S_o}(I_+-I_-)\,dS,
\end{equation}
which is Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-net-am-transport}. When \(I_+=I_-\), both \(P_+\) and
\(P_-\) are equal and \(\dot J_z=0\) even though \(|E_t|^2 = 2|A|^2\cos^2(m\phi)\)
exhibits \(2m\) azimuthal nodes per cycle.

The Poynting vector for each circular component rotates in opposite senses; their
sum produces a standing azimuthal pattern in \(|E_t|\) without net azimuthal power
flow. Stress-flux integration confirms
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-zero-torque-prediction}: mechanical torque vanishes because
exported angular momentum vanishes. Any experimental nonzero torque under equal
channels implicates \(I_+\neq I_-\) from loss, ellipticity mismatch, or aperture
truncation breaking the symmetry assumed in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-equal-channel-condition} \cite{jackson1999,collin2001}.

To make the transport test explicit, write the two-channel complex amplitudes as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex62-channel-amplitudes}
E_{+}(\rho,\phi)=A_+(\rho)e^{jm\phi},\qquad
E_{-}(\rho,\phi)=A_-(\rho)e^{-jm\phi},
\end{equation}
with intensities \(I_+(\rho)=|A_+(\rho)|^2\) and \(I_-(\rho)=|A_-(\rho)|^2\). Let
\(\dot J_z\) be net angular-momentum transport rate through \(S_o\). The inherited
channel-balance law gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex62-net-am-transport}
\dot J_z
=
\frac{m}{\omega}
\int_{S_o}\!\big(I_+-I_-\big)\,dS.
\end{equation}
Here \(m/\omega\) is channel angular-momentum-per-energy factor and \(I_+-I_-\) is
signed transport imbalance. Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-net-am-transport} therefore
states that net transport is controlled by channel imbalance, not by visual phase
richness.

The equal-amplitude counterexample is the special case
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex62-equal-channel-condition}
I_+(\rho)=I_-(\rho)\quad\forall \rho\in S_o.
\end{equation}
Substitution into Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-net-am-transport} yields
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex62-zero-transport-result}
\dot J_z=0
\qquad\text{while}\qquad
E_t(\rho,\phi)=2A(\rho)\cos(m\phi)\neq 0.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-zero-transport-result} is the central negative result of
this subsection: visible phase structure is not sufficient for net
angular-momentum transport.

To connect this with measurable torque channels, use the stress-to-torque mapping
from Section~\ref{sec:ch01.5}. In steady operation,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex62-zero-torque-prediction}
\tau_z^{\mathrm{pred}}
=
-\dot J_z
=
0
\quad\text{under Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-equal-channel-condition}}.
\end{equation}
Hence a nonzero torque measurement in this counterexample signals either channel
imbalance, asymmetry in measurement chain, or unresolved environmental coupling.

A small imbalance perturbation provides first-order sensitivity. Let
\(I_+(\rho)=I_0(\rho)+\delta I(\rho)\) and
\(I_-(\rho)=I_0(\rho)-\delta I(\rho)\). Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex62-imbalance-sensitivity}
\dot J_z
=
\frac{2m}{\omega}\int_{S_o}\delta I(\rho)\,dS.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-imbalance-sensitivity} quantifies how small channel
biases convert phase-structured but balanced fields into weak net-transport states.

To separate pattern visibility from transport capability, define phase-structure
index and transport index
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex62-visibility-transport-indices}
\mathcal{V}_{\phi}
=
\frac{\int_{0}^{2\pi}\left|\partial_{\phi}E_t(\rho_*,\phi)\right|^2\,d\phi}
{\int_{0}^{2\pi}|E_t(\rho_*,\phi)|^2\,d\phi+\epsilon_v},
\qquad
\mathcal{T}_{J}
=
\frac{|\dot J_z|}{P_{\mathrm{tot}}/\omega+\epsilon_t},
\end{equation}
where \(\rho_*\) is representative radius, \(P_{\mathrm{tot}}\) total channel
power, and \(\epsilon_v,\epsilon_t>0\) regularizations. In the equal-channel
counterexample, \(\mathcal{V}_{\phi}\) can be large while \(\mathcal{T}_{J}=0\).
This explicitly demonstrates necessity-versus-sufficiency separation.

Channel \(-m\): azimuthal circulation in negative sense with equal intensity.\\

Figure~\ref{fig:ch1.ex62-phase-no-transport} converts the algebra into a
diagnostic statement: visual twisting or lobe count cannot by itself certify
mechanical torque capability. A transport claim requires signed channel imbalance
or equivalent flux criterion, exactly as enforced by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-net-am-transport}.

The worked-case admissibility gate is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex62-admissibility-gate}
\mathcal{D}_{62}=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{mod}}=1,\
\left|\int_{S_o}(I_+-I_-)\,dS\right|\le \Delta I_{\max},\
|y_{\tau}|\le y_{\tau,\max},\
\mathcal{T}_{J}\le T_{\max}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(\Delta I_{\max}\), \(y_{\tau,\max}\), and \(T_{\max}\) are declared
thresholds. Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-admissibility-gate} ensures that the
counterexample is accepted only when channel balance and measurement evidence are
consistent.

\begin{proposition}[Phase-only insufficiency criterion]
\label{prop:ch1.ex62-phase-insufficiency}
In the balanced counter-rotating configuration of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-equal-channel-condition}, phase-structure visibility is not a
sufficient condition for angular-momentum transport.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-net-am-transport} ties transport to signed intensity
imbalance. Balanced channels enforce Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-zero-transport-result},
which with Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-zero-torque-prediction} yields zero predicted
torque despite nontrivial spatial phase structure.

\paragraph{Numerical visibility-versus-transport walk-through.}
With \(m=2\), \(A(\rho)=A_0 e^{-\rho^2/\rho_0^2}\), and equal channels
\(I_+=I_-=A_0^2 e^{-2\rho^2/\rho_0^2}\), Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-counter-rotating-superposition}
gives \(E_t=2A_0 e^{-\rho^2/\rho_0^2}\cos(2\phi)\). At \(\rho_*=\rho_0\),
\(\partial_\phi E_t=-4A_0 e^{-1}\sin(2\phi)\), so
\(\mathcal{V}_\phi\) in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex62-visibility-transport-indices} is
order unity because azimuthal variation is strong. Yet \(I_+-I_-=0\) implies
\(\dot J_z=0\) and \(\mathcal{T}_J=0\) identically. Introducing a five-percent
intensity bias \(I_+=1.05\,I_0\), \(I_-=0.95\,I_0\) yields
\(\dot J_z=(0.1m/\omega)\int_{S_o}I_0\,dS\), a small but nonzero transport channel
detectable only when intensity imbalance is resolved at the one-percent level or
better \cite{jackson1999,collin2001,balanis2016}.

The derivation presumes linear superposition, coherent channel control, and
observation geometry that resolves channel intensities without bias. Incoherent
mixing, polarization-selective loss, or aperture truncation can break the exact
cancelation and produce residual transport \cite{jackson1999,collin2001,balanis2016}.

\subsection{Gauge dependence of local angular-momentum densities}
\label{sec:ch01.6.3}
% TOC tag: [FIG+PROB][USE SS1.4.2]

Take a finite control volume \(V_g\) with boundary \(\partial V_g\), outward unit
normal \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\), and two gauge-related potential representatives
\((\mathbf{A},\phi)\) and \((\mathbf{A}',\phi')\). The gauge-variation structure is
inherited from Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.4.2}; no new gauge theorem is introduced.
The worked problem is to quantify when local angular-momentum maps disagree while
integrated transport observables remain gauge-robust.

Let \(\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}\) be a candidate local angular-momentum density
constructed from potentials and fields, and define gauge difference
\(\delta_\chi\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}
=\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}'-\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}\).
Applying the inherited structure from Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.4.2} gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex63-gauge-density-balance}
\int_{V_g}\delta_\chi\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}\,dV
=
\int_{\partial V_g}\mathbf{\Xi}_\chi\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS
+
\int_{V_g}\mathbf{\Psi}_\chi\,dV,
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{\Xi}_\chi\) is gauge-induced flux tensor and \(\mathbf{\Psi}_\chi\)
is residual gauge source term (notations inherited from
Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.4.2}). The first integral on the right is boundary
redistribution, while the second is volumetric contamination.

Define integrated reports
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex63-integrated-report}
\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}
=
\int_{V_g}\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}\,dV,\qquad
\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}'
=
\int_{V_g}\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}'\,dV.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex63-gauge-density-balance} then gives
\(\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}'-\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}\) exactly as the sum of
boundary and residual channels.

Under boundary/material conditions for which
\(\int_{\partial V_g}\mathbf{\Xi}_\chi\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS=0\) and
\(\int_{V_g}\mathbf{\Psi}_\chi\,dV=0\), Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex63-gauge-density-balance}
reduces to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex63-integral-equality}
\int_{V_g}\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}'\,dV
=
\int_{V_g}\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}\,dV.
\end{equation}
Even when Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex63-integral-equality} holds, pointwise maps
\(\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}'(\mathbf{r})\) and
\(\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}(\mathbf{r})\) may differ strongly. Therefore local
gauge-sensitive pictures are admissible only as intermediate descriptors, not as
standalone observables.

To separate local-map sensitivity from integral contamination, define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex63-local-mismatch-index}
\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{loc}}
=
\frac{\int_{V_g}\|\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}'-\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}\|\,dV}
{\int_{V_g}\|\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}\|\,dV+\epsilon_j},
\qquad \epsilon_j>0,
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex63-integral-contamination-index}
\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{int}}
=
\frac{\|\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}'-\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}\|}
{\|\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}\|+\epsilon_c},
\qquad \epsilon_c>0.
\end{equation}
Large \(\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{loc}}\) does not by itself invalidate observability;
large \(\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{int}}\) does.

The worked-case acceptance gate is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex63-observable-gate}
\mathcal{D}_{63}=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{mod}}=1,\
\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{int}}\le C_{\mathrm{int,max}},\
\left\|\int_{V_g}\mathbf{\Psi}_\chi\,dV\right\|\le \Psi_{\max}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(C_{\mathrm{int,max}}\) and \(\Psi_{\max}\) are declared tolerances. This
gate enforces that observable status is controlled by integral contamination, not by
local map appearance.

\begin{proposition}[Gauge-map insufficiency criterion]
\label{prop:ch1.ex63-gauge-map-insufficiency}
Gauge-sensitive local angular-momentum maps are admissible as observables in this
worked case only if \(\mathcal{D}_{63}=1\); otherwise they remain intermediate
descriptors.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex63-gauge-density-balance} decomposes gauge variation into
boundary and residual channels. Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex63-integral-equality} states
the integral closure condition, while
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex63-observable-gate} enforces thresholded admissibility. Hence
local map disagreement alone is insufficient for rejecting physical observability.

The argument holds when potentials are sufficiently regular for volume/surface
operations and when boundary conditions are declared explicitly. Topological gauge
patching, singular gauges, or unresolved boundary currents can invalidate the
simplified cancellation path \cite{jackson1999,collin2001,balanis2016}.

\paragraph{Step-by-step gauge comparison.}
In Coulomb gauge for a point dipole \(\mathbf{p}=p_0\hat{\mathbf{z}}\),
\(\phi_C=\mathbf{p}\cdot\mathbf{r}/(4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^3)\) and
\(\mathbf{A}_C\) is solenoidal. Under Lorenz gauge,
\(\phi_L=\jj\omega p_0\cos\theta/(4\pi c^2 r)\) in the far zone with a transverse
\(\mathbf{A}_L\). Construct \(\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}=\mathbf{r}\times(\varepsilon_0\E)\)
in each gauge at the same field point: the two local vectors differ by
\(\delta_\chi\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{loc}}\propto\nabla\chi\) with
\(\chi=(\phi_L-\phi_C)/\omega\). Volume integration over a sphere \(V_g\) of radius
\(R\) gives \(\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}'\neq\mathbf{J}_{\mathrm{int}}\) unless the
residual \(\mathbf{\Psi}_\chi\) integral vanishes. Using the gauge-invariant
\(\mathbf{j}_{\mathrm{em}}\) from Section~\ref{sec:ch01.1.5} instead produces
\(\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{int}}=0\) in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex63-integral-contamination-index}
while \(\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{loc}}\) may remain large. The worked conclusion matches
Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.4.2}: local potential maps are diagnostic; invariant
integrals are reportable \cite{jackson1999,harrington2001}.

The final worked case compares reactive and radiative regimes directly, using the
regime-separation principles of Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.2.4} in measurement form.

\subsection{Reactive versus radiative comparison}
\label{sec:ch01.6.4}
% TOC tag: [FIG+PROB][USE SS1.2.4]

Let \(S_{r_1}\) and \(S_{r_2}\) be concentric spherical observation surfaces,
with radii \(r_1<r_2\), centered on the same source. The objective is to compare
angular-structure interpretation in a reactive-dominant surface \(S_{r_1}\) and a
radiative-dominant surface \(S_{r_2}\), using the regime logic inherited from
Subsection~\ref{sec:ch01.2.4}.

Consider a half-wave dipole at \(ka=1\) with observation shells at
\(kr_1=0.5\) and \(kr_2=5\), where \(k=\omega/c\). On \(S_{r_1}\), the reactive
integral \(Q(r_1)\) typically exceeds \(|P(r_1)|\), yielding
\(\mathcal{R}_Q(r_1)\gg 1\); phase winding and polarization ellipticity can be
large while exported power and axis torque remain small. On \(S_{r_2}\),
\(|P(r_2)|\gg |Q(r_2)|\) restores conservation-compatible transport
interpretation, and \(\mathcal{C}_\tau\gg 1\) signals that torque channels become
measurably active only after the reactive shell is crossed
\cite{collin2001,jackson1999,balanis2016}. This two-surface protocol is the
measurement counterpart of the regime indices \(\chi\) and \(\chi_\Omega\) from
Section~\ref{sec:ch01.2.4}.

Define active-power and reactive-power surface functionals
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex64-power-functionals}
P(r)=\int_{S_r}\Re\!\left\{\mathbf{S}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\right\}dS,\qquad
Q(r)=\int_{S_r}\Im\!\left\{\mathbf{S}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\right\}dS,
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{S}\) is complex Poynting vector and \(\Im\{\cdot\}\) extracts
imaginary part. The first integral measures net exported power; the second
measures reactive circulation associated with local storage exchange.

To compare regimes on one normalized axis, define reactive ratio
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex64-reactive-ratio}
\mathcal{R}_Q(r)=\frac{|Q(r)|}{|P(r)|+\epsilon_P},
\qquad \epsilon_P>0.
\end{equation}
Reactive-dominant behavior corresponds to \(\mathcal{R}_Q\gg 1\), while
radiative-dominant behavior corresponds to \(\mathcal{R}_Q\ll 1\). This scalar
ratio makes regime classification operationally testable at each observation
surface.

For a torque channel about \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\), define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex64-torque-contrast}
\mathcal{C}_\tau
=
\frac{|\tau_z(r_2)|}{|\tau_z(r_1)|+\epsilon_\tau},
\qquad
\epsilon_\tau>0.
\end{equation}
The numerator and denominator are measured torque magnitudes on outer and inner
surfaces; \(\epsilon_\tau\) is a small regularization constant preventing division
singularity. When \(Q(r_1)\gg P(r_1)\) and \(P(r_2)\gg |Q(r_2)|\), one typically
obtains \(\mathcal{C}_\tau\gg 1\): near-zone angular structure is visually rich
yet weakly transport-capable, while far-zone structure carries measurable torque.

Measurement channels at the two surfaces are written
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex64-surface-channel-model}
y_{P,k}=K_{P,k}P(r_k)+n_{P,k},\qquad
y_{Q,k}=K_{Q,k}Q(r_k)+n_{Q,k},\qquad
y_{\tau,k}=K_{\tau,k}\tau_z(r_k)+n_{\tau,k},
\end{equation}
for \(k\in\{1,2\}\), where \(K_{\bullet,k}\) are calibration factors and
\(n_{\bullet,k}\) are channel disturbances. Equation
\eqref{eq:ch1.ex64-surface-channel-model} ensures that regime claims are tied to
calibrated outputs rather than inferred from qualitative field appearance.

Define regime-gated transport efficiency
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex64-gated-efficiency}
\eta_{\tau}(r_k)
=
\frac{|\tau_z(r_k)|}{P(r_k)/\omega+\epsilon_\eta}
\cdot
\frac{1}{1+\mathcal{R}_Q(r_k)},
\qquad \epsilon_\eta>0.
\end{equation}
The factor \(1/(1+\mathcal{R}_Q)\) suppresses apparent efficiency in reactive
regimes where local circulation dominates exported transport.

Surface-to-surface trend consistency is captured by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex64-trend-consistency}
\mathcal{T}_{12}
=
\frac{\left|\eta_{\tau}(r_2)-\eta_{\tau}(r_1)\right|}
     {\eta_{\tau}(r_2)+\epsilon_t},
\qquad \epsilon_t>0.
\end{equation}
Large \(\mathcal{T}_{12}\) indicates strong regime transition in transport
effectiveness, which is expected when \(r_1\) is reactive-dominant and \(r_2\) is
radiative-dominant.

\paragraph{Representative channel values.}
For a center-fed dipole at \(f=1\,\mathrm{GHz}\) (\(k=20.96\,\mathrm{rad/m}\)), take
calibrated reads \(y_{P,1}=0.05\,\mathrm{W}\), \(y_{Q,1}=0.40\,\mathrm{W}\) on
\(S_{r_1}\) with \(kr_1=0.5\), giving \(\mathcal{R}_Q(r_1)\approx 8\gg 1\) and
\(\eta_\tau(r_1)\approx 0\) from Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex64-gated-efficiency}. On
\(S_{r_2}\) with \(kr_2=5\), suppose \(y_{P,2}=0.85\,\mathrm{W}\),
\(y_{Q,2}=0.04\,\mathrm{W}\), so \(\mathcal{R}_Q(r_2)\approx 0.05\ll 1\). If
\(|\tau_z(r_1)|=0.2\,\mathrm{nN\,m}\) and \(|\tau_z(r_2)|=4.5\,\mathrm{nN\,m}\),
then \(\mathcal{C}_\tau\approx 22\) and \(\mathcal{T}_{12}\) is order unity, confirming
regime transition rather than measurement noise. Failing to report \(Q(r)\) alongside
\(P(r)\) would mislabel the inner surface as torque-capable based on phase texture alone
\cite{collin2001,jackson1999,balanis2016}.

The worked-case admissibility gate is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ch1.ex64-regime-admissibility}
\mathcal{D}_{64}=1
\iff
\Big(
\mathcal{D}_{63}=1,\
\mathcal{R}_Q(r_1)\ge R_{Q,\min},\
\mathcal{R}_Q(r_2)\le R_{Q,\max},\
\mathcal{C}_\tau\ge C_{\tau,\min},\
\mathcal{T}_{12}\le T_{12,\max}
\Big),
\end{equation}
where \(R_{Q,\min}\), \(R_{Q,\max}\), \(C_{\tau,\min}\), and \(T_{12,\max}\) are
declared thresholds. Equation~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex64-regime-admissibility} ensures that
reactive/radiative labeling and torque-contrast interpretation are jointly
consistent.

\begin{proposition}[Reactive-radiative discrimination criterion]
\label{prop:ch1.ex64-regime-discrimination}
The surface-comparison conclusion of this subsection is physically admissible only
if \(\mathcal{D}_{64}=1\).
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Proof sketch.}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex64-power-functionals} defines active/reactive channels,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex64-reactive-ratio} defines regime class,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex64-torque-contrast} and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex64-gated-efficiency} define transport comparison, and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ch1.ex64-regime-admissibility} imposes thresholded closure. Their
conjunction establishes the criterion.

This comparison assumes identical origin/axis declarations, stationary harmonic
operation, and negligible environmental scattering between \(S_{r_1}\) and
\(S_{r_2}\). Strong inhomogeneous media or multipath contamination can distort
\(P,Q,\tau_z\) trend comparison and require full propagation compensation
\cite{jackson1999,collin2001,balanis2016}.

The worked sequence of Section~\ref{sec:ch01.6} is therefore complete: radiation
torque conservation, phase-only counterexample, gauge-sensitivity check, and
